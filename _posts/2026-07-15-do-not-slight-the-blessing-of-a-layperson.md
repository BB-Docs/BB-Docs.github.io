### Opening

This session is structured as a moderated workshop: a short reading from the day's Rabash article is followed by recorded clips of Rav Laitman and an extended, live Q&A with students joining from several countries. The organizing question, named at the outset: what is this thing called pride — the sense of self that "I can't break it, I can't do anything about it" — and if a person truly cannot cancel it alone, what actually can?

---

## Part 1 — Pride as the Root Obstacle

### Two Kinds of Pride

The workshop opens by distinguishing negative pride from positive pride. Negative pride is described as a demand or condition a person quietly sets before he'll do his part in the work — and, crucially, a condition he usually can't see in himself:

> "If in this thing there is a demand or a condition, that only then he will be able to do some work on his part, that's called that he has pride. And then until he removes this demand out of his heart, he won't be able to move."

Positive pride is different: pride in the path itself, in being chosen for this method, in the friends. It is described as something that "gives lots of strength" and lifts others up — but it, too, must be built, not simply willed: "pride is not something that a person can build on his own... it's just to increase the society, just to increase the friends."

### Pride as a Closed Bubble

Pride is also described structurally: it is what makes a person refuse any opinion from outside his own path — "it's like a bubble where I don't accept any opinion against the path, the Rav, the way." The moderator notes this can even be positive, when it protects fidelity to the path against distraction — but the same mechanism, turned toward the friends, becomes the obstacle the rest of the session addresses.

---

## Part 2 — Pharaoh, the Stony Heart, and Why a Person Cannot Do This Alone

### The First Clip: Pride Is Pharaoh

The first clip of Rav Laitman frames pride directly as Pharaoh — a force standing in the way that cannot be canceled except through the work of "the children of Israel," i.e., the ten:

> "This pride is Pharaoh. That standing there that you really can't cancel... unless the children of Israel aside from the work that a person really cleans up all those that he asks for these forces and he gets additional force from the creator that can be greater than his sense of pride, and then he annuls his pride."

A person cannot simply wait to see the Creator's greatness and then get rid of pride on that basis — "there's no other force that can apply pressure to the person that will bring him down from his natural pride" except the greatness of the Creator received *through the society*. A person alone cannot generate enough force to even ask for this.

### The Fourth Clip: The Power of Shattering and the "Third Thing"

A later clip goes further, describing pride as "the power of shattering" — the force separating a person from the Creator, strong enough that a person facing it alone would rather die than set it aside. Rav Laitman's resolution: the group is a third force, distinct from both the person and the Creator:

> "It's the third thing that I can add to myself... which isn't existence from absence, nor existence from existence, not mine, not the Creator's, but a third thing that exists in nature... See how much the power of the group needs to be outside of the connection between myself and the Creator."

**Baal HaSulam's claim, as relayed:** the entire future of the work depends on this — the group is described as the only thing that can actually help.

## Part 3 — Working Through the Point in the Heart

### A Student's Question: How Do I Feel Small Before the Ten?

Asked directly how to come to feel low relative to the group, the moderator answers that the desire to ask is itself already a form of prayer — "that is already a prayer that I want to annul myself toward the friends. I can't, but I really want to." The work is to see the friends as great, since without that, "the ego won't take a step back."

### Looking Through the Point in the Heart, Not Through the Body

The distinction is illustrated with a concrete image: a friend whose head is nodding because he's falling asleep during the lesson.

> "Look at the friend, not when his head falls, but when he's making the effort not to fall asleep. Here you look through the point in the heart, and before that you look through the body... You look at him saying, he's so tired, but he still came to the lesson."

Re-reading the article is offered as a practical tool for this shift: "read it again, and you'll look at the friends differently."

## Part 4 — Practical Q&A: When Friends Fall Short

### Can We Comment on a Friend Without Hurting Him?

Several students raise variations of the same problem: a friend isn't keeping quiet during meals, isn't participating, or the Creator himself seems not to "let" a friend keep the group's agreed conditions. Every comment, the moderator notes, is "a stab to his will to receive." The guidance given is not to place conditions in front of a person at all — "if I love the friends, I will do everything as if they were my children... to show him how much we are with you, how much we care about you."

### Envy, Not Criticism, Draws a Friend Back

Rabash's principle is invoked directly: a person cannot advance from criticism, only from envy.

> "Rabash says you cannot advance in negativity. A person cannot advance from a criticism. But from envy, Rav said in the lesson that the ten can plan how to evoke envy within a friend — to tell him how great was a certain event, and a friend is envious of them and can't believe how I missed it."

The stated goal is never to punish or exclude a struggling friend, but to project the group's own joy so strongly that "he won't be able to sit on the sidelines for long... we're drawn to the good."

### A Friend's Own Deficiency vs. the Group's Deficiency

Asked how a person can be certain he is even entitled to pray over his own deficiency (since confidence in oneself is itself a form of pride), the answer distinguishes a private lack from one incorporated from the friends: "if it's a deficiency he receives from the friends... then it's something else... it's not him and his deficiency. He multiplies." The instruction repeated through the session: move "from me to we, from I want for myself to I want for the friends."

## Part 5 — Right Line, Left Line, and Justifying the Friend

### Why Not Ask the Creator to Reveal the Left Line?

A student asks whether, after long effort on the right line, one should actively ask the Creator to reveal one's dependence (the left line) in order to strengthen the ten. The answer is no:

> "No need to ask the creator to show the left line... our effort is really to cling to the right line, and it's not simple — it's practically impossible. That's what we're going for... if the Creator needs to reveal us negativity, left, or disturbances, the Creator will do his work. We don't need to worry about it."

The moderator adds that trying to "take off the gloves" and confront each other's faults directly tends to end badly — "usually this conversation ends when nobody wants to come the day after that."

### Justifying the Friend Is Real Work

Asked how to hold both truths — that the friends are great, and that a specific friend keeps missing the lesson — the moderator names this directly as a skill: **judging to the side of merit.** The natural inclination resists this ("they don't have the importance of the goal" — while for one's own absences, "I always have the justifications of why I can't"). The corrective is to actively imagine the friend's actual conditions — work, family, country — and ask what it would take for oneself to show up in that friend's place, rather than assuming indifference.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Pride is a hidden condition, not an obvious flaw.** It shows up as a quiet demand — "only then will I do my part" — that a person usually cannot see or name in himself.
2. **There is a positive form of pride** (pride in the path and the friends) as well as the negative form (a closed bubble that rejects outside correction) — and the positive form still has to be deliberately built through the group, not simply felt.
3. **A person cannot cancel his own pride alone.** Only additional force received through the group — greater than the pride itself — allows a person to annul it and turn to the Creator.
4. **The group functions as a genuine "third thing"** — distinct from the person and from the Creator — the only actual source of the strength needed to work against pride.
5. **The point in the heart, not external behavior, is where a friend should actually be seen** — the practical difference between judging someone by his tired, nodding head and recognizing that he still made the effort to come.
6. **Correction of a struggling friend works through envy and warmth, never through criticism** — the group's job is to project joy and make the path desirable, not to confront or exclude.
7. **The work stays on the right line by design.** A person does not need to ask to see his own left-line dependence; the Creator reveals it in due course, and justifying the friend — not confronting him — is the ongoing discipline of the right line.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Pride (Ga'avah / Self-Love)** | A hidden demand or condition placed before a person will do his part in the work; also, more positively, pride in the path and the friends when consciously built through the group. |
| **Pharaoh** | The image used for pride as an obstacle — a force a person cannot cancel alone, requiring the collective force of the group ("the children of Israel") to overcome. |
| **The Power of Shattering** | Rav Laitman's term for the force of pride separating a person from the Creator — strong enough that facing it alone feels unbearable. |
| **The Group as a "Third Thing"** | The group's help, described as neither existence-from-absence nor existence-from-existence, neither the person's own nor the Creator's — a distinct source of force available only through connection with others. |
| **Point in the Heart** | The vantage point from which a person is meant to see the friends — as opposed to seeing them "through the body" (external behavior, tiredness, mistakes). |
| **Judging to the Side of Merit** | The discipline of imagining a struggling friend's actual conditions rather than assuming indifference — praised in this session as real, difficult work. |
| **Right Line / Left Line** | Right line: mutual appreciation and connection, the default, constant orientation. Left line: dwelling on faults or one's own dependence, not to be sought out — the Creator reveals it when needed. |
| **Envy vs. Criticism** | Rabash's principle that advancement never comes from criticizing a friend, only from evoking envy of the good he is missing. |
| **Arvut (Mutual Guarantee)** | The bond of mutual responsibility between friends; referenced here as the name of the group's live Q&A submission system used during the workshop. |
| **Existence from Absence (Yesh MiAyin)** | The mode of creation with no root in the Creator (who lacks nothing); invoked to explain why the stony heart cannot be corrected by a person alone, and why the group's help is a genuinely separate "third" force. |
| **Kedusha** | Holiness — the quality of bestowal; referenced repeatedly as the measure a person feels he has "some grip" on, however small. |

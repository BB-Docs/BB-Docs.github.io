### Opening

The lesson opens with a phrase from the Zohar: a burnt offering is called "male," because it atones for the thought — which is chochmah, and is "male," as opposed to the heart, which is bina, and is "female." Rabash uses this to ask a much larger question: what, exactly, is "the thought of the Creator" as opposed to "the thought of man," and why does correcting a blemish in faith specifically require an offering associated with thought rather than desire? The article works through a parable about a mother feeding her children to explain why reward that is revealed now, in the very act, is fundamentally different from reward promised for later — and then reads that difference back into what it means to have faith in the Creator as "the good who does good." The second half of the lesson turns to a recorded 2002 talk and workshop, which asks the same question from a different angle: if the goal of all this work is simply "rest," why must a person work at all to reach it — and how much of what a person does is actually his own choice to begin with?

---

## Part 1 — Reading the Article: The Zohar's Puzzle

### The Two Thoughts

The Zohar is quoted: "If his offering is a burnt offering… for My thoughts are not your thoughts." From the thought of the Creator, "ways and paths extend to devise the Holy Name," and from it emanate the written and oral Torah — which is Malchut. But from the thought of man emerges "the filth of the evil inclination to harm oneself and all others," along with transgressions, idolatry, incest, and bloodshed.

Rabash poses the question directly: what is the thought of the Creator in the Work, and what is the thought of man, that the two are said to produce such opposite results? And what does it mean that "a burnt offering is male" — that it atones specifically for the thought?

### Burnt Offering, Chochmah, and Bina

The commentary on this passage explains: the burnt offering "rises over the heart" — meaning over the thought, which sits above the heart. **Thought is chochmah**, and is regarded as **male**; **the heart is bina** ("as in, the heart understands"), and is regarded as **female**. A burnt offering atones for a blemish in the thought, since it corresponds to the male element being corrected.

## Part 2 — The Two Kinds of Reward: The Mother Who Feeds Her Children

### Why Reward Matters at All

Since every created being was made with a desire to receive delight and pleasure, a person cannot act unless he believes the action will bring him pleasure — otherwise, the act contradicts the very root of his nature. Sometimes a person acts even without feeling pleasure in the act itself, because he trusts he will be rewarded with pleasure later. But **if the reward isn't certain, a person has no strength to work without enjoying the work itself.**

### The Allegory

> A mother feeds her children. Buying the groceries and cooking is work she would gladly give up — a wealthy woman can hire someone to do it. But watching her children eat, she would never give up. She doesn't ask, "what do I get out of watching them eat?" She needs no reward, because the act itself is the pleasure.

Rabash ties this to the sages' teaching that the 613 mitzvot are called **613 pkudim** — "deposits." Each mitzvah contains a special light. If that light and its pleasure were revealed now, no faith would be required — the whole world would rush to observe Torah and mitzvot naturally. But the pleasure is concealed until a person earns vessels of bestowal, and until then, the body resists: why should it give up the pleasure it was built to receive?

### Two Ways to Want the Reward

The mitzvot can be approached in two ways:

- **Reward in vessels of reception** — the work itself gives no pleasure; the only reason to do it is a reward that comes *later*, in "the next world." A person working this way inevitably asks: why do I need all 613 mitzvot? Why not receive the same reward for observing fewer of them, since it's the *reward*, not the work, that matters to him? This view treats the work as a pure means, exactly like being paid for labor — anyone would take a higher wage for less work if offered it.
- **Reward in vessels of bestowal** — a person feels each of the 613 deposits as a distinct pleasure, felt *in the doing*, in this world, now. Here it would never occur to him to ask for fewer mitzvot in exchange for the same reward, exactly as no one complains that the Creator gave too many corporeal pleasures. **In this form, the 613 commandments become rest, not labor** — the mother watching her children eat does not experience it as a job to be shortened.

## Part 3 — Resolving the Puzzle: The Two Thoughts and Equivalence of Form

### What "The Creator's Thought" Means

Rabash now returns to the Zohar's language. **The thought of the Creator is the thought of creation itself: to do good to His creatures.** The "ways and paths" that extend "to devise the Holy Name" refer to the fact that the Name — "the good who does good" — is hidden from creatures until they attain vessels of bestowal. Torah and mitzvot were given as the means (*etzot*, counsel) toward those vessels; observing them isn't for the Creator's benefit, but so the created being corrects himself and can come to feel the Name is true.

### Why Man's Thought Produces the Opposite

Man, as a created being — "existence from absence" — thinks only of receiving, not bestowing. Since a restriction was placed on this will to receive, the **evil inclination** arises from this very thought, showing a person false pictures of how much he will "suffer" by giving to others.

Rabash invokes the image of the serpent casting *zuhama* (filth) into Eve, reading **zuhama as za-mah, "what is this?"** — the serpent's question whenever a person tries to act for the Creator's sake: *what will you get out of this?* This is the meaning of "from that thought emerges the filth of the evil inclination."

### Eye, Thought, and Heart

Drawing on "the eye sees and the heart covets," the eye is identified with chochmah/thought — the male element — which impresses the heart (bina/desire, the female element) with what to want. **Equivalence of form** with the Creator therefore has two discernments:

- **In the mind** — not thinking about oneself at all (whether one exists, whether one is watched over), since the Creator's own thought contains no self-concern; any such thought creates disparity of form and separates a person from Him.
- **In the heart** — once the mind holds this faith above reason, it "impresses" the heart, so the will to receive stops wanting to use its own quality (reception) and instead wants, like the thought, to become a giver.

### Back to the Burnt Offering

If a person's thought is blemished — if he does not believe the Creator is "the good who does good," watching over creation — that blemish specifically requires a **male offering**, corresponding to the thought (chochmah) that was damaged. This is why "a burnt offering is male": it corrects a flaw in faith, not in desire.

Finally, since Malchut (in the Work, the kingdom of heaven, i.e., faith) and Torah both emanate from the Creator's thought, **faith must come before Torah** — "it is forbidden to teach idol worshippers Torah," meaning a person cannot be rewarded with Torah until he has first been rewarded with faith.

## Part 4 — Recorded Lesson, May 30, 2002: Why Is the Goal Called "Rest"?

### The Joke About the Man Under the Tree

A person is told: work now, and later you'll be able to rest under a tree. He answers: I'm already lying under a tree — why work for it? Rav Laitman uses the joke to sharpen the question the article raises differently: if the goal really is rest, is this teaching just an excuse for laziness?

### Reality Doesn't Change

The answer given: the upper light is already in a state of complete, pre-ordained rest. We are, in some sense, already in that state — it is simply under concealment. **Work means preparing the vessels** to feel what is already true. Once the vessels are prepared and reach equivalence of form with the light, a person arrives at the same absolute rest the light is already in.

### What Makes Something "Rest"

Rest isn't measured by stillness. **It's measured by whether a vessel feels a gap between what it has and what it wants.** A vessel that feels such a gap is pushed toward more spiritual motion — it isn't at rest. A vessel that has reached equivalence with the goal feels no gap, "and can fly like a child with a ball" — this is rest, even in motion. (An aside compares this to relativity: what matters isn't speed, but whether nothing is gained or lost relative to one's surroundings — being "in balance.")

### How to Get There

The path to equivalence of form with the light is through the **written and oral Torah together**: one gives the intention to bestow, the other teaches what to do in order to bestow. Combining the two — acting in order to bestow, through mitzvot that are acts of bestowal — brings a person to the goal.

## Part 5 — Workshop: Mind, Heart, and Free Choice

### Mind and Heart Both Come from the Heart

A student asks: doesn't desire normally drive thought, rather than the reverse? Rav Laitman answers that this can't really be explained before it's felt — but that in truth, **both mind (mocha) and heart (liba) originate in the heart.** The heart contains a part that feels without discernment, and a part that feels with discernment (bina); the will to receive builds for itself a "head" (rosh) out of its own feeling, planning how it wants to resemble the Creator's actions. This is the sense in which "the heart understands."

### Only Two Options: My State, or the Upper One

On the deeper question of where any of this comes from: a person cannot use his current state to work against his current state — "the state governs the person." There are only two possibilities: **the person's own state controls him, or the state of the upper one controls him.** The one thing a person can choose is whether to draw, from society, an impression of the greatness of the goal — and it's only through that impression that he can turn to the upper one at all.

### The Friends, Ein Sof, and the One Narrow Opening

Everything a person receives, he receives from Ein Sof — directly, or indirectly "through the friends," i.e., the souls he's connected to. If ten people are connected in Ein Sof, each one, in principle, has access to all ten discernments; by connecting here, a person draws that same state down as a small illumination. ("Friends" is also said to include books and authors — anything through which the "external force" can be drawn.)

This leaves a person, in this telling, with **one single point of free choice**: whether to be impressed by society's exaltation of the goal. Every other decision in life — what to eat, where to go — is, in this description, hidden from us, determined, and only *appears* to be freely chosen. The one real choice is to work that narrow opening: to keep drawing, through the group, the sense that the goal is great.

## Part 6 — Workshop: Building Importance, and the Closing Summary

### Darkness Is Relative

Whether a person experiences his current state as "darkness" depends entirely on what he values. If someone is absorbed in the World Cup, that isn't darkness to him — it's what he's alive for. It only becomes darkness once he redefines his priorities and measures his state against something greater — the purpose of creation.

### The Tea and the Daughter

Two examples illustrate how importance can be added to something that has none by itself:

- **The tea** — a person may not enjoy an herbal tea on its own, but if he trusts a doctor's recommendation, it becomes more pleasant to him because of the *importance* he now attaches to it, not the taste itself.
- **The daughter** — a parent who has invested in a child for twenty years finds that child's requests automatically important, not because of any special negotiation, but because of the accumulated investment.

The instruction drawn from both: **a person doesn't naturally have "flavor" for the Creator**, so he must build it the same way — by investing attention and disconnecting from competing pulls (the workshop names watching the World Cup on television as an example), and by placing real weight on what the group says, the way one would with a child whose words already matter.

### Closing Summary

In the closing round, participants named: that although we feel we have free choice, the only real choice is to be incorporated in the environment and draw an inspiration toward the Creator from it, which generates reflected light and desire; that the goal called "rest" mirrors the child who is told to sit still but experiences running as his real rest — for us, "rest" means aiming for bestowal, not literal inactivity; and that everything, including whether one notices this at all, still depends on the group.

The lesson closes moving toward the study of the Ten Sefirot, with one open question raised but left for later: if "everything is in the hands of Heaven," what does it mean that "a hero is one who conquers his own inclination" — and is fear (of punishment, in this world or the next) the only lever available to do that?

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **A burnt offering is called "male" because it atones for the thought (chochmah), not the heart (bina, desire).** The two require different corrections.
2. **Reward in vessels of bestowal turns the work itself into pleasure; reward in vessels of reception turns the work into pure labor**, judged only by how much reward it yields later — the mother watching her children eat needs no reward, unlike the shopping and cooking she'd gladly delegate.
3. **The Creator's thought is only to bestow; man's native thought is only to receive** — and it's from this second thought, under restriction, that the evil inclination arises, always asking "what's in it for you?"
4. **Equivalence of form has two parts: the mind (not dwelling on oneself, believing above reason) and the heart (which follows the mind once faith is established).**
5. **"Rest" is not stillness — it is the absence of a felt gap** between what a vessel has and what it wants; a vessel in equivalence with the goal is at rest even in motion.
6. **A person's only real free choice is whether to draw, through the group, an impression of the greatness of the goal** — every other decision is described as effectively determined.
7. **Importance isn't felt automatically — it's built,** through investment and through disconnecting from competing pulls, the way a parent's years of investment make a child's words matter, or trust in a doctor makes a tea taste better.

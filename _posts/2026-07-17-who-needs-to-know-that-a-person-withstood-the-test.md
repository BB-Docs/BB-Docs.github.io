### Opening

The day's article asks why a person needs to *know* he has withstood a test — since the Creator already knows everything, the counting or testing can only be for man's own sake, to let him recognize which degree he belongs to. The second, longer recording picks this up from the practical side: if everything really comes from the Creator, what is left for a person to actually *do*? Rav Laitman's lecture and the Q&A that follows work out how two seemingly opposite attitudes — "if I am not for myself, who is for me" and "there is none else besides Him" — have to operate together, not one after the other, for a person to test himself honestly and grow from it.

---

## Part 1 — Two Sayings, Morning and Evening

### The Instruction from Baal HaSulam's Letters

Rav introduces a practical instruction from Baal HaSulam's Igrot (Letters, p. 25): in the morning, a person should say "if I am not for myself, who is for me"; in the evening, "there is none else besides Him."

> "In the morning a person needs to say, if I'm not for myself, who is for me, and in the evening, there is none else besides Him."

### What the Two Sayings Actually Mean in Practice

Rav unpacks this with the image of a person going to work: he goes out and works hard, sweating, believing at the time that his sweat earns his wage. Only afterward does he examine it and see that it was the Creator who gave him both the work and the payment. The real "work," Rav says, was never the labor itself — it was the person's attitude toward it.

> "It's not I who sweated and earned the payment, rather it's the Creator who gave me both the work and the payment, and really my work is not in performing the work itself, my work is in forming my attitude toward it."

Morning and evening are not literally two separate times of day — they describe two states. Morning is described as acting "without Hochma" (without illumination, in mercy/Hesed); evening is when Hochma illuminates what was done, even though it looks like there's no light left. The light earned through the day's work becomes "on your account" rather than only on the Creator's.

## Part 2 — Which Comes First?

### You Begin With "There Is None Else Besides Him"

A student asks where the confidence comes from to go out and act "as if it depends on me" without losing the sense that everything comes from the Creator. Rav's answer: a person actually starts from "there is none else besides Him" — accepting from the outset that whatever now happens to him certainly comes from above — and only *afterward*, relying on that acceptance, goes and acts as "if I am not for myself, who is for me."

> "I say there is none else besides Him even before I'm not for myself... That whatever now comes to me, there is none else besides Him. It certainly comes from above."

### Why the Self-Driven Half Is Still Needed

If a person stayed permanently inside "there is none else besides Him" without ever acting on his own initiative, he would never discover anything about himself, because he would never notice a difference between his own desire and the Creator's:

> "If, like a horse, you never make a single movement, then you'll never know how you differ from it... By beginning to act, you begin to feel the difference between your desire to act and his desire to act."

The two attitudes have to be "clothed" in one another — held at the same time, not swapped in sequence. Separating from "there is none else besides Him" entirely, Rav warns, turns the action into an ordinary, ungoaled act — "you're simply like a Gentile."

## Part 3 — Action as Self-Examination

### What "If I Am Not for Myself" Is Actually For

The purpose of acting "as if it depends on me" isn't independence from the Creator — it's examination. A person performs an action, takes ownership of it, and through the friction between what he wanted and what actually happened, comes to recognize the Creator's hand more precisely each time.

> "I perform my own corrections as though everything depends on me... according to that difference, you start becoming aware of yourself and him, recognizing yourself and him."

### From Examination to "There Is None Else Besides Him" Again

After enough of these individual checks — examining an action, discovering it was really the Creator's doing, accepting that — a person arrives back at "there is none else besides Him," now earned rather than assumed:

> "After all those checks, you say there is none else besides Him. That means that you've accepted His entire action upon yourself."

Rav describes this as a cycle repeating through the day: "if I'm not for myself, who is for me... there is none else besides Him... if I'm not for myself, who is for me... there is none else besides Him," each pass adding another increment of the second attitude.

## Part 4 — Staying With It: Reminders and the Inner Split

### Mechanical Reminders Before Felt Ones

Asked how to actually hold onto "there is none else besides Him" while doing ordinary things — studying, eating, working — Rav says the state is first built through artificial signs and reminders, returning to it every fifteen minutes or half hour, mechanically, before it is ever felt:

> "A person needs to make for himself all kinds of signs, reminders... at first mechanically... later he does it himself, that's the work."

He distinguishes this from full disconnection: a person who reaches at least "single concealment" still feels the Creator concealed somewhere in the background, rather than a "double concealment" where nothing is felt at all. Rav recalls that Rabash himself seemed to live constantly in that awareness, something he noticed only gradually after getting to know him.

### The Split Between Heart and Point in the Heart

Rav describes an ordinary inner split that develops once a point in the heart awakens: the heart carries the desires of this world (its light described as a "thin candlelight," Ner Dakik), while the point in the heart yearns for something from a different source — a surrounding light from beyond this world. A person swings between the two, sometimes absorbed by corporeal desire, sometimes by the pull of the point in the heart, most often a mixture of both. Over years, this passes through a stage of "recognition of evil" — questioning whether one's own desires are actually bad, and why — until, gradually, spirituality becomes the primary lens through which all corporeal activity is seen, without the person's ordinary life being cancelled.

## Part 5 — Correcting the Vessel, Not the Action

### The Problem Is the Vessel, Not What's Given

Rav applies this directly to how a person interprets difficulty: everything felt as bad is actually something good, received through an uncorrected vessel. The task is not to ask the Creator to change what He gives, but to want one's own vessel to change — to invert toward an intention to bestow — so the same giving is felt as good.

> "The problem is not that I need to change His actions. I need to change my impression of His actions."

### Lo Lishma to Lishma, Restated

He reframes lo lishma and lishma here in terms of whose feeling is at stake: wanting to feel good for one's own sake is still lo lishma; wanting the Creator's giving to be received well because it matters to the giver — "because it matters to me that I want to bless you" — is lishma.

### Redefining Prayer

Rav closes the discussion by redefining prayer itself: not words of request or gratitude, but the point a person reaches after enough self-examination where nothing more needs to be said — the settled, final recognition of exactly where he stands.

> "A prayer means the deepest feeling in the heart... I seemingly reach the final decision of what I am and where I am, and then they call it a prayer."

Students close the lesson noting the practical takeaway: tie every action, however small, to the larger goal — "a person can dig a hole even to the middle of the earth" and feel purpose in it, as long as it's tied to the Creator — and that the two attitudes, rather than being sequential, must be clothed in one another from the start.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **"If I am not for myself, who is for me" and "there is none else besides Him" are not sequential — they must be held together ("clothed" in one another) at all times.**
2. **Practically, a person begins with "there is none else besides Him"** — accepting that whatever happens comes from above — and only then acts as if it depends on him.
3. **Self-driven action exists for the sake of self-examination**, not independence: acting "as if it depends on me" reveals the gap between one's own desire and the Creator's, which is how self-recognition grows.
4. **The real work is not the task itself but one's attitude toward it** — illustrated by the image of a laborer who only later recognizes that the Creator gave both the work and the payment.
5. **The felt state is built through mechanical repetition before it becomes natural** — regular reminders, at first artificial, eventually become "single concealment," a felt sense the Creator is present even while concealed.
6. **A person lives with an ordinary inner split between the heart (desires of this world) and the point in the heart (yearning for something beyond it)** — until, gradually, spirituality becomes the lens through which all of life is seen.
7. **Prayer is redefined as the settled, final feeling reached after full self-examination**, not a request or expression of thanks in words.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **"If I Am Not for Myself, Who Is for Me"** | The self-driven attitude of acting as though everything depends on one's own effort; from Baal HaSulam's Igrot, paired with "there is none else besides Him." |
| **"There Is None Else Besides Him" (Ein Od Milvado)** | The attitude that everything comes from the Creator alone; taken up first, before self-driven action, and returned to after self-examination. |
| **Ner Dakik** | "Thin candlelight" — the small, natural light/desire belonging to this world, as opposed to the surrounding light drawn from beyond it. |
| **Point in the Heart (Nekuda SheBaLev)** | The seed of spiritual desire, distinct from the heart's desires for this world; the source of the pull toward the surrounding light. |
| **Single Concealment / Double Concealment** | Single concealment: the Creator is felt as present though hidden in the background. Double concealment: nothing is felt at all. |
| **Recognition of Evil** | The stage of questioning why one's own desires are "bad," passed through gradually on the way to the barrier (Machsom). |
| **Lo Lishma / Lishma** | Wanting to feel good for oneself (lo lishma) versus wanting the Creator's giving to be well-received because it matters to Him (lishma). |
| **Prayer (redefined)** | Not a request or expression of gratitude in words, but the deepest, settled feeling reached after complete self-examination of one's actual state. |
| **Hesed / Hochma (morning / evening)** | Morning work is described as acting without Hochma's illumination (in mercy); evening is when Hochma illuminates what was done during the day. |
| **Bread of Shame** | The unpleasantness a created being would feel receiving unearned pleasure; the reason for concealment, discussed in the article portion of the lesson. |

### Opening

The lesson opens from a Zohar puzzle: when the Torah and the Tabernacle were erected, the Creator wanted to count the armies of Israel — but the Creator already knows everything without counting. So who actually needs this count? The same question is asked of Abraham after the binding of Isaac: "for now I know that you fear God" — didn't the Creator already know Abraham feared Him before the test? The lesson's central question is this: if the Creator loses nothing by not testing or counting, what does a person gain by coming to know his own spiritual state? The second half of the lesson, from a separate 2002 recording, extends this into a practical formula for daily work: how to hold "if I am not for myself, who is for me" and "there is none else besides Him" together, without either one canceling the other.

---

## Part 1 — Why Does a Person Need to Know?

### The Test Is for Abraham, Not for the Creator

Baal HaSulam's question on the Abraham story sets the pattern: the Creator did not need proof of Abraham's fear of Him — only Abraham did. Withstanding the test simply lets a person know something about himself that he could not otherwise know for certain.

**Why would knowing matter?** The lecture ties this to a proper understanding of fear of God. The sages said, "everything is in the hands of God except for the fear of God" — meaning the Creator does everything, and the one thing left for a person to bring is fear:

> "Fear means that he is afraid that he will decline in bringing contentment to his Maker."

Fear, in this sense, is not fear of punishment. It is a person's own reluctance to receive anything he cannot be sure is for the Creator's sake rather than his own — "for this reason, he avoids asking the Creator to impart upon him delight and pleasure, as it might not be for the Creator's sake, but for his own."

### Bread of Shame and the Reason for Concealment

The lecture roots this in the purpose of creation: the Creator's desire to do good to His creations, so that they receive delight and pleasure. Because unearned reception carries shame, the Tzimtzum and concealment were made so a person could instead work to receive only in a form that brings contentment to the Creator:

> "In order not to have the bread of shame... the Tzimtzum and concealment were made so they would not feel the delight and pleasure in the purpose of creation, and would be able to work for the sake of the Creator and not for their own sake."

A person who has withstood real tests has already reached a degree of confidence that whatever he receives will be aimed at bestowal, not self-benefit — "for now I know that you fear God" becomes, in the work, a person's own confirmed awareness of himself.

## Part 2 — The Seer of Lublin: Paid, but Losing the Next World

### A Story of Reward Without Love

The lecture tells a story about the Rebbe of Lublin, who one Shabbat eve gathered his students and posed a paradox from the verse "and repays those who hate Him to their faces, to destroy them" — meaning the Creator pays His enemies with good deeds done in this world specifically so they will lose the next world. If a man chases money, he's paid in money; if he chases honor, he's paid in honor. But what of someone who wants neither money nor honor, but only status — to be called a rabbi or a teacher? The answer: he is given exactly that, high degrees and title, and by receiving it, he loses the next world all the same.

### Why Being Rewarded Can Mean Losing Everything

The lecture uses this to explain why the evil inclination is called an "enemy": it advises a person toward what feels like enjoyment, but by keeping his Torah and Mitzvot aimed at his own reward rather than love of the Creator, it blocks him from bestowal entirely — "one who observes Torah and Mitzvot for a reward... is considered the Creator's enemy because he obstructs the Creator, delaying what the Creator wants to give the created beings."

The corrective is framed through parenthood: a parent gives to a child purely out of love, wanting nothing back, and the child's enjoyment brings the parent contentment. Applied to the work, a person who observes Torah and Mitzvot out of love for the Creator likewise wants nothing in return — and so what he receives comes "in the vessels of bestowal," with no shame attached, rather than in the vessels of reception.

## Part 3 — Two Ways of Saying "I Withstood the Test"

### Before and After: Mighty Man or Helped by the Creator?

The lecture draws a distinction in how a person is meant to speak about his own effort, citing Baal HaSulam directly:

> "Before the fact, a person should say, if I am not for me, who is for me... Afterward, a person should say that everything comes from above, and he must not say, my power and the might of my hand have gotten me this wealth."

Both statements are true, depending on which side of the action a person is standing on. Before acting, a person must proceed as though everything depends on his own effort. After acting, he attributes the outcome entirely to the Creator's help. Knowing that he has withstood tests — on either accounting — gives him the confidence to keep demanding of the Creator that he be brought to the goal.

## Part 4 — Second Lesson: "If I Am Not for Myself" and "There Is None Else Besides Him"

The second half draws on a separate 2002 recording built around the question: what does it mean that a person needs to be tested, and what is the relationship between two seemingly opposite instructions — going out to act as though everything depends on oneself, and recognizing that there is none else besides the Creator?

### The Kli Comes to Know the Light Through Its Own Reaction

The lecture frames all spiritual work as the vessel (Kli) coming to recognize what the Light is doing to it: the Light shapes the Kli entirely, but the Kli's task is to examine the depth of that shaping and arrive at genuine gratitude or genuine prayer — "from your actions, we know You." Once a person has fully felt and justified an action of the Creator's, he becomes aligned with it, ready for the next stage.

### Dressing Desire: Doing It Myself, in His Desire

The lecture describes the relationship between the two mantras as one desire "dressed" inside the other, not two separate stances:

> "That's called the dressing desire and the desire that is dressed... you receive that movement in your own desire and, as it were, carry it out together with Him."

A person begins by acting as though "if I am not for myself, who is for me" — taking full responsibility, as if no other force will act for him — while never fully detaching from the underlying awareness that it is really the Creator's action taking place through him. Detaching from that awareness completely, the lecture warns, reduces a person to acting with no goal-orientation at all.

### Baal HaSulam's Morning and Evening

Citing Baal HaSulam's letters directly, the lecture gives a daily formula: in the morning, a person says "if I am not for myself, who is for me" and goes to work — entering the vessels of reception, laboring, "sweating" — without yet the light of Chochma to illuminate why. In the evening, having examined the day, he says "there is none else besides Him," recognizing that the same work and the same payment came entirely from the Creator:

> "In the morning, it was on his account. And in the evening, even though there is darkness, on your account, there is the light."

### The Tailor Example

To show what changes and what doesn't, the lecture offers the image of a tailor sewing clothes. Someone who fully lived "there is none else besides Him" might imagine simply sitting back and watching the garment sew itself — but that isn't the point. The physical actions of sewing remain exactly the same; only the tailor's inner attitude toward the work changes. The Creator "clothes Himself" within the person's attitude, not within a different set of hand movements.

## Part 5 — Q&A: Holding Both Attitudes Without Losing Either

### How Not to Forget

Asked how a person keeps "there is none else besides Him" in mind while actually studying, working, or eating, the answer is that a person cannot rely on constant conscious recall — instead, he sets himself reminders, at first mechanical (every fifteen minutes, every half hour), to return to that awareness:

> "A person needs to make for himself all kinds of signs, reminders... at first mechanically... later he does it himself. That's the work."

Over time, these small returns awaken what the lecture calls surrounding light, which gradually makes the awareness feel constant rather than deliberately manufactured — described as reaching "single concealment," where the Creator is felt as present but concealed in the background, rather than "double concealment," where His presence isn't felt at all. Rabash himself is given as an example of someone who lived in that constant awareness without ever really disconnecting from it.

### Why Not Just Stay in "There Is None Else Besides Him"?

A student asks whether pursuing greater adhesion risks losing the connection already gained. The answer: the two stances must remain "dressed" in one another. Fully separating from "there is none else besides Him" in order to act turns the action into something with no spiritual aim at all — while fully staying in it without acting leaves a person passive, unable to do the work of examination that leads to real prayer. Neither extreme is the goal; the two must be carried together, continually, throughout a day's actions.

## Part 6 — Recognition of Evil and the Method of Self-Study

### The Two Lights, the Two Desires

The lecture describes an inner split that begins once the point in the heart awakens: alongside the ordinary heart, driven by the "thin candlelight" of this-world pleasure, a new point arises drawing on light from outside this world altogether. A person is then pulled between the desire for this world and the desire for the next, sometimes one dominating, sometimes the other — "usually it's a mixture of both."

### Recognition of Evil

This split eventually forces a person to examine his own desires honestly: are they bad, and if so, why, given that they bring pleasure? The lecture frames this stage — asking whether one's desires are opposed to the point in the heart, or simply competing with it — as the recognition of evil, a necessary stage on the way to the barrier (Machsom), reached only gradually, over long periods, through many incarnations of alternating longing and distraction.

### Self-Study as the Whole Method

The lecture closes by naming the entire practice "self-study": a person examines what he receives, how he is impressed by the Creator's influence, and what impression he would rather have — not expecting the Creator's actions to change, but working to change his own vessel's reception of them:

> "The problem is not that I need to change His actions. I need to change my impression of His actions."

This reframes the difference between Lo Lishma and Lishma one more time: wanting to feel good for its own sake is Lo Lishma; wanting to feel good specifically so as not to end up resenting or "cursing" the Creator over a bad feeling is Lishma. The final, settled feeling a person reaches after all this scrutiny — not a request made in words — is what the lecture calls prayer.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **A test reveals something to the person, not to the Creator.** Abraham's test and the counting of Israel's armies both answer the same question: who needs the knowledge? Only the created being does.
2. **Fear of God, properly understood, is reluctance to receive anything not aimed at the Creator's contentment** — not fear of punishment, but care that one's reception stays free of self-serving intent.
3. **Being rewarded in this world for self-serving good deeds can still mean losing the next world.** The Seer of Lublin's paradox shows that reward without love of the Creator obstructs, rather than fulfills, the purpose of creation.
4. **Effort and dependence on the Creator are both true, at different moments.** Before acting, proceed as if everything depends on you; afterward, attribute the outcome to the Creator.
5. **"If I am not for myself, who is for me" and "there is none else besides Him" must be dressed in one another, not held separately.** Detaching fully from either one — pure self-reliant action, or pure passive surrender — removes the spiritual aim from the work.
6. **Awareness of the Creator's constant presence is built gradually through deliberate, even mechanical, reminders**, not sustained by will alone; this is how "single concealment" is reached.
7. **The method is self-study: changing one's own impression of what one receives**, not the circumstances themselves — the endpoint of that scrutiny, arrived at honestly, is what the lecture calls prayer.

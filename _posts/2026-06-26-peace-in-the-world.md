### Opening

The session picks up Baal HaSulam's article on peace in the world at the heading "In the Absence of the Ability to Establish the Attribute of Truth, They Tried to Establish the Basic Attributes." The central question: if the ideal — a society run entirely on the "measure of truth," each person restricted to exactly what he needs — has failed every time it has been tried (communism, the kibbutz), what actually explains the sequence mercy, justice, and peace that societies fall back on instead, and can real equality ever be reached at all?

---

## Part 1 — Why the Measure of Truth Always Fails

Rav Laitman frames the article's opening problem: an ideal society run purely on "the measure of truth" — each person a "cogwheel" consuming only what he needs and contributing all he can — is repeatedly attempted and repeatedly fails, because every person also carries the Creator's quality of uniqueness: the wish to rule, to be the center, to decide. Communism failed; the kibbutz failed; every form of communal living built purely on this ideal failed for the same underlying reason.

### Mercy, Justice, and Peace as Fallback Measures

Because the measure of truth cannot be sustained, exploitation and weakness increase in society, giving rise to **mercy and justice** as compensating supports for the weak. When even this produces enough friction that the exploited protest against the successful, **peace** emerges as a further, still-lower fallback measure. The lesson traces this as a real historical and social sequence, not merely an abstract one.

---

## Part 2 — Is Inequality Itself the Problem?

### Confusing Corporeal Organization with Spiritual Correction

A student's question about "what's mine is mine, and yours is yours" prompts Rav Laitman to draw a sharp distinction: Baal HaSulam is describing how corporeal society can be organized, not describing the spiritual world — these should not be confused. In corporeal terms, natural inequality (some more capable, more persistent, more successful) is real, and the complaint against it is ultimately a complaint against the Creator, who made people unequal to begin with — not against the people who happen to benefit from that inequality.

### Why Inequality Exists At All

Rav Laitman states the deeper purpose directly: people are not equal specifically so that, through this very inequality, they can reach equality with the Creator — a state unreachable if people were already equal to begin with. **True equality is not sameness of ability, circumstance, or result; it is equality in the effort of bestowal.**

> *"We are not equal in order that, by this lack of equality between us, we will be able to reach equality with the Creator."*

---

## Part 3 — What Real Equality Actually Means

### Equal in Bestowal, Not in the Will to Receive

Rav Laitman explains that people will never be equal in the will to receive — some can "carry" a small load, others a large one, in the metaphor he uses (100 kilograms versus two tons) — but this is irrelevant, because what matters is only whether each person implements his full capacity to bestow. A person who gives 100% of his own ability, whatever that ability's size, is completely equal, in this respect, to anyone else who does the same. **"It can be Rabbi Shimon or Shimon from the market. They are both bestowing, and equal."**

### One Formula, Different Results

The practical consequence: there is one formula for everyone — restrict the will to receive, act entirely in order to bestow — even though the visible results differ. Those differing results are attributed to how the Creator organized each person, not to unequal effort. Equality with the Creator, in other words, is reached through the common act of bestowal, not through equal starting conditions or equal outcomes.

## Part 4 — Asking for What You Have No Experience Of

### The Puzzle Vlad Raises

A student (Vlad) raises a subtle problem: everything a person can ask for is necessarily shaped by his own accumulated experience — so how can a person ask the light for something entirely new, something he has no experience of at all, since he doesn't even know what to ask for? Rav Laitman frames this as needing to "disconnect from your experience" and ask as if from beneath the light itself, though he acknowledges the difficulty of fully grasping this in words.

### An Answer: Effort Toward the Group, Not Personal Outcome

Another participant (Chaim) offers a resolution: real equality is not in the differing results each person receives, but in the shared **effort to help each person — the group, the ten — advance toward spirituality**. Since this effort is the same kind of action for everyone, regardless of outcome, it is where the equality actually exists. He adds that on days of descent, a person may not realize it, but those very days are where the greatest attainment of true equality was available and, potentially, missed.

### Being Impressed by What You Don't Have

A further comment links this back to being impressed by the friends' qualities — one has one quality, another has another — which pulls a person "out of himself" toward something genuinely new, not derived from his own prior experience. This is offered as a practical way to actually reach for what one has no personal basis to ask for: through the example of the group rather than introspection alone.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The ideal of a society run purely on "the measure of truth" has failed every time it has been attempted** — because the Creator's quality of uniqueness (the wish to rule, to be central) exists in everyone.
2. **Mercy, justice, and peace emerged historically as successive fallback measures**, compensating for the failure of truth as an organizing principle.
3. **Inequality in this world is not a mistake to be corrected at the corporeal level** — the complaint about it is really a complaint against the Creator, who made people unequal from the start.
4. **People are unequal specifically so that, through that inequality, they can reach equality with the Creator** — a state that sameness would foreclose.
5. **Real equality is equality in the effort of bestowal, not in ability, circumstance, or outcome.** Full use of whatever capacity one has is what equalizes people, not the size of that capacity.
6. **Asking for something entirely outside one's own experience is a genuine puzzle** — one proposed answer is to focus effort on the shared work of the group rather than on personal, experience-bound requests.
7. **Being impressed by qualities one does not personally have is one practical way to reach beyond one's own experience**, drawing a person out of himself toward something new.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **The Measure of Truth** | The ideal of a society where each person acts entirely for the collective and takes only what he needs; repeatedly attempted and repeatedly failed. |
| **Quality of Uniqueness** | The wish to rule, decide, and be the center of the world — described as an aspect of the Creator's own quality present in every person, which undermines the measure of truth. |
| **Mercy, Justice, Peace** | Successive social fallback measures that emerge once the measure of truth proves unsustainable in practice. |
| **Equality of Bestowal** | The true form of equality in this teaching — full use of one's own capacity to bestow, regardless of the size of that capacity or the resulting outcome. |
| **Equivalence of Form** | The principle by which a person draws the light — matching one's own quality (bestowal) to the Creator's, rather than matching outcomes to other people. |
| **Disconnecting from Experience** | The challenge of asking the light for something genuinely new, not derivable from what a person has already experienced or already knows to want. |
| **The Ten (Tent/Group)** | The immediate group whose shared effort toward spirituality is proposed as the site of genuine equality, since the effort itself is common to all regardless of individual result. |

## Overview

This lesson studies Baal HaSulam's article *"Peace in the World,"* continuing from the previous session. The lesson explores the tension between the individual and the collective, the four social attributes (mercy, truth, justice, and peace), why the attribute of truth cannot be practically implemented in our world, and what the actual path to true equality looks like.


> **Central Question of the Article**
>
> How can we build a world where each person receives according to the root of their soul — as much as they deserve — when by nature every person is driven by the desire to rule, receive for themselves, and place their own benefit above all others?
{: .callout .callout-question}

---

## 1. The Individual and the Collective

### We Are Not Equal — By Design

A foundational insight of the article: human beings are not equal to one another, and this is not a flaw or a mistake. The Creator intentionally made each person different — in physical capacity, psychological makeup, desires, abilities, and inner structure. No two people are alike, even in their medical or psychological profiles.

- The question is not how to make everyone the same — that is impossible and would violate the Creator's design
- The question is how to build a society where each person receives what they genuinely need and deserve, according to the root of their own soul
- The problem: a person cannot know what they truly need until they know the root of their own soul — and most people do not know it


> "Until a person doesn't know his soul, his inner essence, he won't know what he's lacking."
{: .callout .callout-quote}

---

### The Individual as a Wheel in a Machine

Baal HaSulam describes society as a machine, and each individual as a wheel within it. The wheel does not move independently — it moves with the machine, and its value is measured entirely by what it contributes to the whole.

- Good and bad are not evaluated by the individual alone, but by the individual's impact on the collective
- One who benefits the collective takes their share of the benefit; one who harms the collective takes their share of the harm
- The freedom of the individual and the freedom of the collective are one and the same thing

This leads to a critical insight: it is in every person's self-interest to care for the collective, because the individual's wellbeing is entirely dependent on the collective's wellbeing.

---

## 2. The Four Attributes and Why Truth Fails

### The Four Social Attributes

Baal HaSulam identifies four attributes through which human society has tried to advance: **mercy, truth, justice, and peace.** These have driven human development — but through the slow and painful "authority of heaven" rather than through human choice.

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| **Truth** | Each gives fully to the collective and receives exactly according to their labor. The most ideal — but impossible to implement in practice. |
| **Mercy (Chesed)** | "What's mine is yours and what's yours is yours" — giving without taking. Cancels the need for other attributes, but cannot govern society alone. |
| **Justice** | Fair distribution; compensating for inequality. Contradicts both mercy and peace. |
| **Peace** | Emerged as a pragmatic response when the others failed — but is built on a shaky foundation. |

---

### Why Truth Cannot Be Implemented

The attribute of truth is theoretically perfect: each person contributes fully and receives exactly according to their labor. But Baal HaSulam shows it fails for two interconnected reasons.

#### Reason 1 — We Cannot Measure True Labor

- Physical capacity varies enormously — a weak person may exhaust themselves in one hour more than a strong person in two
- Psychological inclination varies — a naturally lazy person may strain more in one hour than an energetic person in three
- You cannot simply count hours or outputs; there is no objective, fair unit of labor that can be universally applied

#### Reason 2 — The Law of Singularity

More fundamentally, the nature of every person contains what Baal HaSulam calls the **"law of singularity"** — the feeling that the entire world was created for me, that I should rule, decide, receive, and determine reality for my own benefit.

- This is not a character flaw — it extends directly from the Creator, who is singular and unique, to the human soul which branches from Him
- Every person carries this quality: the great according to their greatness, the small according to their smallness
- It is an unbreakable law. No one escapes it. The only difference is the way it is expressed — through wealth, power, honor, or all three


> "The nature of each and every person is to exploit the lives of all other people in the world for his own benefit, and all that he gives to another is only out of necessity."
{: .callout .callout-quote}

This law is called the **"absolute truth"** — not because it is good, but because it is the actual operating reality of human nature. Society in theory agrees to truth; in practice it does the opposite.

---

## 3. How Mercy, Justice, and Peace Arose

Because the attribute of truth could not be implemented, the other three attributes emerged as support systems — props for a broken structure:

- When truth failed, the exploited and weak multiplied. From this emerged **mercy and justice** — society's successful members supported the struggling to prevent social collapse
- When mercy and justice were insufficient, the struggling proliferated until they could protest and fight the successful. From this pressure emerged the attribute of **peace**
- Peace was not born from wisdom or genuine care for the collective — it was born from conflict and the threat of rebellion


> **Baal HaSulam's Insight**
>
> Mercy, justice, and peace are not ideal attributes. They are compensations — born from the weakness of truth and the exploitation that followed. They are bandages, not cures.
{: .callout .callout-warning}

---

### Constructors vs. Destructors

Baal HaSulam divides society into two basic types:

| Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Constructors** | Inclined toward mercy and charity; willing to sacrifice for the collective's benefit. Tend to be the weaker, "faint-hearted" force in society. |
| **Destructors** | Inclined toward "what's mine is mine" — bold, willing to risk everything for their truth. Tend to dominate society despite causing harm. |

The destructors, paradoxically, always end up as the "peacemakers" — because they have the courage and power to impose their vision. But peace built by destructors is inherently unstable, because it never satisfies the dissatisfied minority, which grows, finds fuel in new quarrels, and produces new cycles of conflict.

## 4. The Path to True Equality

### Inequality Is Not the Problem — It Is the Tool

One of the most striking insights from the lesson: our inequality is not an error to be corrected. It is the very mechanism through which true equality with the Creator can be reached.


> "We are not equal in order that from this lack of equality between us we will be able to reach equality with the Creator."
{: .callout .callout-quote}

- If we were all equal, there would be no differentiation — and therefore no way to reveal the Creator, who can only be revealed through contrast and difference
- The lack of equality creates the need for connection, for each one to complete the other
- True equality is not equality of desires or capacities — it is **equality of effort in the direction of bestowal**

---

### What True Equality Actually Means

Rav clarified this in the lesson discussion:

- Each person is different in their vessel (will to receive) — and will always remain so
- But above the vessel, in the quality of bestowal, everyone can be equal — because the measure is **effort**, not capacity
- Whether you are Rabbi Shimon or Shimon from the market, if both are bestowing at 100% of their ability, they are equal in the eyes of the Creator
- In spiritual reality, one does not act from one's own desires at all — only from the desires of others


> **Key Formula**
>
> **True equality = equality of effort in bestowing, not equality of desire or capacity.**  
> The result differs (each soul's vessel is different), but the effort is one standard for all.
{: .callout .callout-tip}

---

### The Obstacle: Working Without Experience

A key question raised in the lesson discussion (Vlad's question):


> "Everything I ask for or want is based on my experience. Here, what's necessary is to reach something new that I have no experience in. How do I ask the Light to do something to me when I don't even know what to ask?"
{: .callout .callout-quote}

This is the fundamental challenge of the spiritual path: the next state is entirely outside your current frame of reference. Your desires, your imagination, your past — all of it belongs to the current degree.

- This is why the group (the ten) is essential — you can be inspired by qualities your friends have that you do not
- When a friend's bestowal impresses you and you incorporate it into yourself, something truly new enters — something not built on your own experience
- Days of descent are precisely the days when the most could be attained — they are moments of greatest potential for real growth, not obstacles to it

---

## 5. The Singularity — From Destruction to Construction

The "law of singularity" appears destructive. But Baal HaSulam reveals it has **two sides**:

| Aspect | Description |
|---|---|
| **Lower Side (Current State)** | The singularity operates in self-reception: I want everything for myself — wealth, power, honor. This is the source of all destruction and conflict in the world. |
| **Upper Side (Corrected State)** | The same singularity, when corrected, becomes bestowal upon all others — because the Creator, who is singular, only bestows and never receives. When we align our singularity with its source, it flips direction. |

This means the very quality that drives all human selfishness is also the quality that — once corrected — will drive complete and universal bestowal. **The tool does not change; only the direction of its use changes.**

- We are not in the lowest phase anymore — there are already people who use their singularity in forms of bestowal
- At the end of correction, all singularity will operate only as bestowal, and self-reception will have no instance

---

## 6. Hasten It vs. In Its Time

Baal HaSulam builds on a teaching from the Talmud (*Sanhedrin* 98): *"Rewarded — I will hasten it; not rewarded — in its time."*

- The end of correction is **guaranteed** — it will come regardless. The only question is how
- **"In its time"** means through the slow, painful steamroller of development — degrees of suffering accumulating until the soul can no longer remain in the lower state and is forced upward
- **"I will hasten it"** means taking the law of development under one's own authority — choosing to correct bad attributes proactively, through study, group work, and inner effort


> **The Choice Before Each Person**
>
> You can wait for suffering to push you forward — or you can run toward correction yourself. The destination is the same. Only the journey differs. Taking development under your own authority frees you completely from the chains of time.
{: .callout .callout-note}

## Article Summary

*"Peace in the World"* by Baal HaSulam proceeds in a logical sequence:

1. **Everything develops gradually.** Bad qualities are not to be destroyed — they are in a stage of development toward their completion.

2. **Two authorities govern development.** The authority of heaven (slow, painful, inevitable) and the authority of earth (rapid, chosen, merciful).

3. **Individual and collective are one.** Each person is a wheel in a machine; good and bad are evaluated only by impact on the whole.

4. **Truth is ideal but unworkable.** Labor cannot be measured; the law of singularity makes genuine truth-based society impossible in the current human state.

5. **Mercy, justice, and peace arose as substitutes.** They are compensations for the failure of truth, not genuine solutions.

6. **The four attributes contradict one another.** Mercy cancels truth; peace contradicts justice; they fight both between people and within each individual.

7. **The law of singularity — the root of all destruction — is also the seed of complete correction,** when redirected from self-reception to bestowal upon others.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Baal HaSulam** | Rabbi Yehuda Leib Ashlag (1884–1954); author of "Peace in the World" and the Sulam commentary on the Zohar. Name means "Master of the Ladder." |
| **Law of Singularity** | The natural feeling in each person that they are the center of the world. Extends from the Creator's singularity; currently operating in self-reception but destined to be redirected to bestowal. |
| **Absolute Truth** | Baal HaSulam's term for the law of singularity — not morally good, but the actual operating truth of human nature as it currently exists. |
| **Authority of Heaven** | The slow, painful process of development through suffering — guaranteed to bring correction eventually, but over a very long time. |
| **Authority of Earth** | The human choice to take one's own development in hand — proactively correcting bad qualities through study and effort, independent of time. |
| **Constructors** | People inclined toward mercy and charity; willing to sacrifice for the collective's benefit. Tend to be the weaker force in society. |
| **Destructors** | People inclined toward "what's mine is mine" — bold, willing to risk everything for their truth. Tend to dominate society despite causing harm. |
| **The Ten (Minyan)** | The foundational group unit in Kabbalah study; the environment where new spiritual experience can enter from outside one's own frame of reference. |
| **Bestowal** | Acting entirely for the benefit of others without any self-interest; the quality of the Creator and the goal of spiritual correction. |
| **End of Correction (Gmar Tikkun)** | The state where all singularity operates only as bestowal; complete equivalence of form with the Creator. |

## Reflection & Study Questions

1. Baal HaSulam says that in theory, society agrees to truth, but in practice does the opposite. Where do you see this in your own life — publicly agreeing to values you privately don't follow?

2. The law of singularity says every person feels the world was created for them. Can you observe this quality operating in yourself? What form does it take — wealth, power, respect, something else?

3. If true equality is equality of effort in bestowing rather than equality of desire or result, how does that change how you evaluate your own spiritual progress?

4. Vlad asked: how do you ask for something you have no experience of? How would you answer that based on what you heard in the lesson?

5. On a day of descent — when spirituality feels impossible and distant — the lesson suggests that is actually when the most could be attained. What would it look like, practically, to use a day of descent in that way?

6. We are not equal so that through our inequality we can reach equality with the Creator. Where in your ten do you see this principle at work — where the differences between people create something none of them could reach alone?

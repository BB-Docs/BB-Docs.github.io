### Opening

This is the annual memorial lesson for the Ari, built around a single practical question: given the greatest spiritual opportunity in history, why did even the Ari's own students find excuses not to act on it? The story of the Ari calling his students to go up to Jerusalem becomes the live test case for the lesson's real topic — announced partway through by Rav Laitman himself — annulment and subjugation before the Rav, and what that actually requires of a person today.

## Part 1 — Connecting to the Soul of the Ari

### Why Mark This Day

Studying a Kabbalist's words on the day of his passing is described as the way to "connect with that very special soul." A student asked directly how this connection is made, and Rav Laitman's answer was simple: not through any special technique, but "through the ten, for sure... through the same rules of the wisdom of Kabbalah that are the same for everyone... through that same rule of love your friend as yourself."

### Who Was the Ari Writing For?

Asked who the Ari wrote his book for, given that only a handful of students ever studied with him directly, Rav Laitman answered:

> "Ari wrote his things for everyone... When a Kabbalist writes and he opens up whatever he wrote, it's for everyone... So long as the person would want to know what does he exist for, who is managing him, and to whom should he come closer."

### Was the Group a "Vessel" for His Revelation?

A student asked whether the Ari's group of students was somehow the vessel through which he received his revelation. Rav Laitman was direct in rejecting this:

> "He was so high, he took from them a certain deficiency, nevertheless, by being connected with them. But it's not that the group was seemingly somehow took part in his writing. No, it was such a soul that was truly disconnected from everyone."

### Two Roles: Revealer and Disseminator

Rav Laitman drew a distinction between the Ari's role and that of the Baal Shem Tov a century later: the Ari revealed the system of the upper worlds "scientifically," while the Baal Shem Tov clothed it in stories so ordinary people could approach it.

> "One of them acted in order to reveal... the system of reality, the forces that work in reality... This is what the Ari did, and how the Baal Shem Tov clothed this in all kinds of stories... The Ari revealed, and Baal Shem Tov disseminated."

## Part 2 — The Story: The Ari's Call to Jerusalem

On the eve of a Sabbath, the Ari told his students it was the most auspicious moment for redemption since Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's time, and asked them to go with him to Jerusalem at once to build the Temple. Some students objected that Jerusalem was too far to reach in time; others agreed to go, but first wanted to tell their wives so they would not worry. By the time they returned, the moment had passed, and the Ari told them the opportunity for redemption was lost.

### What Does "the Slandering of Satan Succeeded" Mean?

A student asked for clarification of this phrase from the story. Rav Laitman explained that Satan is not a figure but a force:

> "Satan means the force that works against connection, the force that works against... holiness, which the Creator Himself is managing on purpose... This is what it means that the slandering of Satan succeeded — that the force of resistance, the opposing force, was so great that they were not able to overcome it."

### Great Opportunity, Great Difficulty

Asked why the students hesitated at all given the stakes, Rav Laitman connected it to a general principle of the work:

> "Along with the fact that a person is given an opportunity that there is none greater than that, along with that he's also given the burdening of the heart, some difficulties, and he cannot do anything against it but only with faith above reason."

### The Temple Is Not a Building

Several students pressed on what "going up to Jerusalem to build the Temple" actually means in the work. Rav Laitman's answer reframed the entire story as being about connection, not geography or construction:

> "It's not a building. It's a vessel that wants to be in bestowal like the Creator... We only need to get close in the connection between us, and that this coming closer to connection between us shall determine the form of the temple, meaning the form of our soul itself... That's called the third temple. We don't need to build it... We need to build it only out of our desires."

Asked whether we are the architects of this Temple or its builders, he answered: "We only want to participate in this building, because in the extent that we agree, it is built."

## Part 3 — Faith Above Reason and Adhesion to the Teacher

### Two Actions of Adhesion

A student asked what "adhesion to the teacher" concretely means today, since we cannot always follow instructions the way the Ari's own students were asked to. Rav Laitman's answer named two specific actions:

> "We have two actions. In this, we perform what the teacher, our teachers, say — that's action of connection in the ten — and action of dissemination to the entire vessel of Adam HaRishon, all of humanity."

### Faith Above Reason as Trained Response

One student compared this to how soldiers are trained to act automatically, before thinking, and asked how a person reaches that kind of immediate response to the Rav's instruction. Rav Laitman's answer:

> "That's on the condition that instead of your mind, you are adhered to the mind of the group. And then you perform without thinking twice what the group thinks and decides. That's it. That's called to be in faith above reason."

### Why the End of Correction Isn't Reached "Right Now"

Asked whether the end of correction could be reached in that very moment, Rav Laitman located the obstacle not in effort but in collective will:

> "If you'll connect inside the heart such that you'll be as one heart... the request of the Creator to perform this connection — for you the Creator needs to do this, we're not capable — on us is only dependent the request. Then it will occur, and it only depends on my ten."

## Part 4 — Annulment and Subjugation Before the Rav

Rav Laitman named this as the actual topic of the lesson's second half, using the Ari's story as its moral. The excerpts here move from the Ari's teachings to Rabash's article "May We Be the Head and Not the Tail."

### The First Condition: Annulment

> "If you come to study, the first condition is annulment. Why? Because the Creator chose for us to be — me, a teacher, and you, the student — you have to annul yourself before the teacher... You don't have to lick my shoes, but you need to annul your reason towards my reason in the spiritual work."

### How Criticism Is Handled

Rav Laitman explained that criticism of the teacher is not simply forbidden outright, but has to be turned back into a tool for annulment rather than separation: "It's impossible for there to be any criticism, and if there is criticism, it's criticism in order to annul himself even more with the backing of that criticism."

### Achap, Katnut, and Gadlut

The excerpt from Rabash on the relationship between the "upper one" and "lower one" was read and unpacked:

> "The lower one must first believe that there is an upper one in reality... Hence, when one begins to observe the greatness of the upper one... the lower one also ascends and begins to attain the feeling of godliness. And this depends on the measure that it pains him that he sees the faults of the upper one. Thus, to that extent, the upper one becomes ascended in him."

Rav Laitman's summary: this dependency is not really about the upper one at all — "it follows that this is a correction for the sake of the lower one."

### Faith in the Sages

The second Rabash excerpt addressed why a person is asked to follow the sages "with eyes shut":

> "Since as long as one is placed under the governance of the will to receive for himself, he cannot know what is good and what is bad, [he] must accept everything the way the sages determined for us... specifically by this, we are rewarded with knowledge of holiness."

The reasoning given: self-love cannot judge correctly what is good, so going above reason — accepting guidance without first subjecting it to self-interested judgment — is what earns the vessels of bestowal, not a rejection of the mind itself.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. Connecting to a great Kabbalist's soul, even on his memorial day, happens the same way as any spiritual connection — through the ten and through "love your friend as yourself" — not through any special ritual or technique.

2. The Ari's revelation and the Baal Shem Tov's dissemination were two distinct, necessary roles: one uncovered the system, the other made it approachable. Both were required for the teaching to reach later generations.

3. A great spiritual opportunity always arrives bundled with a great difficulty — this is not a design flaw but the mechanism by which faith above reason becomes necessary and possible.

4. "Satan" in the work names the force that resists connection, not a being; the Ari's students lost their opportunity because that resistance overpowered them, not because of literal travel logistics.

5. The "third temple" is the corrected form of the collective soul, built only through the connection between people — never a physical structure to be assembled from outside.

6. Adhesion to the teacher today consists of two concrete actions: connection within one's ten, and dissemination of the method of correction outward to all of humanity.

7. Annulment before a teacher does not mean suppressing all response to what one sees as flaws — it means turning that very discomfort into the leverage for one's own correction, since what is truly at stake is the lower one's growth, not the upper one's standing.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Ari (Rabbi Isaac Luria) | 16th-century Kabbalist, author of *Etz Chaim* ("Tree of Life"); first to lay out the complete system of the upper worlds and their descent into ours. |
| Chaim Vital | The Ari's foremost disciple, who recorded and transmitted his teachings after his death. |
| Baal Shem Tov | Founder of the Hasidic movement; disseminated the Ari's system in accessible story form rather than adding new revelation. |
| Annulment (Bittul) | Nullifying one's own reason before the teacher's, the first condition for receiving spiritual guidance. |
| Faith Above Reason | Accepting guidance from the sages without first subjecting it to self-interested judgment, since the will to receive cannot reliably judge good from bad on its own. |
| Achap / AHP | "Ear-Nose-Mouth" — part of a Partzuf's structure; here used for the "greatness" of the upper one that determines the lower one's Katnut. |
| Galgalta veEinayim (GE) | "Skull and Eyes" — the other part of a Partzuf's structure, paired with AHP. |
| Katnut / Gadlut | Smallness / greatness — the lower one's perception depends on first recognizing the Gadlut (greatness) of the upper one. |
| Adam HaRishon | The single collective soul containing all souls before the shattering; "the vessel of Adam HaRishon" is the target of dissemination. |
| Satan / Sitra Achra | Not a being but the force of resistance to connection and holiness. |
| Third Temple | Not a building — the corrected, unified form of the collective soul, built only through connection between people. |
| Baal HaSulam | Rabbi Yehuda Ashlag, author of the Sulam commentary on the Zohar; founder of the teaching lineage including Rabash and Rav Laitman. |
| Rabash | Rabbi Baruch Shalom Ashlag, son and successor of Baal HaSulam; author of "May We Be the Head and Not the Tail," discussed in Part 4. |

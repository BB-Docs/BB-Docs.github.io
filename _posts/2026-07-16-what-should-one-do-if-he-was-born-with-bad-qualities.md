### Opening

The article read at the start of the lesson takes up the Zohar's claim that a person's natural character — lustful, quick-tempered, arrogant, or lazy — comes purely from the merging of the four elements (fire, wind, water, dust) in the body, and has nothing to do with the good or evil inclination, "like the beasts of the world." Rav Laitman's lecture and the Q&A that follows push this into a practical question: if a person's basic nature genuinely cannot be changed, what is there to actually do? The lecture works through why these four qualities can never be eliminated, why connection between people is impossible on the level of the beast, what pride and its cure ("Pharaoh") actually are, and — in the closing exchange — how a person's demand from study itself matures from a first, small Lo Lishma into what Rav Laitman calls "the greatest Lo Lishma," standing right at the door of Lishma.

---

## Part 1 — Beast, Adam, and the Four Elements

### The Four Qualities Are Not the Evil Inclination

Rav Laitman opens from the article's own conclusion: the four beastly qualities — lazy, lustful, quick to anger, prideful (the "spender") — are simply what fire, wind, water, and dust produce when they merge in a body, and this is true "and it doesn't matter with which character one was born."

> "It's not that one hinders the other. Being lazy doesn't hinder being lustful, right? And being easily agitated also doesn't stand in the way... Meaning, these four elements can really exist all together."

### Why Psychology and Self-Improvement Don't Touch This

A person can become "a very great Kabbalist," he says, and still keep all four qualities untouched, because working on them directly is not the discipline at all:

> "So these things cannot be changed... and remain with all of these qualities and not relate to them. This is simply not the alphabet, the one and two, of our method... not related to the beastly qualities but to the relation to the Creator."

Engaging the qualities themselves psychologically, trying to eliminate laziness or pride as such, "will come out of nothing" — because these traits belong to the beast in a person, not to the Adam that the work is meant to grow.

---

## Part 2 — Where Good and Evil Inclination Actually Begin

### Only With Respect to the Creator

Rav Laitman draws the line sharply: the good and evil inclination do not describe a person's natural character at all — they only exist in relation to the Creator, once the "Adam" within a person starts to grow.

> "With respect to the Creator... we can weigh the good inclination, the bad inclination, all of those things that we have to sort and grow, or vice versa eliminate — but it is only with respect to the Creator, not with respect to myself, my nature."

The same rule applies to how a person relates to a friend: not by weighing his beastly character, but by the same relation to the Creator that governs one's own work.

### Connection Is Impossible on the Beastly Level

Asked about working together with a friend, Rav Laitman says that on the level of the beast, "my beast and his beast" make real connection between two people impossible — "each one is alone... a person is alone in the world." He points to the collapse of family, tribe, and nation in the modern world as this same ego growing without limit: "The ego destroys everything... it makes the person the individual."

> "The connection can be only through the Creator... only through Godliness. Only through the connection with the Creator can there be a connection between people."

Even a small group, he says, can exist on this condition alone — and this is the only basis on which "love your friend as yourself" can ever actually be kept.

### Faith Is the Measure of the Bond

He names this connection to the Creator directly as faith — "this parameter within me is called the measure of faith that I have" — and ties it to a single condition: "My connection with the Creator can exist on the condition that I want to bestow to Him. It cannot be in any other way." Bestowal, he adds, can only happen "on the condition that I feel him" — that the light comes and corrects.

## Part 3 — Pride and "Pharaoh"

### What Pride Actually Is

A student's question about pride leads Rav Laitman to a direct definition: pride is the feeling of one's own "I" as the true, unsurrenderable foundation.

> "What is pride? Pride means that I feel myself, my 'I'... my self is more important than anything else, more than any other person, more than the Creator, more than everything. This is called a Pharaoh."

He casts this inner Pharaoh in the biblical line — "Who is the Lord that I should hear his voice? I am in control, not him, not anyone else" — and says that as long as this quality remains uncorrected in a person, there can be no real closeness or equivalence of form with the Creator. Corrected, however, this same substance — "Pharaoh is all of our substance" — becomes, in his words, "the most holy," used entirely in order to bestow.

### Pride and Lowliness Together

A student objects: if a person genuinely feels, through the light, that he is "the lowest and worst in the whole world," how can pride coexist with that? Rav Laitman answers that it does, and that this is not a contradiction:

> "You can feel together with being the most prideful person, with that also that you are the most despicable zero as well. One does not cancel the other."

Without the light showing a person the qualities opposite to his pride, he says, the pride isn't even visible as pride — "it's a quality that's clothed upon me." Once the light does shine, a person receives two separate discernments at once: that he is prideful, and what the opposing quality (the quality of the light) actually is.

## Part 4 — The Root That Cannot Be Changed

### Adam HaRishon and the Four Phases

Rav Laitman traces the four qualities back further: they are the "root of the soul" from Adam HaRishon after the shattering, which "lost the screen" but kept its structure — "the tip of Yud, Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei, the four phases... with the Keter, it's five. These four phases must be in every created being, in every desire."

> "You cannot, also, not any one of them, nor the merging of them, you can't change that. That's your root in Adam HaRishon. That's your original 'I,' the matter with which you're created."

### Suppression Is Not Correction

The most that effort or willpower alone can do, he says, is suppress these qualities — never change them. He names the Stoics and "all the philosophers... all the ancient times" methods, along with modern self-control techniques, as all falling into this same category: teaching a person "to control forcefully over the natural qualities," making him "see himself as a hero, as a man" — "but by this he is not an Adam. He's not a human... he is suppressing them, is not changing them."

He extends this to education itself: decades of trying "as little pressure as possible" have not solved the underlying problem, because — as a student proposes — the qualities themselves (pride, lust, laziness) get judged only through simple, corporeal concepts, measured against what one's own society happens to respect or condemn, rather than in relation to the Creator.

## Part 5 — Q&A: Aiming the Qualities, Not Eliminating Them

### The Four Qualities Are Not Disturbances — They're the Material

Asked whether the disturbances a person receives always arrive specifically through these same four qualities, Rav Laitman answers that they are not disturbances at all, but the raw material of the will to receive itself: "Laziness is a calculation you make in the will to receive — why should I move a finger?" People differ from one another only in how these four elements combine, in both corporeality and spirituality — and the very same qualities that exist today will still be present at the end of correction, "in complete negation," but reoriented entirely toward the Creator.

### A Concrete Test: Getting Up at 3 A.M.

Rav Laitman gives a direct, personal example of what it means to aim a beastly quality rather than eliminate it. Lying in bed at three in the morning, deciding whether it's "worthwhile" to get up for the lesson, he says the same calculating quality is at work either way:

> "If I aim those qualities toward my spiritual advancement, I'm using them correctly. But if I use them toward the world around me, then what will I get out of it? They gave me those qualities... From those qualities I need to determine my attitude to the Creator."

He applies this to his own impatience and pride as well — "also my spending, my pride, my impatience, all those things from which I advance... I aim them at the Creator, not those things in and of themselves... not for people around me. I have no calculation with that." Asked how this squares with also saying these qualities assist the work, he affirms both at once: "Of course I use them — how can I not use them? They are in me, they act in me... I can only determine in what form, what measure, what attitude... I use them more correctly."

## Part 6 — From Lo Lishma to the Greatest Lo Lishma

### Reasoning Toward the Creator

In the closing exchange, a student walks through his own reasoning the previous day: recognizing life as fundamentally empty ("just like I was born, that's how I'll die"), then recalling that connection to the Creator would be good, and asking how — concluding that only through study, and thinking of Him during study, does "some light, some force" bring a person closer.

> "Rather, only through help from above, the light will come and open my eyes. So I need this light. That's why I come and study. That's the beginning of Lo Lishma."

### The Greatest Lo Lishma

From this small beginning, Rav Laitman describes a gradual escalation: the demand shifts from wanting things to be good for oneself, toward genuinely wanting to bestow to the Creator and be connected to Him — even while still, honestly, doing this because "I am certain that by that it would be good for me." This continues until it reaches what he calls the greatest Lo Lishma:

> "The greatest Lo Lishma is: I want to bestow to the Creator, and to be with Him, like I love Him, until I truly don't feel myself to that extent... just like with love, where the feeling of love fills a person to such an extent that it seemingly turns off his will to receive."

He is careful to distinguish this from actual Lishma: even at its greatest, this state still "constantly wants to come to implement, to realize his desire, his expectations" — it is Lo Lishma still, just of an increasingly refined kind, each time the demand from study rises to "a different way, in a higher way," until the person enters Lishma itself.

### What the Study Actually Draws

Rav Laitman closes this line of questioning by stressing that a person must come to study with a precise, felt lack — otherwise "that's study without benefit." The light that answers this lack, he says, comes through the labor of the study itself and through the friends: "through the labor that you're giving forth here, during the study, you draw upon yourself this light that brings life, that brings health, that brings strength, that brings everything." He gives a plain, corporeal example of the same principle — a friend struggling with his character coming simply to study so "the light in it will give you the answer how to do your character, how to do your role" — and notes that before the study, a person needs some clarity, worked out together as a group, on exactly what he is asking the surrounding light to change in him.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The four beastly qualities (lazy, lustful, quick-tempered, prideful) come purely from the four elements (FWWD) and have nothing to do with the good or evil inclination** — that distinction exists only in relation to the Creator, not in relation to one's own nature or to other people.
2. **These qualities cannot be changed, only suppressed or aimed** — willpower, philosophy, and education can at best control them by force, producing "a hero" but not an Adam; real correction reorients them toward the Creator rather than eliminating them.
3. **Real connection between people is impossible on the beastly level** — "each one is alone"; connection, including "love your friend as yourself," exists only through a shared connection to the Creator.
4. **Pride is the feeling that one's own "I" outweighs everyone else, including the Creator** — personified as an inner "Pharaoh"; uncorrected, it blocks equivalence of form, but corrected, this same substance becomes wholly devoted to bestowal.
5. **A person can feel simultaneously prideful and utterly lowly — one does not cancel the other**; the light reveals both the pride itself and the opposing quality at the same time.
6. **The four qualities trace back to the root of the soul in Adam HaRishon (the "four phases" plus Keter)** and are present, unchangeable in essence, in every created desire — the same qualities remain even at the end of correction, only fully redirected toward the Creator.
7. **The demand from study matures gradually, from a first small Lo Lishma to "the greatest Lo Lishma,"** each stage raising the same underlying request — until it finally passes into Lishma itself; study only pays off when a person brings a precise, felt lack into it.

### Opening

After a brief personal reflection carried over from the prior session, the group reads and works through Rabash's article on mourning for Jerusalem — tracing how the will to receive develops in coarseness across "roots" one through four, and how a screen, correction, and the middle line get built on top of it (the article-reading portion, not summarized here, per scope). The Q&A that follows takes up one connected question from several angles: is the desire to bestow that a person acquires genuinely something new, created by the person himself — or is it borrowed, already the Creator's? And since the Creator's actual qualities are hidden from us, how does a person even come to recognize them and start working with them at all? The session closes with the group sharing impressions and naming what they're taking into their Ten.

## Part 1 — Two Kinds of "Existence from Absence"

### The Question

A participant asked: once Bina became mixed with Malchut, is what results still rightly called a created being that exists "from absence" (Yesh MiAyin)?

### The Will to Receive Is — the Desire to Bestow Isn't

Rav Laitman's answer split the person in two, and gave each half a different origin:

> "Since the Creator has no desire to receive, only the desire to bestow, and we have in our foundation the desire to receive — that's us. The desire to bestow which we acquire is not the desire to bestow from the Creator. I can't cut this quality from the Creator and join it to me — there is no such thing as that in nature. But when the Creator bestows to me, with His bestowal upon me, just as a Great One bestows upon a small one, He changes me to such an extent with His bestowal that my desire to receive becomes similar to Him."

He was precise that this isn't a transformation of substance: "It's not that it becomes a desire to bestow, but rather that He makes His external actions as if it's a desire to bestow — and you can't distinguish between them, rather only if we look at who He is from within." From within, only two options exist: "either the Creator, which is the desire to bestow — within and without, the same form, same quality — or a created being, who has the quality of the desire to receive naturally within him, and on the outside has inverted and taken upon itself to form externally as the desire to bestow."

### Only the Will to Receive Is Truly "From Absence"

This is what answered the original question directly:

> "The desire to bestow that a person acquires upon his desire to receive is not existence from absence — it's existence from existence. He received it from the Creator. He learned from the Creator how to behave with his desire, to see it like a desire to bestow... But with that, the desire to receive remains existence from absence, and will never disappear."

Becoming similar to the Creator, he added, is earned "not by the actions" alone but by an inner attainment: "we have also come to the inner understanding of who the Creator is, to the inner attainment of who the Creator is — meaning, to us, the true stature, standing, like the Creator."

## Part 2 — Spirituality Is Above Time

The same answer then turned to a second, related paradox: how can something be "created" — pass from not-existing to existing — at all, if spirituality has no time in it?

> "We can't talk about it without the framework of time — that the desire to receive at one point wasn't, and then the Creator created it, and then corrupted it, and then corrected it, and then it reaches the final correction... We look at it as something that is going by and changing forms, and once it was, and once it wasn't. And in truth, spirituality is above time... There is no time. We can't grasp this today."

Pressed on how something can be "existence from absence" — once it wasn't, then it was — without a timeline to place the "wasn't" on, he held the paradox open rather than resolving it:

> "How could it be, if not according to the axis of time, that it wasn't, then it is? Rather, it exists eternally, both this and that, together. We can't, with our minds — which are made of one quality, the quality of Malchut, and also corrupted — grasp this. This thing comes in accordance with how we correct our vessels; then from our vessels we will understand that these spiritual concepts exist above time."

He tied this to a felt, not just intellectual, outcome: "By a person correcting his vessel, he is rewarded with the feeling that there is no time, that there is no life or death — that there is a certain eternal flow, until one reaches a state where there is no flow altogether, that everything is simply one and eternal... According to how a person reaches being similar to the Creator, he stops thinking about himself or feeling that he is existing or not. He rises to eternity, and all these questions disappear."

## Part 3 — Learning by Contrast: How the Creator's Qualities Reach Us

A second question opened, referencing Rabash's article directly: if a person takes on qualities from the Creator and works with them, what does that actually mean — how does one even begin?

> "The qualities of the Creator come to us by the reforming light. We ourselves, who are in shattered qualities, both Malchut and Bina, cannot understand from this how to work with an opposite form that we have in us. Oppositeness is also not attained unless by presenting the opposite. By me seeing the black, it won't come to be some attainment in white, a desire for it to be white — I need to see the difference between them, to recognize the difference, and then to compare them within my vessels."

From that comparison — "this emotional comparison, this intellectual comparison" — a person advances toward one of the two forms, black or white. And that comparison is itself only possible because the light has begun to work on a person from outside pleasure altogether:

> "Even though we are in the shattering, and we have vessels of Malchut and vessels of Bina in the manner of in order to receive — we can't understand from this what the form of bestowal is. It's simply not given to us. We sometimes feel the extent to which we don't understand what in order to bestow is, and that's already a great revelation given to us — the light beginning to illuminate from above as its own quality. Not something that draws us through pleasures, showing us there's a pleasure for us, but rather it shows us, outside of the pleasure, what He is for us, and who He is. Meaning, the Creator is showing us His characteristics."

## Part 4 — Mourning Jerusalem: What the Regret Is Actually For

The discussion then turned back to the article's own title, and Rav Laitman made the connection explicit — the "mourning" the article names is not sentiment, it is a precise, two-part work:

> "The beginning of the correction is called everyone who mourns Jerusalem. Meaning, when a person understands what is his mourning — what exactly he's mourning, what discernment is it that's called Jerusalem and the ruin of Jerusalem. What is actually that spiritual concept we have to mourn? Meaning, this is what I want to exist in me correctly."

He was specific about what that regret does and where it goes:

> "He's rewarded with this vessel being corrected according to the extent of the sorrow in him, because the extent of his sorrow is already directed against his ego, against the will to receive in him, against all the qualities that are called the actual ruined Jerusalem. And when he regrets it, that sorrow, we call it raising MAN. So he's rewarded with the light correcting his MAN from above, and he will have the correction of the screen on the vessels that regret that they are ruined — that they are in the desire in order to receive. We need to regret two things: to regret being in broken vessels, meaning that we think and yearn to satisfy only ourselves — one. And two, that we need to increase in us the greatness of the Creator, the greatness of the quality of bestowal, that should bring us yearning for the built Jerusalem. So it's not enough to regret the bad situation — we also have to yearn for the good state."

He named the two measures this yearning is tracked by: "records of coarseness and records of clothing — a record of the vessel and a record of the light. The record of the vessel is what state the vessel is in compared to where it should be, and the record of the light is the extent to which my relation to the Creator should be — that the Creator is the ideal, the most perfect, complete."

## Part 5 — The Quality of Bestowal Is Itself the Pleasure

Asked what the quality of bestowal actually feels like from the inside, Rav Laitman located the pleasure inside the giving itself, not as a separate reward for it:

> "The feeling of the quality of bestowal is bestowal — nothing more. You don't need anything else. If I bestow, this is my pleasure. Of course, in order to be on the degree of the giver, I have to know to whom I am bestowing, how he enjoys my bestowal. I have to incorporate within me his vessels, his deficiencies — I have to unite with the Creator and acquire from Him all His vessels, all the pleasures He derives from filling me. It's a baby that reaches the degree of the mother, and feels all the things that are in the mother toward him, both in vessels and in pleasures — the way she feels toward him when he receives everything from her, and then enjoys her one hundred percent."

This, he explained, is why bestowal — unlike reception — has no natural ceiling:

> "The form of bestowal obligates a person to reach huge vessels, and the form of reception isn't. Because in reception, the minute he receives, the vessels are blocked — they don't need anymore. I'm happy, I'm content, and I begin to be afraid it will disappear. So it's actually the quality of bestowal that obligates a person to grow indefinitely, without any boundary, constantly, to constantly be in an ascent."

He illustrated how greatness itself becomes the source of that growth with an everyday analogy:

> "You were taken to serve in a palace as a cleaner, and you were given some tiny salary, so you would sustain yourself. And the rest of the pleasure is the more you recognize where you are, what important the place is where you are. There are people who say they are next to the government — they don't get a salary, they get the feeling of the importance of what they belong to. So you can compare that to what happens to us with pleasures above this world. All those pleasures come through the greatness of the Creator in our eyes. You can be next to the king, and these hundred dollars, you don't even count them — his importance fills you."

## Part 6 — Measuring the Light Only Through the Vessel

Pressed on how a person could ever perceive the Creator's *intentions*, as opposed to His visible actions, Rav Laitman drew a hard line: intention cannot be seen directly, only inferred through the vessel that receives it.

> "How do I measure the light as great or small? Because the intention is the light — I can measure it only according to the vessels. I have no other way. And the more the vessels can join the intention to bestow, the more the left line can incorporate in the right line. I measure everything according to the vessels. The light, I don't even know what it is. I have some phenomenon in the vessels which I call light. That's it."

Asked how, then, a person could ever come to know someone else's intention at all, he returned to the mother-and-baby image, this time to explain understanding itself:

> "How can I value the mother who gives and gives and gives, if I only receive and receive and receive, and I have no quality of bestowal in return? But when I myself begin to realize what it means to give — because the same desires awaken in me like the mother's — I begin to remember what the mother did for me, and I recognize how much she loves me. I can know someone's intentions only because I will have the same intentions."

He extended this to explain why the Creator's actions can feel painful while His intention is still trusted as good — using a vivid personal story:

> "In the Creator, there are no intentions and actions separately — but toward us, His action can seem bad, and we realize that His intentions are revealed to us as good because we attain them through our vessels. The act can be reception, but the intention is bestowal... It's like a doctor. I had an operation — I remember, before it, he sat on my chest, a big, strong man, and said, 'Now I'll give you a bad feeling.' He drilled a hole in me without any anesthesia, because I was in a state where I couldn't wait. I lost consciousness and regained it, but I remember I had no hatred toward him. He hurts you, he gives you pain that makes you want to die just to get away from it — but you don't feel hatred toward it. To me, this is a very good discernment: the intention completely controls the action."

## Part 7 — Closing: Sharing Impressions and Carrying the Lesson Into the Ten

Once the Q&A closed, Rav Laitman opened the floor for the group to share impressions and name what they were taking into their Ten. Several friends spoke to the same throughline — the transition from will to receive to will to bestow, and the reforming light that makes it possible:

> "Especially, you know, Rav was talking about how the transition from will to receive to will to bestow happens — that was pretty amazing, the level of detail he went into... To draw the reforming light together — that is the work. That was a common thread."

One participant, joining from Egypt near dawn, tied the lesson back to its own theme of pleasure found specifically in bestowal:

> "One of the things I admire a lot is Rav talks about how there is a pleasure in the giving. Giving is a quality of the Creator, and if we are to achieve equivalence of form, we are trying to become like the Creator — we have to mimic his good qualities, so that quality of restoring for sure absolutely brings pleasure. He also talked about the importance of intention — that you have to have the right intention, and pray for doing things with the right intention... I'm happy every time I join you."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. A person's desire to receive is the one thing genuinely "existence from absence" — brought into being with no root in the Creator, who has none. The desire to bestow a person subsequently acquires is not: it is "existence from existence," learned from and given by the Creator Himself.

2. Becoming similar to the Creator is not a change of internal substance but of external form — the will to receive itself never disappears; what changes is that a person's outward actions come to resemble bestowal, matched by an inner attainment of who the Creator actually is.

3. Spirituality has no time in it: framing correction as a sequence ("it wasn't, then it was, then it will finally be corrected") is only how it looks to us; the truer picture is that it "exists eternally, both this and that, together," graspable only to the extent a person's own vessels are corrected.

4. The Creator's qualities reach a person only through the reforming light, and only by contrast — a person cannot recognize bestowal directly, only by first feeling the gap between his own shattered qualities and something outside pleasure altogether, the way black is only recognized against white.

5. "Mourning for Jerusalem" is named here as a precise, two-part work, not a mood: regretting being in broken, self-serving vessels, and separately yearning for the "built Jerusalem" — the greatness of the Creator's quality of bestowal. That regret itself, correctly aimed, is what the lesson calls "raising MAN."

6. The quality of bestowal carries its own pleasure and has no natural ceiling, unlike reception, which is satisfied and closes the moment it is filled — this is why bestowal alone can obligate a person to grow without end.

7. A person cannot perceive the Creator's intention directly, only through the vessel that receives it — and can only come to recognize someone else's intention by first developing the same intention in himself, whether toward the Creator or, as the mother analogy and the doctor story both show, toward another person.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Existence from Absence (Yesh MiAyin) | The will to receive's unique mode of creation — brought into being with no root or parallel in the Creator, distinguishing it from anything derived by ordinary cause and effect. |
| Existence from Existence (Yesh MiYesh) | The mode by which the acquired desire to bestow comes to a person — not created from nothing, but received and learned directly from the Creator, who already has it. |
| Bina | The sefira of "understanding," the quality of bestowal; mixed here with Malchut in the created being, and the source a person draws on when correcting the will to receive toward giving. |
| Malchut | The receiving vessel, "Kingdom"; described here as the very quality our minds are made of and therefore corrupted, which is why we cannot grasp spiritual concepts that exist above time. |
| Reforming Light | The light through which the Creator's qualities reach a person; the means by which someone in shattered qualities can begin to work with a form opposite his own. |
| MAN (Mayin Nukvin) | "Female waters" — a person's request or prayer; correctly aimed regret over one's broken vessels is described here as "raising MAN," drawing the light that corrects them. |
| Reshimot (Records) | Spiritual impressions carried forward from the shattering; here specified as two kinds tracked together — the record of the vessel (its current state of coarseness) and the record of the light (the ideal, the Creator's own perfection, against which the vessel is measured). |
| Above Time | The description of the spiritual world as a single, eternal state without sequence — contrasted with how correction necessarily appears to us, as something happening along an axis of past and future. |

### Opening

Baal HaSulam wrote relatively little about relationships between friends — most of his writing addresses sublime, abstract subjects like the Ten Sefirot or the Zohar. "Arvut" is one of the rare exceptions, and it is treated as one of his most important articles because it is so practical. The central question the lesson poses: if "Matan Torah" gives us the theory — that through loving one's friends a person can adhere to the Creator through equivalence of form — what does that theory actually look like in practice? What, concretely, does mutual guarantee require of a person, and why did the Torah require unanimous agreement to it before it could even be given?

---

## Part 1 — From Theory to Practice

"Arvut" picks up directly where "Matan Torah" leaves off — Matan Torah ends at item 17, and Arvut begins at item 18. Baal HaSulam himself characterized the relationship this way: Matan Torah is the theory (love your friend as yourself, as the means to adhere to the Creator), and Arvut is the actual practice — applied to the individual, to the group, and ultimately to the whole of humanity.

The lesson recalls that the group first studied this article around 2002–2003, and that in 2003 a world convention was organized around the theme of Arvut for the first time — participants were even required to study the article and pass a test on its content before they were allowed to attend.

> "It requires that the person take responsibility, not only over his own spirituality... here, all of a sudden, you find out that there's a condition, and that Arvut is a real law."

---

## Part 2 — Mutual Guarantee as a Law of Nature

A key move in the lesson is to show that mutual guarantee is not an abstract spiritual ideal divorced from how the world actually works — it is a law that already operates at every level of nature:

- **Gravitation** — the Earth, sun, and moon hold each other in balance.
- **The forest** — a strong tree supports a weaker one.
- **The human body** — organs cooperate automatically: the heart supplies blood, the liver and kidneys clean the body, with no need to "ask favors," because this is simply how the body was built.

The one level that does *not* automatically keep this law is **Adam** — not the body, but the level of human desires and thoughts. A person feels no natural responsibility for anyone else, and does not feel that harming another harms himself, because the underlying connection between all people is hidden.

This concealment is deliberate. Nature keeps Arvut without being asked; a person must instead discover it and choose it — "not out of fear... but out of our own desire, and that's called the degree of man."

## Part 3 — "All of Israel Are Responsible for One Another"

Baal HaSulam opens the article with a phrase taken from Sanhedrin 27: *all of Israel are responsible for one another.* The lesson pauses to clarify a term that recurs constantly through the article:

**"Israel" does not mean a nationality.** It is built from *Yashar-El* — "straight to God" — and refers to anyone, regardless of birth or nation, who yearns to draw close to the Creator. The article is addressed to "any person in whom the point in the heart has awakened," not to a specific people in the modern, national sense.

Baal HaSulam describes a condition that had to be met before the Torah — the reforming light — could be received: each person had to be asked, individually, whether he agreed to take upon himself the obligation to care for every other member of the nation (the group) and to satisfy their needs **no less than the measure he uses to care for his own needs.** Only once the whole group unanimously agreed — saying "we will do and we will hear" — and mutual guarantee was actually formed between them, did they become worthy of receiving the Torah. Not before.

---

## Part 4 — What Kind of "Need" Are We Responsible For?

The lesson is careful to define what this responsibility means concretely, because it can easily be misread as a demand for unlimited material care:

- We live in societies that already provide for most material and basic needs — that is not what a person is obligated to supply.
- The actual obligation is to make sure that **the goal — discovering the Creator — becomes important to the other person**, and to actively help advance him toward it: investing in him, making the effort, building the right environment, thinking of him, praying for him.
- This law operates specifically within "the ten," the group bound together by a shared spiritual goal — not with family, coworkers, or humanity generally. Those relationships continue as they were; nothing here instructs a person to change how he treats people outside the group.

> "If from morning till evening, I make sure that a friend from my nation... that the goal will be important to him, and I advance him toward that goal... that means that I love him."

## Part 5 — A Question Left Open: Where Is the Choice?

Toward the end of the session, a participant (Kabir) raises a difficult question: if the very desire to draw close to the Creator is itself placed inside a person by the Creator — and not everyone receives that desire, even within the same family — then in what sense is a person actually "agreeing" to anything? Where is the responsibility, if the desire that produces the agreement was never the person's own to begin with?

The recording ends at this question, before the lesson's answer is given.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Aravut is the practice; Matan Torah is the theory.** Loving one's friend as oneself, introduced as an abstract goal in Matan Torah, becomes concrete and binding through Aravut.
2. **Mutual guarantee already governs all of nature** — gravity, forests, the human body — and operates automatically everywhere except at the level of human desire and thought, where it must be freely discovered and chosen.
3. **The condition for receiving the Torah was agreement, not ability.** Each person had to consent to take full responsibility for the group's needs before the group as a whole became worthy of the light.
4. **"Israel" means anyone who yearns toward the Creator,** not a nationality — the article speaks to every person in whom the point in the heart has awakened.
5. **The responsibility described is for a friend's spiritual advancement specifically,** not for material or social needs already met outside the group.
6. **"We will do and we will hear" reverses the ordinary order** — committing to act before fully understanding what that action will require.
7. **Concealment preserves free choice.** If the connection between people were obvious the way gravity is obvious, there would be nothing left to choose — the very hiddenness of Aravut is what allows a person to adopt it as a real, independent choice.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Aravut (Mutual Guarantee)** | The law that each person is responsible for every other person's needs, no less than for his own. Baal HaSulam presents it as the practical fulfillment of "love your friend as yourself." |
| **Matan Torah (The Giving of the Torah)** | The article immediately preceding Aravut; establishes the theoretical goal — adhesion to the Creator through equivalence of form, reached via loving one's friend. |
| **Israel (Yashar-El)** | "Straight to God." In this article, anyone who yearns to draw close to the Creator — not a nationality or place of birth. |
| **Adam (the Human Level)** | The level of desires and thoughts, as distinct from the body. The only level of nature that does not automatically keep the law of Aravut, because the connection between people is concealed here. |
| **We Will Do and We Will Hear** | The group's unanimous commitment to act before fully comprehending — the condition that preceded receiving the Torah. |
| **The Torah / The Reforming Light** | The light the Creator wishes to give, received only once mutual guarantee has actually been established among the group. |
| **The Ten (the Group)** | The immediate circle of people sharing a spiritual goal. The lesson specifies that the obligations of Aravut apply within this circle, not to humanity, family, or coworkers generally. |
| **Baal HaSulam** | Rabbi Yehuda Leib Ashlag; author of "Aravut" and "Matan Torah," among many other foundational works on the wisdom of Kabbalah. |

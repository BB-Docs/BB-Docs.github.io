### Opening

This session continues the group's ongoing study of how the upper worlds are built — from the first restriction, through the screen and reflected light, to the descent of the Partzufim and worlds. Having established *how* the worlds cascade downward, the lesson turns to *why* a person only ever receives "a thin line" of the light meant for him, and what happens to the rest of it. The central question: what is the **Ohr Makif** (surrounding light) — the light that does not enter the vessel — and how does something that stays "outside" still pull a person from one degree to the next?

---

## Part 1 — Review: How the Vessel Is Built

### From Malchut of Ein Sof to the Thin Line

Before the new material, Rav reviewed the structure already covered: Malchut of Ein Sof is filled completely with light. The desire, wanting to be like the Giver, undergoes the first restriction (**Tzimtzum**) — the natural desire to receive is restricted down to **Behina Dalet**. From this point, reception can no longer happen directly; a new vessel is needed.

This new vessel works with the **Masach** (screen) and **Ohr Chozer** (reflected light): pleasure can now only be received in the measure that there is adhesion with the Above. The screen carries the quality of **Ovyot** (coarseness, hardness), which comes in five degrees — 0, 1, 2, 3, 4. The striking of the direct light on the screen produces the Ten Sefirot of the Toch ("inner part received") and the Ten Sefirot of the Sof ("the part that cannot be received") — together forming a **Partzuf**.

### The Five Partzufim and the World of Adam Kadmon

- **Galgalta** (first Partzuf) is followed by the smaller **AB**, then **SAG**, **MA**, and **BON** — the five Partzufim, corresponding to the five degrees of Ovyot (0–4).
- All five together are called the world of **Adam Kadmon**.
- After Adam Kadmon, the worlds of **Atzilut, Beriah, Yetzirah,** and **Assiya** emerge — each smaller (i.e., with greater Ovyot) than the one before it.

**Why does each world descend and shrink?** Once Malchut begins receiving in order to bestow, what is actually revealed to her in that reception is not the light of Nefesh itself but the far greater pleasure of **adhesion** with the Giver — described as roughly 620 times greater. Malchut cannot take this in all at once; she can only receive a very small part of it. This is why the whole structure built this way is called **a thin line** of reception in order to bestow. Each time she examines how incapable she is of receiving the full pleasure of adhesion, this incapacity is precisely what causes the next descent — the next, smaller Partzuf and world.

> As Rav put it: the outburst of warmth and love born between the created being and the Creator — "from your actions do we know you" — happens as the creature begins to act like the Creator. Each such action opens another gate onto how sublime and great the Creator truly is.

## Part 2 — What Is Ohr Makif? The Definition from Baal HaSulam

### The Passage (Preface to the Wisdom of Kabbalah, Item 32)

The first excerpt read explains that when Ein Sof "wished to dress" in Behina Dalet (as it did before the restriction), the screen in the Rosh (head) of Malchut struck and repelled it — detaining it from spreading further. Because **there is no change or absence in the spiritual**, that repelled light does not simply vanish. Since the light of Ein Sof was already drawn toward clothing Behina Dalet, it must still, in some way, be sustained. It becomes the **surrounding light** — circling and shining on the vessel from the outside, "with a certain illumination," until the vessel is corrected enough to receive it directly.

### The Two-Part Definition (Ohr Makif)

A second excerpt gives Baal HaSulam's formal definition:

> "Surrounding light is the light that is intended to clothe the degree, but is detained because of some boundary in it."

This has two meanings:
1. **Distant illumination** — light that does not yet fill the vessel because the vessel lacks the capacity, but the light is nonetheless present and real.
2. **Certain illumination** — an illumination that is certain to eventually clothe the vessel, "since the light surrounds it from all sides without giving it room to escape, until it is fit to receive it in full."

### Ohr Pnimi vs. Ohr Makif

| | What it is | Proportion (example) | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ohr Pnimi** (inner light) | The portion of the direct light (Ohr Yashar) that the Partzuf actually receives | e.g., 20% | Fills the Toch of the Partzuf |
| **Ohr Makif** (surrounding light) | The portion the Partzuf cannot yet receive, so it remains around the Partzuf | e.g., 80% | Surrounds the Sof of the Partzuf; continually presses to enter |

**Ohr Chozer** (reflected light) is different from both — it is the light that clothes the intention itself, arising from the meeting between the direct light and the screen: an initial, complete rejection, followed by the created being's equalization with the Giver ("I don't want it for myself").

## Part 3 — How the Worlds Are Born from the Calculation

### The Zivug at the Rosh, and the Birth of the Next Partzuf

At the Rosh (head) of each Partzuf, a calculation is made: how much can be received, and how much cannot. What can be received becomes the Ohr Pnimi of that Partzuf; what cannot is left as Ohr Makif shining opposite the vessels of the Sof. The Malchut then takes the portion of vessels it found it *can* work with and, from these, gives birth to the next Partzuf — which goes through the same calculation, gives birth to the next, and so on.

This cascading process is how the world of **AK** (Adam Kadmon) is born, followed by **Atzilut, Beriah, Yetzirah,** and **Assiya** — the whole structure shining down so that, ultimately, a person is drawn to want to climb these worlds from below.

### The Host-and-Guest Example

Rav returned to the image of a guest calculating how much he can take from the host's table with the intention to bestow:

- First calculation: can I receive this and still be thinking of the host, expressing love to him?
- As the guest takes more, a **giant pleasure** of equivalence of form starts to appear — the pleasure of being like the Giver.
- This triggers a second calculation: is this new pleasure being enjoyed for my own sake (which disconnects the person, throwing him into the opposite of the light), or can it too be received in order to bestow?

Because this second pleasure is so much greater, the created being can only take a small amount of it "in a thin line" — hence the ongoing restriction and recalculation at every stage, building what Rav described as a boundary that keeps a person from slipping back toward pure self-interest.

## Part 4 — Q&A: What Pulls Us From Below

### Is Ohr Makif Only in the Point in the Heart?

A participant asked whether Ohr Makif exists only in the "point in the heart," or in all of creation. Rav's answer: the whole structure — the cascading of the worlds from Adam Kadmon down through Atzilut, Beriah, Yetzirah, and Assiya — is itself built on the thin line, and is the very reason for the many concealments (the worlds themselves), so that the created being can exist as a separate recipient with something to bestow toward the Creator.

**From above down**, the whole system is a thin line: only a small amount of the endless light comes through as the worlds descend. **From below up**, the relationship reverses: as a person climbs from this world back up through Assiya, Yetzirah, Beriah, and Atzilut toward Galgalta, the entire volume of Malchut of Ein Sof is meant to be filled — with each person filling their own unique place.

**What pulls a person up from one rung to the next?** The Ohr Makif itself. At each Zivug at the Rosh, a person receives both the ability to receive and an understanding of how much he still cannot receive — and that felt gap is what invites him to the next degree.

### Reforming Light vs. Surrounding Light vs. Reflected Light

Asked to distinguish these, Rav explained: to develop Ohr Chozer (the intention to bestow, going against one's own nature of pure reception), a person first needs an outside force to enter and change him — this is the **reforming light** (Ohr Makif drawn through Torah study and the work in a group of friends). Once that force builds the Masach and Ohr Chozer in a person, he becomes a spiritual Partzuf with its own Ohr Chozer, and moves to a different level of work — no longer working in total concealment, but examining the whole of the Creator's desire to benefit creation and asking how much of it can be returned.

### What Does It Mean to Increase Corporeal Pleasure Instead of Connection?

Asked what it means when a person notices growth in corporeal (bodily) pleasure rather than a feeling of connection to the Creator, Rav answered plainly: this is a sign that the person needs to increase connection within the group (the "ten"), not that spiritual pleasures are literally growing. There is no point chasing corporeal pleasures — the real task is to increase, as much as possible, the felt importance of the Creator.

### Setting Boundaries in the Group

Asked for a practical example of "setting boundaries in the ten," Rav pointed to the principle "distance yourself from evil and do good": don't be the one who decides and forces the group toward your own idea of what's good; instead, be the one who gives, who elevates the importance of the shared goal among the friends.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Ohr Makif is light that is "there" but not yet received.** It is not absent — it surrounds the vessel from the outside, certain to eventually clothe it once the vessel is fit to receive it.
2. **Everything is received "in a thin line."** Because the pleasure of adhesion with the Creator is roughly 620 times greater than the light of Nefesh alone, a vessel can only take in a very small part of it at each stage — this incapacity itself drives the descent of the Partzufim and worlds.
3. **The Rosh calculation is what generates the next world.** What can be received becomes Ohr Pnimi; what can't becomes Ohr Makif; and the portion that can be worked with gives birth to the next Partzuf, cascading all the way down to Assiya.
4. **From above down is concealment; from below up is filling.** The descending structure hides the light behind successive worlds, but the ascent from below is what fills Malchut of Ein Sof, each person filling their unique place in it.
5. **Ohr Makif is what pulls a person upward.** The felt gap between what one can receive and what remains outside is precisely what invites the next degree of growth — without it, a person would settle for fleeting, worldly attainment.
6. **The reforming light is Ohr Makif at work through Torah and the group.** It is the outside force that builds the Masach and Ohr Chozer in a person, moving him from total concealment toward a more active, examining relationship with the light.
7. **Corporeal pleasure is a diagnostic, not a goal.** Its increase signals a need for more connection in the group — not a competing spiritual path worth pursuing on its own.

| **Ovyot** | Coarseness/thickness of desire; comes in five degrees (0–4), corresponding to the five Partzufim of Adam Kadmon. |
| **Behina Dalet** | The fourth phase of desire, upon which the first restriction (Tzimtzum) is made. |
| **Tzimtzum (Restriction)** | Malchut's restriction of her natural desire to receive, made so that reception could instead happen in order to bestow. |

| **Galgalta, AB, SAG, MA, BON** | The five Partzufim making up the world of Adam Kadmon, corresponding to the five degrees of Ovyot. |

| **Atzilut, Beriah, Yetzirah, Assiya** | The four worlds that emerge after Adam Kadmon, each with progressively greater Ovyot (further from the Creator). |

| **Zivug (Coupling)** | The striking of light on the screen at the Rosh of a Partzuf, from which the calculation of what can/cannot be received emerges. |

### Opening

The lesson opens with a question from the Zohar: "there is no blessing of above on something that is counted." But the Torah has the Creator command that Israel be counted — through a census taken by collecting a half-shekel ransom rather than a direct head count — and Israel were blessed both before and after the count, so no death occurred among them. Rashi adds that the Creator counts Israel "every hour, out of his fondness for them." This raises the article's real question: what is a blessing in the Work, what is a count in the Work, and why does counting without a blessing bring death?

---

## Part 1 — Two Matters in the Work: Purpose and Correction

### The Purpose of Creation

The Creator's aim is to do good to His creations — "his desire to do good to his creation." For this purpose He created in the creatures a desire to receive delight and pleasure, certain that, having created such a desire, they would want to receive the pleasure He intends to give.

### The Correction of Creation

But receiving without giving back creates **disparity of form** between the creature and the Creator, and in spirituality disparity of form causes **separation** — cutting a person off from "the life of lives." As the Zohar states, **"the wicked in their lives are called dead."** Even though, as the Zohar also notes, "all the grace that they do, they do for themselves" — meaning their good deeds are aimed at their own glory rather than the Creator — they remain cut off, because they have nothing in them in the form of bestowal.

The correction of creation, therefore, is for the creature to walk the Creator's path: **equivalence of form**. As the Creator gives, the creature must learn to give in return. This obtaining happens gradually, since the lower one cannot simply overpower the will to receive that the Creator built into him.

### Two Kinds of Vessels

This produces two kinds of vessels in a person's work: **vessels of reception**, where a corrective intention must be placed on top of the act of receiving, and **vessels of bestowal**, where the act of giving is already aimed outward and only the sincerity of the intention needs correcting.

---

## Part 2 — Correcting the Two Vessels

### Vessels of Reception: Receiving in Order to Bestow

On the will to receive, a **judgment** was placed — a prohibition on using it as it is, because using it for oneself creates disparity of form. The correction is to place the aim **"in order to bestow"** on top of the will to receive: a person actually receives, but his intention is to give. The act and the aim are now opposite one another.

Before this correction, anything spiritual a person touched tasted **bitter** — holiness felt remote and inaccessible to the will to receive, because of the restrictions placed on it. Once the aim to bestow is placed over the desire, the same holiness is felt as **sweet**. This corrected light — reception with an aim to bestow — is called the light of **Chokhmah**, and the correction itself is called **mitigation of the judgment**: what was dark (coarseness, where the light could not shine) becomes a place where the light shines in the vessel.

### Vessels of Bestowal: Bestowing in Order to Bestow

Separately, a person also has vessels of bestowal — actions that genuinely benefit another, like a refined person who tries to make others happy. Here there is no judgment on the act itself; there is "no deficiency" in giving. But the *intention* still needs correction, since a person can do good for others while really aiming at his own respect — again, "all the good that they do, they do for themselves." When the act and the aim are both truly outward, this is called **bestowing in order to bestow**, and the light received through it is the light of **Chassidim**.

The article stresses that this correction belongs to the heart: a person is commanded to love the Creator "with all your heart and with all your soul," and, quoting Rabbi Abraham ibn Ezra, "all the Mitzvot that are written in the Torah... are all in order to correct the heart, for the Lord searches all hearts." Whether the act is reception or bestowal, both need the same correction of aim.

| | Vessels of Reception | Vessels of Bestowal |
|---|---|---|
| What needs correcting | The act itself (a judgment was placed on the vessel) | Only the intention behind an already-outward act |
| Corrected state | Receiving in order to bestow | Bestowing in order to bestow |
| Light received | Chokhmah | Chassidim |

## Part 3 — The Gift That Must Be Counted

### Seeing What You've Received

The purpose of creation requires that a person actually *see* and *feel* what he has received — otherwise the good intended for him does nothing. The article illustrates this with two brothers: one rich, in America, the other poor, in Israel. The rich brother deposits a million dollars in the bank under the poor brother's name, but never tells him, and the bank doesn't either — so the poor brother stays poor, simply because he doesn't know. In the same way, if the creatures do not know and do not feel the delight and pleasure prepared for them, the purpose of creation accomplishes nothing.

This is why the light of the purpose of creation is called **Chokhmah** — wisdom and seeing, "the light of the Panim," as in "a man's wisdom illuminates his face." In the Work, receiving this light is called **"something is counted"** — it is received specifically in the vessels of reception, where it can be seen and measured.

### Why a Gift Must Be Known

The article connects this to the ordinary logic of a gift: when a person gives his friend a gift, he wants the friend to notice and value it, because the value of the gift is how he shows his love. If the receiver doesn't look closely enough to see the gift's importance, he "blemishes the measure of the love" the giver meant to express — and undermines the very point of the gift, which the sages describe as a way to **"buy yourself a friend."** A gift that goes unnoticed cannot do this work.

Applied to the Creator: if the creatures cannot count and measure what they receive, the Creator's purpose — to do good, and to have that good actually attained — is not reached. Hence the necessity that something in the purpose of creation be countable, seen, known.

## Part 4 — The Charity That Must Stay Hidden

### Charity in Concealment

Vessels of bestowal work by the opposite logic. The light received through them is called **covered Chassidim** — covered because it stays hidden. With true charity, the giver tries to ensure the receiver never learns who gave, and the receiver is happier not knowing — which is why charity collections for someone in need are often made "anonymously," so the receiver isn't ashamed. Chesed (mercy/giving) is spoken of from the giver's side: he acts **above reason**, giving without knowing where it lands, trusting that it reaches its purpose. This is called **charity in concealment**.

### Blessing as the Purest Form of Giving

A **blessing** is the clearest expression of this logic. To bless someone is to give what cannot actually be given in fact — to give with the heart what is shown only in words. A blessing means wanting one's friend to have *more* than one is actually able to give him. Applied to the Creator: a person engaged in bestowal says, in effect, *"more than the good deeds that I can give you, I bless you that I will be able to give you more than the good deeds."* One should always bless the Creator in this sense — wishing to give Him more contentment than one is actually giving. Because a blessing is pure bestowal with nothing yet handed over in fact, there is no question of counting it at all.

| | Gift | Charity / Blessing |
|---|---|---|
| Associated light | Chokhmah (seeing) | Chassidim (covered, mercy) |
| Corresponds to | Purpose of creation | Correction of creation |
| Must be... | Counted, seen, known | Concealed — unknown to giver and/or receiver |
| Why | The measure of the gift shows the measure of love | Concealment keeps the giving purely outward, free of self-interest |

## Part 5 — Resolving the Opening Question

### "Out of His Fondness, He Counts Them Every Hour"

This resolves Rashi's statement about why the Creator counts Israel. The question was whether the Creator needs Israel to report their own number before He can know it — which cannot be the case. Rashi's "fondness" means something else: the Creator sees that Israel have already achieved the **correction of creation** — they are working in bestowal — and so He wants to give them the **purpose of creation** as well: the light of Chokhmah, the light of seeing, which by its nature must be counted.

### The Danger Inside Counting

But this is exactly where the danger lies. The light of Chokhmah is received in the vessels of reception, and "the act might govern the aim" — meaning the very act of receiving pleasure can overpower a person's intention and pull it back toward reception for himself, rather than reception in order to bestow. Reception for oneself recreates disparity of form, which causes separation from the life of lives — which is death, exactly as stated earlier: "the wicked in their lives are called dead."

This is the answer to the opening question of "why does death increase because of the counting": counting draws the light of Chokhmah into the vessels of reception, and without an accompanying blessing — without chassidim added to hold the intention in place — the act of receiving can quietly take over the aim. As the article puts it, "when the blessing is gone, the other side is on him."

### The Middle Line

The fix is structural: Chokhmah shines in the vessels of reception, called the **left** line, and needs correction so that a person is not simply carried along by the act of reception. The light of **Chassidim**, working through the vessels of bestowal, must be drawn in alongside it — the **right** line — so that counting (seeing what one has received) stays anchored to bestowal rather than collapsing into reception for oneself. Together, these two lines form the **middle line** the Work aims at.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **There are two matters in the Work: the purpose of creation (to receive delight, seen and counted) and the correction of creation (to bestow, ideally concealed).** The article's whole argument runs on this distinction.
2. **Disparity of form causes separation — spiritual death — even for someone who appears to be doing good,** if the underlying aim is self-serving ("all the good that they do, they do for themselves").
3. **Vessels of reception need a correction placed on the act itself; vessels of bestowal need correction only in the intention,** since the act of giving is already outward-facing.
4. **A gift must be counted to fulfill its purpose** — an unnoticed gift fails to convey the giver's love, just as an unfelt purpose of creation accomplishes nothing.
5. **Charity (and blessing) must remain concealed to fulfill its purpose** — true bestowal works precisely by neither side counting or knowing, which is why a blessing carries no issue of counting at all.
6. **Counting becomes dangerous specifically in the vessels of reception**, where the pleasure of the act itself can override the intention to bestow and pull a person back into reception for himself.
7. **The Creator counting Israel "out of fondness" is the purpose of creation being added on top of an already-achieved correction of creation** — and the remedy for the risk this creates is the middle line: chassidim drawn alongside chochmah so reception stays anchored to bestowal.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Blessing (Bracha)** | Pure bestowal expressed through the heart/words rather than an actual handed-over thing; wanting to give more than one actually can. Carries no issue of counting. |
| **Counting (Mispar)** | Receiving and measuring what one has been given. Necessary for the purpose of creation to be felt, but risky in the vessels of reception. |
| **Purpose of Creation** | The Creator's aim to do good to His creations — to give delight and pleasure that must ultimately be seen and felt (counted) to be fulfilled. |
| **Correction of Creation** | The creature's work of returning to equivalence of form with the Creator by learning to give (bestow) rather than only receive. |
| **Disparity of Form / Equivalence of Form** | Disparity: receiving without giving back, which causes separation. Equivalence: giving as the Creator gives — the goal of the correction. |
| **Vessels of Reception** | The will to receive; requires an added aim ("in order to bestow") placed over the act itself to be corrected. |
| **Vessels of Bestowal** | Capacity to give to others; the act needs no correction, but the sincerity of the intention does. |
| **Receiving in Order to Bestow** | The corrected use of the vessels of reception: actually receiving, while the intention is to give. Yields the light of Chokhmah. |
| **Bestowing in Order to Bestow** | The corrected use of the vessels of bestowal: both act and aim are outward. Yields the light of Chassidim. |
| **Light of Chokhmah (Light of Seeing / Panim)** | The light of the purpose of creation; must be seen, felt, and counted to fulfill its purpose. |
| **Light of Chassidim (Covered Chassidim)** | The light of the correction of creation, received through bestowal; stays "covered," hidden from view, like true charity. |
| **Gift vs. Charity** | A gift must be known and counted to show the measure of love; charity must be concealed from both sides to remain pure bestowal. |
| **"The wicked in their lives are called dead"** | Zohar's description of one cut off from the Creator through disparity of form — spiritually dead while physically alive. |
| **Middle Line** | The balance of the left line (Chokhmah in the vessels of reception) and the right line (Chassidim in the vessels of bestowal), so reception stays anchored to bestowal. |
| **Mitigation of the Judgment** | The process by which the prohibition on the will to receive is lifted by placing an aim to bestow over it, turning darkness into a place the light can shine. |

## Reflection Questions

1. Rabash contrasts Esau, "a man of the field," with Jacob, a "tent dweller." In your own life this week, where did you notice yourself acting from the field — from natural desire and reason — versus working against your nature toward something higher?

2. What is "the field" standing in for in your current state? Is there a specific desire or habit of mind you recognize as your own "Esau" right now?

3. Baal HaSulam frames the giving of the Torah as the means of correcting the will to receive into a will to bestow. Where in today's lesson did you sense this same movement — from self-concern toward concern for others — being asked of you?

4. "Love thy neighbor as thyself" is described as the great rule of Torah. What is one concrete way you could test that rule against something that happened today, rather than treating it as an abstract ideal?

5. Taken together, what do these two sources suggest about the relationship between nature (the field) and Torah (correction)? Is Torah presented as opposed to nature, or as completing it?

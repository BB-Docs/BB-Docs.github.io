### Opening

The lesson addresses a single, tightly-focused question: the will to receive, as the Creator created it, has no independent character at all — it is pure substance that simply feels good when fulfilled and bad when not, like a light bulb switching on and off. How, then, does this featureless "unit" ever come to build an identity of its own — to become, in the article's terms, righteous or wicked?

---

## Part 1 — A Substance With No Built-In Character

The will to receive as originally created is described as entirely without independence or self-identification — voltage in, light on; voltage off, light off. Everything a person is born with — intelligence, temperament, natural traits — is already given in the original "drop," and none of it is what the article means by righteous or wicked. Those two qualities are not built into the will to receive; they are the one thing a person must build for himself, through his own path.

**Why the negative is needed:** the Creator wants the will to receive to take on the character of "righteous," and does this specifically by working through its apparent opposite, the experience of "evil" or bad feeling. The life events a person passes through, including across repeated life cycles, exist to make the will to receive increasingly sensitive to its relationship with the Creator — not to develop personality traits, which are irrelevant to this process.

---

## Part 2 — Good and Bad Feeling as an Indicator, Not a Verdict

The lesson describes good and bad feeling as a kind of signal light on a device: red or green, indicating whether a person has succeeded in unifying what he is going through with the Creator, not indicating the objective goodness or badness of what is happening. A bad feeling signals that unification has not yet occurred; the fears, anxieties, and insecurities a person feels as threats to his existence are exactly this un-unified state. If a person manages to unify his experience with the Creator, the feeling shifts to good.

> *"The will to receive in us is a kind of detector... that finds what is our relationship to the Creator."*

The Creator is described as setting up a generally difficult life, except for one specific place of contact — a "ray of light" that a person is drawn toward for relief from everything else that feels bad.

---

## Part 3 — From Lo Lishma to Lishma

### Starting From Self-Interest

A person who cannot bear his negative feelings and cannot unify them with the Creator eventually learns, through accumulated suffering, that peace comes from attributing all of it to the Creator. This is named explicitly as **lo lishma**: "I have a bad life, but if I link that to the Creator, I feel it is good — I use the connection in order to receive [relief]." The lesson is clear that lo lishma is the necessary starting point, and that lishma develops out of it, not instead of it.

### The Grace of Sanctity

Even engaging with the Creator lo lishma — to sweeten one's own life — gradually draws what the lesson calls "the grace of sanctity": the person begins to feel that connection to the Creator is good in itself, not merely because it relieves his suffering. This, too, is still lo lishma. The transition to true **lishma** happens when a person wants connection to the Creator with no reference at all to whether he personally feels good or bad — to the point where he can actually want the difficult feeling, because it serves as the means of connection. This final stage is called **"receiving in order to bestow"** (rendered in the lesson as "receiving in order to be still").

---

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The will to receive has no built-in character.** Righteous and wicked are not innate traits — they are the one thing a person must build through his own path.
2. **Negative experience exists to sensitize the will to receive** to its relationship with the Creator, not to develop personality or ability.
3. **Good and bad feelings function as an indicator, not a verdict** — they show whether a person has unified his experience with the Creator, not whether the experience itself is objectively good or bad.
4. **The starting point is always lo lishma** — using connection to the Creator to relieve one's own suffering — and lishma develops gradually out of it.
5. **The endpoint (lishma) is wanting connection to the Creator independent of how one personally feels**, to the point of welcoming difficulty because it enables that connection.
6. **The soul, in this account, is the will to receive itself, functioning as the substrate through which a person's attitude toward the Creator — righteous or wicked — actually takes shape.**

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Will to Receive** | The basic, characterless substance of creation; feels good when fulfilled, bad when not, with no independent identity of its own. |
| **Righteous / Wicked** | Not innate qualities, but the one attitude toward the Creator a person must build for himself through experience — the only thing not already given in the original "drop." |
| **Unification** | Attributing one's negative or positive feelings to the Creator as their source; success is indicated by a shift from bad feeling to good. |
| **Lo Lishma** | Connecting to the Creator for the sake of relieving one's own suffering or improving one's own state; the necessary starting point of the path. |
| **Lishma** | Connecting to the Creator independent of personal benefit — eventually even welcoming difficulty because it enables the connection. |
| **Grace of Sanctity** | The point at which connection to the Creator begins to feel good in itself, even while still motivated by self-interest (still lo lishma). |
| **Receiving in Order to Bestow** | The final stage described, in which even negative feeling is desired because it serves adhesion to the Creator, rather than avoided. |

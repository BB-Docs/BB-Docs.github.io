### Opening

The article read at the start of the lesson takes up the Zohar's claim that "law and ordinance" — Malchut and Zeir Anpin (ZA) — together form the name of the Creator, and asks how two separate names can be one. Rav Laitman's June 25, 2002 lecture and the Q&A that follows take this in a practical direction: what does uniting "law" and "ordinance" actually require of a person, day to day? The lecture works through equivalence of form as the single mechanism behind all spiritual perception, why a person cannot generate the next degree of desire alone, why running away from the disturbances of this world doesn't work, and what it means that "the Torah is a spice" — before turning, in the closing exchange, to how a person actually knows whether he has moved from Lo Lishma to Lishma.

---

## Part 1 — Law and Ordinance as Correction, Not Restriction

### Corrections, Not Punishments

Rav Laitman opens by naming what "law and ordinance" actually refers to: the corrections the will to receive must take on in order to be fit for its eternal role.

> "These two things are actually, they emerge from the judgment that the will to receive, in order to be in the eternal state, worthy of its role, must take upon itself corrections. Corrections are limitations and changes. This is called law and ordinance."

He adds a reversal that recurs through the lecture: "All the things where we think that we are operating against ourselves, against our desire, it turns out it is the opposite."

### Attaining the Holy Names Only Through Equivalence of Form

Rav Laitman explains that a person cannot attain a quality of the Creator — mercy, grace, goodness — by description alone; he must first acquire the quality himself, "to the same extent," before he can perceive it in the Creator. Attaining a name of the Creator, in other words, means the person himself comes to carry that name.

> "When a person attains the holy names, it works in a different way. He can attain it only according to equivalence of form... If a person attains, what does it mean, merciful? He, to the same extent, becomes merciful."

He ties this to the 613 corrections: the Creator is "a whole general light that is clothed in the 613 corrections that a person performs on his 613 desires," with a specific light deposited in each commandment. When all the corrections are complete, the general light that clothes the person — the NRNHY — is itself called the Creator.

### Ordinance as the Law, Law as the Ordinance

The practical task, Rav Laitman says, is for ZA ("ordinance") and Malchut ("law") to take on each other's qualities — Malchut rising to the qualities of ZA, above her own reason (Da'at), since reason determines the essence of a degree. Uniting the two names this way is what reveals "all of the qualities of the Creator in the created being."

## Part 2 — Equivalence of Form: The Radio Receiver

### You Can Only Capture What You First Generate

Asked how a person actually comes to attain a quality "according to the degree of his correction," Rav Laitman answers with an extended analogy to a radio receiver:

> "In order to capture a radio wave in the radio receiver itself, you have to create a wave that resembles... the same frequency as the wave has... it's called a resonance effect. Otherwise, you won't be able to perceive, to capture anything on the outside... Every receiver is seemingly like a transmitter. It generates. It doesn't just receive."

He extends this to the physical senses themselves — eyes, ears, nerve endings — each built to generate internally the same frequency it is meant to perceive externally, which is why a person "cannot say that he is graceful or merciful" without first equipping himself with that quality.

### Developing the Sixth Sense

Rav Laitman compares this undeveloped capacity to a baby who cannot yet recognize sorrow in someone else because the relevant emotion hasn't developed in him yet. The same is true spiritually: the Creator is "bestowing upon us, imparting, throwing all good on us endlessly," but it isn't felt, because "this emotion is not yet developed in us... this is called concealment." Developing this undeveloped capacity — the "sixth sense" — is, he says, the wisdom of Kabbalah itself: "the wisdom of how to receive what he's giving."

> "We awaken the surrounding lights, and they refine the person... they make this emotion refined, sensitive, gentle, and then we truly begin to feel."

He adds that this refining process cannot be rushed past its natural pace on its own; a person's only lever is to draw as much of the "reforming light" as possible, through maximum changes of state — "ups and downs, all kinds of things... from the book and the group and the books."

### The Soul Develops Only Through a Person's Own Work

This gradual refinement is what Rav Laitman calls Neshama — and he stresses that, unlike ordinary growth, it cannot happen without the person's own effort: "in developing his soul, man is in free choice, and he has to do it by himself... the soul cannot develop without man's work."

## Part 3 — Deficiency, Prayer, and the Artificial Demand of Study

### Every Felt Desire Is Already a Request

Rav Laitman locates prayer not in formal words but in whatever a person actually feels: "Whatever I feel in my heart right now... all of these desires are immediately felt in the Creator... What I want in my heart, this is called a prayer, because my heart is connected with the Creator without anything being in the middle." He names this directly as MAN — a person's request, rising up from below:

> "Every request of mine is MAN. Like the kibbutznik in the Negev in the desert — they feel that they lack rain. They need rain. That's called a prayer."

### Deficiency Is Not Yet a Demand

Not every deficiency, he clarifies, advances a person toward correction — a person cannot reach into his own heart and change a desire directly. Instead, the desire has to be reshaped by external means: the environment, books, music — "external tactics... not by me getting into my heart with some key, and I turn it with some screwdriver. I can't do that. I'm not the master of the desire itself."

### The Artificial Deficiency of Study

Applying this to study specifically, Rav Laitman explains why a person has to manufacture a felt lack going into the study itself, rather than waiting to already have one:

> "This deficiency is an artificial deficiency... the exertion is to make it so that during the study, the desire to be corrected, to absorb surrounding light, to be changed by it — it will determine everything."

He connects this to the pledge described in the introduction to the Study of the Ten Sefirot (TES), Items 17–19: the student pledges, before the study, to strengthen faith in the Creator's guidance, and is warned that even correct quantity of effort is not enough if the quality — directing mind and heart to draw the light that "brings faith to one's heart" (the light of Hassadim, or Bina) — is neglected.

### One in the Heart, One in the Mouth

A student's question about "aiming the heart" leads Rav Laitman to distinguish the heart from the mouth: saying something with the mouth doesn't mean literally speaking it, but performing whatever external actions actually change the heart — chiefly, exposure to the right environment. "The peh, the mouth, is the place of the screen... we have only one action by which we can change the desire in the heart... to hear in a directed manner some influence from the outside."

## Part 4 — Why You Can't Run Away From This World

### The Question: Why Not Just Isolate for a Month?

A student proposes an efficient shortcut: take a month off, isolate from the world, and study intensively — ten hours of TES, six hours of Zohar a day — to force a breakthrough. Rav Laitman calls the premise short-sighted:

> "Everything where we exist, we exist in a degree called this world... it's simply the lowest one, the least important one, the most forgotten... How can I get out of it if I don't work with whatever is in it?"

Since the "surrounding conditions" of this world are actually a person's own uncorrected qualities projected outward, escaping the degree requires correcting those qualities — which requires staying in contact with the very disturbances a retreat would remove. He predicts what actually happens if the group tries: "after a day, maximum two days, you'll reach boredom... we do not supply matter to one another... you won't even have a need to engage in one another."

### Three States, Repeated

The real structure, he says, is a repeated cycle of three states: living in this world (absorbing its "coarseness"), then turning to the society to want to be rid of that coarseness, then coming to study to refine it — and then returning to the world again to pick up more.

> "Our whole activity includes... three main states: this world, with all the brainwashing it does to me; then I come and I want to wash my brain from my society... and so I come to study."

### The Pesach Exception

Asked why Passover involves an entire week of special, intensified effort, Rav Laitman treats it as the one deliberate, bounded exception to ordinary daily life — requiring extraordinary labor and expense beforehand ("like 300,000 shekels for Pesach — where has such a thing been heard?") specifically so the joy of the holiday itself can be earned and felt, not because retreat from the world is otherwise the goal.

## Part 5 — The Torah as a Spice: A New Creation

### Why "Creating the Torah" Is Called a Creation

A student asks how the Creator can be said to have "created the Torah as a spice" for the evil inclination, given that "creation" elsewhere describes what is genuinely new and that the Creator is described as the source of light, not darkness. Rav Laitman clarifies that what is created is not the Torah itself, but the corrective action — the spice — that operates within the will to receive:

> "I've created the Torah's spice means that within the desire to receive, the light operates, and it changes the desire to receive to be in order to bestow... this is an action that was made within the desire to receive, which is why it's called creation — which is a new thing."

### Shame as Its Own Creation

Part of that corrective action, he explains, is a specific sequence: the Creator gives pleasure, then reveals that He is the one giving it — and that revelation awakens shame in the receiver, which in turn becomes the seed of a desire for equivalence of form.

> "The Creator once gives me nourishment, and on the second time... He shows me that He is the one who is doing this to me... and awakens in me all kinds of connections in relation to this — shame and responses... And I said this many times, that shame is a creation in and of its own."

### Telling Lo Lishma From Lishma

The final question of the exchange asks directly: when does a person actually move from Lo Lishma to Lishma? Rav Laitman points back to the introduction to TES: Lishma is wanting only for the Creator's sake — to bestow upon Him — and Lo Lishma always retains some measure of wanting for oneself. Pressed on how a person can tell which one he's actually in, he gives a concrete test rather than a feeling to search for:

> "In accordance with opening your eyes, the Creator opens up, and as a result of your desire — if in the same desire you want to bestow to Him — He fills you with His presence, with light. That's considered that, in truth, your desire is in order to bestow to Him, in accordance with the equivalence of form. And if not, then you're just telling us that you want to bestow — you're lying, as usual."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **"Law and ordinance" names the corrections placed on the will to receive** — limitations and changes that, though they feel like restrictions, are actually the opposite of working against oneself.
2. **A quality of the Creator can only be attained by first acquiring it in oneself.** Equivalence of form works like a radio receiver: perception requires generating, internally, the same frequency as what is to be perceived.
3. **The soul (Neshama) develops only through a person's own work, never automatically.** Surrounding light refines the undeveloped "sixth sense," but only in response to a person's request — never without it.
4. **Every desire actually felt in the heart already reaches the Creator as MAN, a request** — but not every deficiency becomes a demand for correction; the desire itself must be reshaped by external means (environment, books), never directly by will.
5. **Study requires manufacturing a felt lack going in — an "artificial deficiency"** — so that the desire to be corrected, not mere information, is what actually determines the study.
6. **Isolating from this world's disturbances doesn't work**, because those disturbances are a person's own uncorrected qualities projected outward; correction requires staying in a repeated cycle of this world, society, and study.
7. **"The Torah as a spice" is itself a new creation** — the corrective action of light within the will to receive, which also produces shame as its own distinct creation once a person recognizes the Creator as the giver.
8. **Lishma is tested by result, not by feeling:** wanting to bestow is confirmed only if it brings the Creator's presence and light; otherwise the claim of wanting to bestow is not yet true.

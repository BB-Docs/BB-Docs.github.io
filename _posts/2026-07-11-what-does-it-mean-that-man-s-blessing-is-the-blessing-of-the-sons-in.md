### Opening

After the article reading, a student asks why the inability of the Creator to bestow upon the lower ones is called the **"sorrow of the Shechina"** rather than the "sorrow of the Creator." Rav Laitman's answer opens into the session's real subject: pain and disturbance are not simply obstacles to be endured — they are the very vessels through which a person builds closeness to the Creator. The rest of the lecture and Q&A work out what that means in practice: how to read pain correctly, why we can't yet decode our own blows, and how to hold a connection to the Creator inside an ordinary day full of news, work, and distraction.

---

## Part 1 — Sorrow of the Shechina: One Thing, Two Perspectives

### Why "Shechina" and Not "the Creator"

A student asks directly why the text says "sorrow of the Shechina" when it is the Creator's desire to do good that goes unfulfilled. Rav Laitman explains that the Creator and the Shechina are, in the words of Baal HaSulam, "one thing" — light and vessel (*Kli*). The Shechina is the meeting place, the point of contact between the Creator and the created beings:

> "That same general vessel of the collection of all of the souls that is revealed at the end of correction towards each and every one — this is called the vessel of the Shechina. That same vessel... also exists in the Creator, in Bina."

Because that shared vessel is not yet full, there is a gap — and that gap, felt from the perspective of the vessel (the created being), is what is called the sorrow of the Shechina rather than the sorrow of the Creator. Rav Laitman notes this is a genuinely difficult point for beginners, since it can sound like there are three things (Creator, created being, Shechina) when there are really only two, viewed from two sides.

**Key point:** "The sorrow of the Shechina is much greater than the sorrow of the lower ones, inasmuch as Bina is greater than the souls" — the lack is proportional to the greatness of what should be filling it.

## Part 2 — Pain as an Indication, Not Just Suffering

### The Doctor Metaphor

Rav Laitman turns to a practical framing: disturbances and difficulties are meant to become vessels, "the place where... the feeling of those lacks that the Shechina feels" can be located and worked with. He compares this directly to physical pain:

> "If you wouldn't feel the pain, you wouldn't go to the doctor. This is an indication that you need to go and make corrections."

Just as bodily pain tells a person something is wrong and needs care, spiritual pain — the pains connected to honor, money, status, and the general friction of life — is meant to show a person "to what extent he's not corrected and how much more he needs to advance." The problem, he says, is that most people (and most of humanity) feel the pain but never learn what caused it or what it points to.

### Learning to Decode the Blow

Rav Laitman extends the doctor image: a good doctor doesn't stop at "it hurts" — he asks when it hurts, how, under what conditions, until the pattern reveals the cause. In the same way, a person is meant to eventually become an expert at reading his own pain back to its source and its correction:

> "This is the difference between knowing, being an expert in that correction, and just suffering like a beast."

He connects this to the verse about the "healer of all patients, the healer to those who are brokenhearted" — ultimately it is the Creator who reveals to a person exactly what pains him and why.

## Part 3 — Why We Can't Yet Decode Our Own Blows

### The Danger of Premature Interpretation

A student asks whether every specific disturbance is meant to become, in the future, "the vessel where the Creator is revealed." Rav Laitman's answer is careful: yes, in principle — but not yet, and not by forcing an interpretation now.

> "We don't yet understand why it is forbidden for us now, for the time being, to make these calculations of why did I receive this kind of blow or that kind... because today we don't have the data to interpret it correctly, and it will just cause us to have all kinds of wild imaginations."

Instead of trying to trace each specific blow to a specific cause, the instruction is to stay "locked on the Creator" — not to let attention drift away from Him — and to tie every event, good or bad, back to that connection as much as possible. Understanding of the discernments comes gradually, "bit by bit," through that sustained connection rather than through analysis.

### Building Toward a Screen

Over time, working in "three lines," a person begins to see the corrupted vessel opposite the light that would fill it, to recognize which quality is missing, and eventually to build a **screen** (masach) that lets him receive correctly. This capacity is described as something built gradually, not granted all at once.

## Part 4 — Holding the Connection Through Ordinary Life

### The First Step: Search, Find, Hold

Asked what the actual first step in the work is, Rav Laitman answers plainly:

> "The first step is, first of all, to search for Him. You found, let's say, some thought, some kind of inner feeling, something with Him. Hold on to it. Hold on to it and then go back to your ordinary life. But together with this feeling, pull Him into your life."

The measure of success is described concretely: "to the extent that you will not run away from His feeling, from feeling Him."

### The News Example

A student presses on a practical case: watching the news. Rav Laitman says that if, while hearing the news, a person can tie what he hears to the Creator — that the suffering in the world is meant to draw us toward inner connection — then the news itself becomes fuel for advancement, "my hub of ascent." But if after a couple of minutes the person is simply absorbed in the business of the news and has lost the connection, he must stop:

> "I need to cut it off and stop and renew the connection."

The boundary is not fixed in advance; it has to be learned by practice — described as building a "screen" so that the connection with the Creator matters more than anything else, "whatever state you're in."

### What About Information You Can't Control?

A student objects: some information doesn't wait for permission — it simply enters. Rav Laitman's answer is that if a person has genuinely "locked himself onto the Creator" from the start, he becomes able to filter what enters and how much it affects him — but this capacity depends entirely on maintaining the prior connection, not on controlling the information itself. He frames this as the meaning of "if I am not for myself, who will be for me?" — the work is done by the person himself, not done to him.

## Part 5 — Environment and a Practical Recipe

### Building a Stronger Environment

A student asks how to build an environment strong enough to outweigh the environment imposed by daily necessity (work, family, obligations). Rav Laitman's answer is that all these environments — online, in writing, with people, in daily necessities — are themselves arranged by the same source that sends disturbances as corrections, and should be related to that way. He offers a general recipe rather than a formula for each case:

> "You'll see how it's possible to live together with your work and the Creator, your wife and the Creator, your groceries and bank and all kinds of things — we have to go in and out of and mingle with — and with the Creator. It's possible."

### Seeing in a New Dimension

Rav Laitman offers an image from a recent television interview: the way a 3D stereogram image only becomes visible when a person deliberately *defocuses* his eyes rather than staring directly at the picture. In the same way, refocusing on reality "with a new quality, with a new attitude" lets a person begin to see depth behind the externality of the world — "a new world opens up there in 3D and in depth." He calls this a stage of preparation, before the actual path of Kabbalah properly begins.

### On Failure and Forgetting

Asked what to make of forgetting the work altogether during the day, Rav Laitman's answer is not to treat it as failure requiring analysis:

> "A person should not be sorry for the moment that has passed... we have to be a bit more understandable and loving towards forgetting and being thrown out... if I'm thrown out of something, that means I've probably completed the previous discernment."

The practical instruction is simply to restart — "another drop and another drop" — and to use reminders (through the group, or something as simple as an alarm) to return to the work.

---

## Part 6 — Closing Round: What the Group Took From the Lesson

In the closing round, several participants summarize what they are taking for practice, and Rav Laitman affirms and adds to their points:

- **Disturbances are the vessels.** Difficulties become the places where a person feels closeness to the Creator, not simply obstacles.
- **When thrown out of a state, relate to it lightly** — it may mean a discernment was completed, not that something has gone wrong.
- **If a person doesn't remember the work at all, there is nothing to do in that moment** — but once he does remember (through himself or through the group), the process of holding the connection can resume.
- **Becoming an "expert"** in reading one's own states is described as building "a new muscle" through repeated practice, not a one-time realization.
- **The work of connecting to the Creator is what the group (the society) exists to advance** — "we'll give what we can to the society and the society will advance us."

One participant closes by highlighting the instruction to stabilize the vision and feeling of the Creator *before* going out into the external world, rather than trying to find Him after the fact.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The "sorrow of the Shechina" is the same fact as the sorrow of the Creator, seen from the side of the vessel rather than the light** — the Shechina is the shared meeting point between them, currently unfilled.
2. **Pain and disturbance are indications, not just suffering.** Like physical pain sending a person to the doctor, spiritual pain is meant to show exactly where correction is needed.
3. **It is currently forbidden — not merely unwise — to try to decode each specific blow to its specific cause.** Without enough data, this leads to wild imagination and loss of the right path; the correct response is to stay locked on the Creator.
4. **The first step in the work is to search for Him, hold onto whatever is found, and carry it back into ordinary life** — measured by how long a person can avoid letting go of that feeling.
5. **Everyday inputs (the news, work, obligations) can become material for advancement or a source of disconnection**, depending only on whether they stay tied to the Creator; when the tie breaks, the response is to stop and renew it, not to force through.
6. **A person's various environments are not obstacles to route around but material arranged by the same source that sends corrections** — the aim is to bring the Creator into all of them, not to escape them.
7. **Forgetting or being "thrown out" of a state should be met with understanding, not self-criticism** — it may indicate a discernment has been completed, and the practical response is simply to restart.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Shechina** | The shared vessel/meeting point between the Creator and the created beings; also exists within Bina. Its "sorrow" is the gap felt because this shared vessel is not yet full. |
| **Malchut** | The will to receive; the vessel of creation; the aspect from whose perspective the Shechina's sorrow is discussed. |
| **Bina** | The quality of bestowal; the sefirah in which the vessel of the Shechina also exists, filling its lower part (Zat de Bina) in order to bestow to the lower ones. |
| **ZA (Zeir Anpin)** | The Creator's aspect that Malchut unites with; the article specifies Malchut unites with ZA, or more precisely with Yesod de ZA. |
| **Yesod** | The sefirah called "righteous" (Tzaddik) within ZA; the channel of bestowal to Malchut, called "Joseph" in the article's terms. |
| **Vessel (Kli)** | The receiving instrument paired with light; a vessel not yet full or not yet corrected is felt as lack or pain. |
| **Screen (Masach)** | The corrected capacity to receive in order to bestow; built gradually through sustained connection to the Creator rather than granted all at once. |
| **Three Lines** | The mode of work in which a person eventually sees the corrupted vessel opposite the light, learns what quality is missing, and how to correct it. |
| **Locked on the Creator** | Rav Laitman's phrase for the required orientation: not letting the Creator out of one's field of attention, and tying every event back to Him rather than analyzing it independently. |
| **Awakening from Below** | The principle that spiritual attainment (e.g., the "lights of Shabbat") is earned through effort rather than given freely; referenced via "I labored and found, believe it." |
| **Olam, Shana, Nefesh** | "World, Year, Soul" — three discernments referenced from Baal HaSulam's writings connected to establishing contact with the Creator; the article recommended for repeated reading (the anecdote of reading a text 70 times). |

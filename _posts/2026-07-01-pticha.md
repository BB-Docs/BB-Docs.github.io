### Opening

This session is described as the fifth (and, in Rav's own correction, possibly sixth) part of an ongoing series walking through the shattering of the vessels — from the unfolding of the worlds of Nikudim, through Katnut and Gadlut, to the shattering itself. Having laid out the technical picture, the lesson turns to why it matters: how the shattering connects to the work in a group, to the history recorded in the Torah, and to a person's own life right now. The central question running through the Q&A that follows: why did the vessels of bestowal and reception have to shatter and intermix at all, and what does "crossing the barrier" into a felt understanding of that process actually give a person?

---

## Part 1 — The Eight Expansions and the Eight Kings

### One Shattering, Eight Expansions

The light that breaks the vessel travels from above down — through Keter, Abba and Ima, ZON, and all the way to Siyum — in eight expansions. Because Malchut is involved in every one of these expansions, they are called the **eight kings** (Melachim, from the word Malchut). The Torah records this as "eight kings that died... that lived in the land of Edom" — the last of the eight did not shatter, and this is already the world of **Atzilut**.

**Rav's clarification:** this is not eight separate shatterings, each with its own thought, intention, and decision. It is one internal process — a single decision expressed as a chain reaction across eight expansions.

### Why Seven Kings in Some Places, Eight in Others

A question from the hall (Oren) raised the discrepancy between "seven kings" and "eight kings" found in different sources. Rav's answer: the kings emerge from the refinement of the partzufim, which happens in four stages of coarseness. The same fourfold pattern governs the shattering — except here the refinement is an actual fall and intermixing, not simply a rise of the screen.

| Coarseness Level | Result |
|---|---|
| Coarseness four | Four kings emerge |
| Coarseness three | Four more kings emerge |
| **Total** | **Eight kings ("that died")** |

The count of seven in some sources reflects how Tiferet is sometimes treated as split into two parts (upper third and lower two-thirds) or how Da'at is counted; Rav noted this distinction isn't worth pursuing too deeply, since Rav himself did not go into it in this particular lesson.

## Part 2 — Why the Vessels Had to Shatter

### Before and After

Before the shattering: the vessel consisted of the "upper nine and Malchut," with no connection between them — the upper nine being the Creator's attitude toward the created being, and Malchut being the created being's attitude toward the Creator.

After the shattering: the upper nine and Malchut become mixed into each other — "the first nine in Malchut, and Malchut in the first nine." This intermixing is, in Rav's words, **the profit of the shattering**: it is what makes it possible to eventually correct the part of the vessel that otherwise would remain forever empty (below the Tabur, where the light of Chochmah cannot enter directly under the law of the first restriction).

> Rav's example: a teacher who enters among criminals, first has to make a connection with them — has to appear like them, to be "wicked" like them — and only then, gradually, can he begin to raise them up.

### One Root, Many Shatterings

Asked whether the shattering of the vessels relates to the shattering of the tablets, Rav confirmed that all the shatterings — the vessels, the tablets, the first and second Temples, the Tree of Knowledge — share the same root cause and the same process. They differ only in the level on which they occur.

## Part 3 — Quantity and Quality of Exertion: Three to Five Years

### Baal HaSulam's Own Words

Rav pointed to Baal HaSulam's statement in the Preface to the Study of the Ten Sefirot: *"I labored and found — believe."* A person can fulfill the necessary **quantity** of exertion (a certain number of study hours every day) but still fail to enter spirituality because he neglected the **quality** of exertion — meaning he did not direct his mind and heart during study toward drawing the surrounding light that brings faith into the heart.

**Faith**, in this context, is described directly as the light that has to come — it is what quality of exertion is meant to draw down.

### Why "Years" Isn't Chronological Time

In response to a question about the three-to-five-year window Baal HaSulam gives for attaining spirituality, Rav explained that *shanim* (years) comes from the root *shinui* (change) — the measure is not chronological time but the number of real changes a person goes through. Time spent not actually exerting toward change "is not considered," however many years pass on the clock.

### The Group Multiplies the Light

Studying in a group rather than alone does not simply multiply the surrounding light by the number of people present — it multiplies it further, because uniting with fifty others requires canceling one's own ego to connect with them, and that added exertion itself draws more light.

> "It's not the surrounding light times 50. It's also times the exertion."

Rav estimated that a path which might otherwise take 2,000–3,000 years alone can be reduced to a few years within a group — provided the guidance of a Rav is also present, since without guidance a person has no way of checking whether a given step is correct.

## Part 4 — Crossing the Barrier

### The Mechanism of Governance

Rav described a person's inner life as a kind of feedback loop: desires and thoughts rise up into an "operating system," and come back down as one's actual experience and governance. Right now, this loop is opaque to us — we don't know whether we are activating it correctly, and so we experience "blows" day after day without understanding why, or how to influence the outcome for the better.

### What Changes When You Cross

Crossing the barrier — from this world toward Ein Sof, Adam Kadmon, and the worlds of Atzilut, Bria, Yetzirah, Asiyah — means beginning to understand this mechanism, at least to some extent. With that understanding comes the ability to actually correct oneself, to see in advance that harmful intent toward another will work against oneself, and to plan one's own future rather than remain blind to what's coming.

**Rav's summary of the stakes:** a person who crosses the barrier becomes, in his own words, "master of his own life."

## Part 5 — Q&A: Separation as the Path to Connection

### Why the Creator Connects to the Worst Qualities

Asked (Tatiana) why the Creator must connect to even the worst qualities of creation in order to correct them, and what specifically could not be known about governance before the shattering, Rav explained: before the shattering, light could not be received below a certain point, meaning parts of the created being were permanently disconnected from the quality of the Creator, unable to reach the purpose of creation. The only way to enable correction was to mix the vessels of bestowal and reception together — because only after that mixture exists can the actual work of correction begin.

### Separation as Springboard

A participant asked whether it's fair to say that the separation between bodies brings about the connection between souls. Rav confirmed this directly — after correction, the very force of separation becomes usable for the sake of connection.

> "It's very interesting that specifically the separation brings connection."

### Closing Note

Rav closed by returning to the practical instruction already given: the work now is to focus on the quality of exertion — on the connection between us — since in the previous lesson it was said there is no good person and no bad person; rather, separation is bad and connection is good. That correction of separation is what the shattering studied in this lesson ultimately points toward.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The shattering is one decision, not eight.** It unfolds across eight expansions of light from Keter to Siyum — called the "eight kings" because Malchut is involved in each — but Rav was explicit that it is a single internal process, not eight separate acts.
2. **Separation had to happen before connection was possible.** Before the shattering, the vessels of bestowal and reception were permanently disconnected; only their mixing opened the possibility of correcting the part of the vessel that would otherwise remain empty forever.
3. **Quantity of exertion is not the same as quality of exertion.** Study hours alone don't guarantee entry into spirituality — what matters is whether the mind and heart are directed, during that study, toward drawing the light that brings faith.
4. **"Years" measures change, not time.** The three-to-five-year window Baal HaSulam describes is measured in real inner changes (shinuim), not chronological years — time spent without genuine exertion doesn't count toward it.
5. **A group multiplies the light beyond simple addition**, because uniting with others requires canceling one's own ego — and that extra exertion is what shortens a path that could otherwise take thousands of years.
6. **Crossing the barrier means understanding your own feedback loop with governance** — enough to begin correcting yourself and planning your future, rather than remaining blind to what tomorrow brings.
7. **Separation, once corrected, becomes the very tool of connection** — the same force that divides bodies is what, when redirected, connects souls.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Pticha (Preface to the Study of the Ten Sefirot)** | Baal HaSulam's introductory work explaining the wisdom of Kabbalah, including the account of the shattering of the vessels discussed in this lesson. |
| **Eight Kings (Melachim)** | The eight expansions of light from above down (Keter through Siyum) during which the vessels shattered, named for Malchut's involvement in each ("Melachim" from "Malchut"). Recorded in the Torah as the kings that died in the land of Edom. |
| **Nikudim / Katnut / Gadlut** | Stages in the development of the vessel — its emergence (Nikudim) and growth through smallness (Katnut) and greatness (Gadlut) — leading up to and through the shattering. |
| **Shevirat HaKelim (Shattering of the Vessels)** | The breaking and intermixing of the vessels of bestowal and reception, without which the parts of the vessel below a certain point could never be corrected. |
| **Atzilut** | The world associated with the eighth king, the one that did not shatter; represents the corrected state beyond the shattering. |
| **Quantity vs. Quality of Exertion** | Baal HaSulam's distinction, from the Preface to the Study of the Ten Sefirot: the number of hours studied (quantity) versus whether the mind and heart are directed toward drawing the light during that study (quality). |
| **Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif)** | The light drawn through study, which a group amplifies far beyond simple addition because of the added exertion required to cancel the ego and unite. |
| **Crossing the Barrier** | Moving from this world into a felt understanding of spiritual governance — the point at which a person can begin to actually correct and plan their own life. |
| **Governance / Providence** | The feedback mechanism through which a person's desires and thoughts are processed and returned as lived experience — currently opaque, which is why its effects are felt as unexplained "blows." |
| **Separation and Connection** | Reframed in the lesson as the real subject of correction: not good people versus bad people, but separation (bad) versus connection (good) — the single thing the shattering points toward correcting. |

### Opening

The article read at the start of the lesson takes up a specific puzzle: how two seemingly contradictory verses — "the Lord will favor you" and "who does not turn His face" — can both be true, resolved through equivalence of form, the correction of Malchut, and the concealment (Achoraim) that governs a person before he is rewarded with the desire to bestow. Rav Laitman's lecture and the Q&A that follows, however, turn to a related but distinct question a student raises directly at the start: if each person stands individually opposite the Creator, why is almost everything done here group work — connecting with friends, building a society, correcting the "shattering"? How does connecting with other people actually get someone connected to the Creator?

---

## Part 1 — The Initial Thought and Why the Created Being Had to Be Divided

### Everything Is Derived From the End

Rav Laitman answers by going back to a principle he returns to throughout the lecture: the end result was present in the initial thought, and everything about the path — its stages, its conditions, its difficulties — has to be understood by tracing back to that final state, not by examining the steps themselves in isolation.

> "The end result meaning the end state, the final state of the created being, and from there, from the end, the Creator, as if, went to the beginning and then started creation from the final picture. The final picture determines everything."

### Why One Created Being Became Many

Since the goal was for the created being to reach adhesion with the Creator through his own free choice, and since direct contact with the Creator is concealed, a person needs some way to examine and supervise himself. Rav Laitman explains that this is exactly what the division of the created being provides: a person cannot measure his distance from the Creator directly, but he can check his distance from the friends next to him, "and know that to the extent that you equalize with them, to the same extent you are also going to equalize with the Creator."

This is why, he explains, Adam HaRishon's structure was divided into 620 individual desires ("organs," he calls them) through the shattering — each one absorbing an incorporation of all the others:

> "The shattering has been made so that as a result, every desire of the 620 desires absorbed into itself an incorporation of all the other desires... any cell in the body needs to include and feel all the other cells in the body. That's how it truly is, and we're revealing this in biology."

### Necessary, But Not Sufficient

Working on these desires — connecting them to one another — builds the quality of bestowal within a person. But Rav Laitman is careful to draw a line: connecting with the other souls doesn't by itself connect a person to the Creator. It only works if the goal was there first.

> "There is this necessary condition of necessary but insufficient... The necessary condition is that, to begin with, I think about the Creator. I want to connect with Him. And therefore it necessitates me to correct my attitude and connect with the rest of the souls."

## Part 2 — Two Processes as One

### The Friends as a Detour Back to the Creator

Rav Laitman describes what actually happens once a person tries, in practice, to connect with friends with this intention: the attempt is difficult, and the difficulty itself becomes the mechanism. Realizing he cannot connect on his own strength turns a person back toward the Creator to ask for help.

> "This work that I cannot do necessitates me to turn to the Creator once more... To the extent that I want to connect with the friends, to that extent I feel the urgency to turn to Him, to ask for strength, and to that extent I connect to Him. And I go back to connect to them, and it becomes one and the same."

He adds that eventually a person sees that connection with the friends and connection with the Creator "are not two directions that I need to connect to each other, but they're literally one — they exist in a single carrier." He describes the meeting point between two people this way:

> "If there is a point of connection, point of contact, that welding between us, where we have that drop of glue between us that will glue us — it is only in that drop of glue the Creator will dwell. Not within you, not inside of me, but between us."

### Connection, Love, and Similarity Are the Same Thing

A student then asks whether connection with the Creator and similarity to the Creator are two separate issues. Rav Laitman answers that in spirituality — unlike in the physical world, where two similar cars can resemble each other without being joined — there is no gap between them:

> "In spirituality, the more they have in common, meaning these qualities, then they become one. That is the law of equivalence of form, the law of the spiritual space."

## Part 3 — Choosing Friends: Why Not Everyone

A further question asks why connection has to be with these specific people rather than with strangers anywhere in the world. Rav Laitman's answer is practical rather than mystical: a person cannot sustain focused connection with too large or too different a group.

> "It's very difficult. I can't focus on a hundred people and give attention to all of them and connect to all of them. So what's better? Better that there will be people that I feel their response, that they feel my effort. They understand me and I understand them."

Real support, he says, requires people close enough in mentality and perception that their influence and interpretation of each other actually land correctly — not a shared résumé or personality, just a workable closeness that lets people "correctly interpret each other."

## Part 4 — From Wanting His Pleasure to Wanting Him

### What "Wanting" Actually Reveals

Asked about a line in the article advising a person not to seek advice on how to attain abundance but only on how to attain the desire to bestow, Rav Laitman starts from the nature of wanting itself:

> "It's impossible to say 'I want' if it means that there's something I lack... In truth, he wants to find the Creator. All of our desires — we don't know that right now — but ultimately we want Him. We want to feel Him. All the pleasures that you see actually come from Him in various clothings."

### Flipping the Ratio: Zero Percent to One Hundred Percent

The real work, he explains, is a reversal in what a person values about the Creator — moving from valuing only the pleasure the Creator can supply, to valuing connection with the Creator Himself:

> "To begin with, the Creator's importance is zero for us. The desire to use Him, to exploit Him, to enjoy Him — that's a hundred percent. Now I need to reach a state where this hundred percent desire to enjoy Him will be zero in me, and the desire to know His importance, to connect with Him, to be with Him — a hundred percent."

This shift, he says, cannot be self-generated: "This desire for importance, I have to receive it. I can't get it from myself. I can only receive it from the environment." The practical description of the process (Lo Lishma) is growing a taste for spiritual pleasure large enough to outweigh ordinary, corporeal pleasure — food, family, money, respect, knowledge — until the preference is constant and permanent.

### Reaching the Machsom

> "If I reach it in a permanent, constant way, it's a sign that I reached the machsom, the barrier. Maximum will to receive, to receive spirituality. Nothing more is required of us, nothing more is demanded... After that, I'm allowed to enter spirituality, but this is given to me through the screen. I'm being taken care of at that point. It's not I who transitions myself."

He warns against two shortcuts: waiting passively for suffering to push a person out of this world ("that is a very long path, thousands of years"), or having the society manufacture artificial excitement about spirituality rather than a genuine, felt impression. When a student (Misha) objected that hyping spirituality like a sales pitch — "look, guys, the Creator is great, look what a product I have" — feels forced and won't land, Rav Laitman agreed it isn't about salesmanship, but about finding language and company that lets each person be genuinely, not artificially, impressed.

## Part 5 — Society Supplies the Deficiency You Don't Have

### Why You Cannot Do This Alone

Rav Laitman returns to why the environment is indispensable rather than optional: a person has no independent capacity to generate the next degree of desire on his own.

> "In me, myself, there's nothing to begin with that I can even rise one step, one degree. There isn't. I need to receive the necessity for that degree from the environment."

He extends this to the vessel itself: if a person already possessed the completed vessel (Kli), he would need neither friends nor the Creator to reveal it. Instead, the deficiency itself — "which is not mine" — is received through the society, and only by turning to the Creator by way of that borrowed deficiency does a person become worthy of receiving the force to bestow.

### Two Different Kinds of Ego

A student's question about "acquiring deficiency" through the friends leads Rav Laitman to a distinction between two very different experiences of self-discovery. Ordinary group techniques — he compares this to psychologists or military training — break down a person's ego just to get him to cooperate for the sake of the group's survival. That is not the same thing as what happens when a person connects to friends specifically in order to reach the Creator:

> "The fact that you're going to connect to the friends in order to reach adhesion with the Creator invites upon you the surrounding light, which opens up your soul... it starts revealing to you the ego, your evil inclination... It's something completely different [from] that animalistic or beastly ego."

### The Couple Example

Illustrating that outcome depends entirely on the underlying intention, Rav Laitman uses the image of a quarreling husband and wife: "It is written, man and woman, the Shechina is between them." Whether the couple's reconciliation actually succeeds, he says, depends on whether the initial thought behind it was truly connection — "if we do not take into account from the beginning that the end of the action is the initial thought," the same corrective work does not land the same way.

---

## Part 6 — Absorbing the Impression: Iron Discipline

### Making an Impression Your Own

Rav Laitman describes the discipline required to actually use whatever a person receives from the friends, rather than simply feeling comforted by belonging to a group:

> "It is written that a creator's servant needs to be as hard as iron... I need to measure with what thought I went to hear some information, some advice, from the friends... Does this impression really give me [only] satisfaction — thank God I'm in loyal hands, I can shut my eyes like a baby and they'll take me somewhere — or have I truly received from them an impression, not mine, but now I make it my own?"

Practically, this means opening a book or an article after receiving an impression from the friends, in order to invite the surrounding light through that impression — using it to notice, and work on, one's own bad vessels rather than resting in the good feeling itself.

### Daily Practice and Support

He adds that this requires steady, daily structure: "each and every one must have every day a part in the study, a part in his work" — along with support from the society to sustain both the inner work and ordinary responsibilities at home and at work, since without that support the whole effort collapses into empty formality.

### Where the Real Self-Check Happens

The final point of the exchange is that self-examination belongs in the good states, not the bad ones:

> "The calculation with a person is in the good states, not in the bad states... Do you use it for good things, or did you fill yourself and you're sitting like that? [Or] you take this impression and you really open a book."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The end determines the path.** Every stage, condition, and difficulty in the work has to be traced back to the final state the Creator intended — not examined as an isolated event.
2. **The created being was divided so a person would have something to measure himself against.** Direct comparison with the Creator is impossible under concealment; comparison with the friends is the substitute mechanism, tied to Adam HaRishon's division into many desires through the shattering.
3. **Connecting with friends is necessary but not sufficient on its own.** It only becomes a path to the Creator if the intention to reach the Creator comes first; otherwise it is just cooperation.
4. **Difficulty connecting with friends is what turns a person back to the Creator.** The two connections — to the friends and to the Creator — end up being one and the same process, not two parallel tracks.
5. **The real work is a reversal of importance, not just of behavior:** from valuing the Creator only for the pleasure He supplies, to valuing connection with Him for its own sake — a shift that must be received from the environment, since a person cannot generate it alone.
6. **Reaching the machsom (barrier) means arriving at a constant, permanent preference for spiritual over corporeal pleasure.** After that point, the movement into spirituality is no longer self-driven — it happens through the screen.
7. **Genuine self-discovery through the friends is different from ordinary group pressure.** Surrounding light invited through friends aimed at the Creator reveals the real, spiritual ego — not the "beastly" ego uncovered by ordinary social techniques — and requires actively absorbing, not just passively enjoying, whatever impression the friends provide.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Necessary But Insufficient** | Rav Laitman's description of connecting with other souls: required for reaching the Creator, but only effective if the intention to reach the Creator was the reason for connecting in the first place. |
| **Shattering (of the Vessels)** | The event that divided the single created being (Adam HaRishon) into 620 individual desires, each one absorbing an incorporation of all the others. |
| **620 Desires** | The parts ("organs") that Adam HaRishon's single vessel was divided into through the shattering; each person's individual desires are pieces of this whole. |
| **Equivalence of Form** | In spirituality, similarity to the Creator and connection with the Creator are the same thing — unlike in the corporeal world, where resemblance and connection are separate. |
| **Single Carrier / Collective Vessel** | The state in which connection with the friends and connection with the Creator stop being two separate directions and become one process, existing in one shared vessel. |
| **Lo Lishma / Lishma** | Not for Her sake (self-motivated, still working to enjoy) / For Her sake. Described here as growing a preference for spiritual pleasure until it constantly outweighs corporeal pleasure. |
| **Machsom (Barrier)** | The point of reaching a permanent, constant preference for spiritual pleasure; after this, entry into spirituality is given through the screen rather than self-produced. |
| **Screen (Masach)** | The acquired capacity to receive in order to bestow; what a person is given only after reaching the machsom. |
| **Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif)** | The light invited by connecting with friends with the intention of reaching the Creator; reveals a person's real (spiritual) ego rather than an ordinary social one. |
| **Kli (Vessel)** | The capacity to receive that a person does not yet fully possess; the deficiency for it must be received from the environment rather than generated independently. |
| **Shechina** | Divine Presence — described as dwelling in the point of connection between two people (or between the created being and the Creator), not within either one separately. |
| **Machsom-Level Ego vs. Beastly Ego** | The distinction between the real, spiritual ego revealed by surrounding light aimed at the Creator, and the ordinary self-interest exposed by group pressure alone (as in psychology or military training). |

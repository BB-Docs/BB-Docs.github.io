### Opening

This is a special two-part Saturday session: the group first reads Rabash's article "The Purpose of Society, 2," then watches a recorded 2003 lesson in which Rav Laitman works through the article's practical implications, followed by a live Q&A. The article opens from a stark premise: a person is built entirely out of self-love, with no other fuel for action — so how can such a being ever be brought to work "in order to bestow," against his own nature? Rabash's answer is that this cannot be done alone; it requires a society built specifically to generate a force strong enough to work against the self. The lesson spends its time unpacking what that actually looks like in practice — seriousness, frivolity, equality among friends, and how to draw what Rabash calls "the reforming light" through the group.

---

## Part 1 — Rabash's Article: Why Self-Love Requires a Society

### The Problem: A Being Made of Self-Love

Man is created with a vessel called **self-love** — without seeing personal benefit in an action, a person has no fuel to make even the slightest move. Annulling this self-love is necessary to achieve **Dvikut** (adhesion) with the Creator, meaning equivalence of form — but this runs directly against nature. Since it cannot be done individually, a society is needed: a group of individuals who unanimously agree to work together on **annulling the will to receive**, which Rabash calls evil, since it blocks the goal for which man was created.

### Seriousness, and Walking Humbly

Rabash instructs that during the assembly, members should remain serious so as not to lose the intention for which they gathered — but *externally*, one should "walk humbly," appearing as though not serious, while a fire actually burns within. Members should watch that frivolity does not enter the society, since **frivolity ruins everything** — though this seriousness is meant to stay an internal matter. When a guest unfamiliar with the group is present, members should show nothing of their inner intent and speak only of ordinary matters, matching the guest rather than displaying seriousness to him.

### Equality Among the Friends

Rabash calls for **equality** among the friends who unite, so that each can be annulled before the other — regardless of age or seniority in the group. Each person should come to know himself relative to the others by seeing the friend's merits, not his faults. A person who believes he stands even slightly above his friends can no longer truly unite with them.

## Part 2 — Rav's Practical Advice on the Article (Recorded 2003 Lesson)

### Two Kinds of Seriousness

Rav distinguishes an early stage from a more advanced one:

- **Beginning stage:** seriousness must be shown outwardly — not as a somber or self-important face, but because the person genuinely carries the goal and wants the group to be impressed by it, to raise the general "uplifted spirit" of the society.
- **Higher stage:** as the goal becomes second nature — like eating or drinking, no longer costing extra effort — the outward expression can loosen. A person can appear to joke and tell stories while an unshakeable internal seriousness continues underneath, invisible to others. Rav notes this was true of Rabash himself: it was impossible to read from the outside what he was actually thinking.

**Why the difference matters:** confusing the two — trying to loosen externally before the internal foundation is actually built — leaves nothing solid underneath, and a person simply drifts "from one feeling to another."

### Frivolity Is the Real Danger

> Rav: there is "nothing more simple, but also nothing more dangerous," than one friend causing frivolity in another. If frivolity enters the society, "you have to remove the friend and remove that inclination" — it cannot be allowed to remain, under any circumstances.

Frivolity here means anything that does not advance the group toward the goal, or that fails to make the goal more exalted in the members' eyes — not "seriousness" in the sense of a stiff or joyless demeanor.

### Equality Is Defined by the Goal, Not by Level

Rav explains that equality among friends has nothing to do with rank, seniority, or attainment — one can be young or old, veteran or novice. Equality is measured entirely against the shared goal: like passengers on a ship, everyone is necessary for the vessel to sail, and each member's care must extend to the general mood and spirit of the whole group, not just those closest to their own level.

### Only From the Sources

Rav is emphatic that the "reforming light" and "surrounding light" can only be drawn from the actual sources — Baal HaSulam, Rabash, the Ari, Rashbi (Rabbi Shimon) — never from outside material, personal writings, or ideas invented along the way. Study of the *Ten Sefirot* itself is described as irreplaceable: "such surrounding lights you won't be able to take from anywhere else."

### The Intention Defines What the Study Draws

The goal of study is not to accumulate wisdom about the text, but to draw a force that raises the student toward the source of the light. Even reading only a single sentence of a page, with the right longing, draws more than reading a thousand pages without it: "the intention is the force that operates" — without it, a person "will only have wisdom and remain a nation," but will not draw a single measure of light.

## Part 3 — Growing (Not Killing) the Desire to Receive

### Why the Desire to Receive Cannot Simply Be Cancelled

A participant asked how to cancel or annul the desire to receive. Rav's answer: it cannot be cancelled — "if I cancel the desire to receive, I won't move, I'll die." The desire to receive itself is not bad; what is bad is using it to calculate benefit for oneself instead of for the Creator. The task is not to destroy the desire but to **redirect it toward the Creator** — to grow it and aim it, so that the Creator becomes the greatest source of pleasure, greater than all the pleasures the world offers (food, friendship, sport, even the pleasure of studying wisdom itself).

### The Society's Role: Convincing the Person

Since a person cannot make the Creator more important to himself alone, the society exists to "convince" the individual — through the collective longing of the group — that the Creator is worth more than anything else the world offers. The world constantly conceals the Creator; a person's own point in the heart gives only limited revelation, so the additional "upliftment" needed can only be drawn from the points in the hearts of the friends.

### Pulling the Rope: Preparation and Drawing the Light

Rav uses the image of the group pulling a rope together to lower something toward them: preparation for the lesson means connecting with the friends around one shared intention, so the group's combined desire draws the light down together. During study, remembering that the goal is not the text itself but the force it draws is what matters — a person who reads only a few lines with the right longing over a two-hour lesson has still done the essential work.

## Part 4 — Live Q&A: The Ten, Drawing Light, and the Community

### Being "Head" of the Ten

Asked how members can be simultaneously equal and yet have gradations like "head of the ten," Rav explains the paradox is resolved through rotation and mutual consent: a person should want to become head of the ten, but the ten must also accept and make him the head that week. Someone unready can honestly say so — that is fine for now, and may change in future rounds — since the exercise of leading is voluntary, not coerced.

### Speaking About Kabbalah Outside the Group

Asked whether staying quiet in outside society conflicts with dissemination, Rav clarifies: it's not about staying silent, but recognizing that "there are few who know how to speak, and fewer still who speak when someone actually wants to listen." When someone shows genuine interest, speaking is appropriate; otherwise, sharing a book or video is the better path.

### Forms of Annulment

Asked what it means to "annul oneself before the friends," Rav lists concrete, ordinary forms: giving up time and money to be present, annulling sleep to attend, and the very act of listening — making the friend "greater" than oneself in order to actually hear their advice. Annulment is not abstract; it appears in the simplest daily choices.

### Balancing Warmth and Seriousness in the Ten

Asked how to keep a group serious without extinguishing its warmth, Rav answers that a gathering with food, laughter, and workshop banter is fine — what matters is that the group constantly checks whether the gathering is contributing to spiritual growth, or simply serving enjoyment for its own sake (which is fine too, just without spiritual benefit). Veteran, established tens can afford more looseness; newer ones may need to maintain more outward seriousness until their internal foundation is built.

### Drawing the Light: Two Means

Asked to clarify what "drawing the light" concretely means, Rav names two distinct channels: (1) study of the sources that describe the shattering and its correction — reading such texts awakens the light even without felt understanding; and (2) the ongoing connection work among the friends carried out *outside* the lesson itself, throughout the rest of the day.

### Why Live Lessons Matter

Asked why the group insists on watching lessons together live rather than each person watching a recording independently, Rav points to the value of shared, simultaneous presence itself — friends as far away as Vladivostok stay awake specifically to be together with the group in real time, rather than watching alone at a more convenient hour.

### Measuring the Growing Gap

Asked how to know the light is actually working, Rav gives two markers to track: the growing sense of the Creator's greatness pulling a person forward, and the growing recognition of the evil within that blocks that advancement. The widening gap between these two is itself evidence that the light is doing its work.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Self-love cannot be corrected alone.** Because a person has no natural fuel beyond self-benefit, a society is required to build a force strong enough to work against the individual's own nature.
2. **Seriousness is internal before it is external.** Early on it must be shown outwardly to strengthen the group's shared spirit; at a more mature stage, it becomes internalized and no longer needs outward display.
3. **Frivolity is treated as the single greatest danger to a group.** Anything that fails to elevate the shared goal in the members' eyes counts as frivolity, and it must be removed the moment it appears.
4. **Equality among friends is defined entirely by the shared goal**, never by seniority, age, or spiritual attainment — like passengers who are all equally necessary to sail a ship.
5. **The reforming/surrounding light can only be drawn from the authentic sources** — Baal HaSulam, Rabash, the Ari, Rashbi — never from outside material or personal invention.
6. **The desire to receive should be grown and redirected, not destroyed.** The goal is to make the Creator the greatest source of pleasure, above every worldly pleasure, using the society to help make this redirection possible.
7. **Drawing the light happens through two channels together:** the intention brought to studying the sources, and the ongoing connection work carried out among friends outside the lesson itself.

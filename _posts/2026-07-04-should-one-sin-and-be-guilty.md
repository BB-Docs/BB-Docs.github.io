### Opening

The lesson opens with a strange turn of phrase in the verse "should one sin and be guilty" — why does it name the sin first and the correction second, and why "be guilty" rather than simply "corrected"? Working through this, Rabash arrives at a much larger claim: every sin, without exception, traces back to a single root — the will to receive — and at the end of correction even that root must enter holiness rather than be discarded. The second half of the lesson pushes the same idea into a live question the students press on directly: if a person's state — thief, robber, or righteous — is simply the result of the light acting on the will to receive he was given, what is actually left for a person to do, and who, if anyone, is doing it?

## Part 1 — "Should One Sin and Be Guilty": Reading the Article

### Why Sin Is Named Before Correction

The verse is read as two distinct clauses: "should one sin" — naming the transgressions committed by created beings — and "be guilty," read as "the guilt is returned to the Lord," meaning the sin is corrected. Rabash notices that the text says the sinner **returns** what he took, not that he **will return** it. Since "returns" (not "will return," the imperative) is used, the correction is meant to come **by himself** — voluntarily, not by command.

### The Root of All Sin

All sin is traced to a single source: the **will to receive**, imprinted in created beings by the Creator so that He could do good to them. After the restriction placed on receiving (to avoid the "bread of shame"), correction became necessary — to receive **in order to bestow** rather than for oneself. From this root come the sins under three categories:

- **Mandatory and optional actions** — a person aims even permitted, non-obligatory things toward bestowal.
- **Positive and negative commandments** — doing or refraining, with the intention aimed at the Creator rather than self-benefit.
- **Intention itself** — the deciding factor is not the act but the aim behind it.

### Extracting from the Creator's Domain: Thief vs. Robber

"If a person receives pleasure into his own authority, his sin is that he takes it out of the Creator's domain and into his own" — since everything, and the person himself, belongs only to the Creator's domain, and "a servant has no authority of his own." Rabash draws a distinction between two ways of extracting from another's domain:

- **Thief** — takes only when he believes he is unseen. Shame at being seen gives him the strength to stop himself.
- **Robber** — takes even when seen, because his will to receive is so strong that shame cannot restrain him.

**The difference is only the size of the will to receive**, not the act itself.

### Why the Root Must Enter Holiness

Rabash illustrates this with an allegory: if the Creator's desire to do good was meant to deliver 100 kilograms of pleasure, He had to prepare a vessel with a matching 100 kilograms of deficiency — since filling can only happen where there is lack. If some of those vessels are left "unclean," unable to receive their share, the Creator's original desire is left unfulfilled. This is why, Rabash says, the Zohar states that "the angel of death is destined to become a holy angel": at the end of correction, every part of the will to receive — even the parts that produced sin — must be corrected into bestowal, or the plan to do good would remain incomplete.

> Baal HaSulam's parable: a king wanted to send gold coins overseas to his son, but his countrymen were all thieves. So he changed the coins into pennies, which — penny by penny — would add up to the same amount. By this everything is eventually corrected.

## Part 2 — Robber, Thief, Ordinary, Righteous: A Ladder Set by the Will to Receive

### What Determines the Level

Turning to the May 20, 2002 talk on the same article, Rav Laitman lays out a scale determined entirely by two factors: the **size of a person's revealed will to receive** and the **degree to which it is corrected**.

| Level | What Determines It |
|---|---|
| **Robber** | Will to receive is fully developed; nothing — not even being seen — can stop him from taking. |
| **Thief** | Will to receive is somewhat corrected; the fear of being seen is enough to stop him. |
| **Ordinary person** | Uses his will to receive only within what others permit; kept in check by the environment. |
| **Righteous** | Will to receive is corrected enough that he begins to think about how to be good to others and bestow. |

A person cannot decide, in the moment a desire is revealed, to feel or act otherwise — "it's the will to receive that determines," and "you cannot at that moment turn to the Creator" in a different measure than what is revealed. Change happens only across states, not within one.

### Society as the "Injection"

Rav Laitman illustrates this with a personal memory: as a boy of twelve or thirteen, he learned his mother, a physician, was researching hormone injections that could make a person start or stop feeling love — purely by chemistry, with no say from the person himself. He uses this to make the point that a person's will to receive works the same way: it is given, not chosen. The only lever available is the **environment** — a person cannot manufacture a desire to change on his own, but the society (the group, "the ten") can awaken in him an additional desire, a felt deficiency that he is not yet what he could be.

### Prayer as the Gap

**Prayer**, in this reading, is the **gap (the delta)** between the desire a person already has and the additional desire for change that the environment awakens in him. This gap — not either desire alone — is what draws a **surrounding light** capable of correcting him. The greater the gap a person can capture, the greater the illumination, and the greater the shift: from robber to thief, from thief to ordinary, from ordinary to righteous — always by the same mechanism, never by will alone.

## Part 3 — Determinism and the Cascade of States

### One State at a Time

A recurring point in the workshop: a person cannot occupy two states simultaneously. He cannot steal and repent in the same instant — "the desire is one, it cannot be two." He can steal, feel remorse a minute later, and steal again a minute after that; each is a separate, total state, each one determined entirely by whatever will to receive is revealed at that moment.

### How States Are Produced

Rav Laitman describes the process as a **cascade**: a state consists of light acting on a desire; that action produces the *next* desire, on which the light acts again, and so on. "It is cause and effect that work here in a deterministic manner. The desire itself cannot beget anything" — only light acting on desire produces change. The light "reforms" not merely by delivering pleasure but by carrying "the quality of the giver" into the desire, so that the desire itself begins to feel — and want to resemble — the one who gives it.

### The Student's Challenge: Isn't Everything Passive?

A student presses the obvious question: if a thought to steal, and the later thought to repent, both come from the same source, where is any scrutiny — or blame — left for the person at all? Rav Laitman's answer does not deny the premise: yes, both come from the Creator. The scrutiny is not over the acts themselves but over **how a person relates to what he is given** — whether he affirms "there is none else besides Him" in both the difficulty and the change that follows it.

## Part 4 — "Who Am I?" — A Self Without a Screen

### The Baby Analogy

Pushed further — if even repentance and the desire to bestow come from the Creator, what independent "self" is left to take credit or blame? Rav Laitman answers with an analogy: a newborn is not treated as an independent moral agent, only as a body being cared for. **A person, before acquiring a screen, is in the same position** — everything that happens in him is simply "the operation of the light," and there is not yet a self positioned to ask the question.

### The Screen as the Beginning of Independence

A **screen** is what allows a person to separate himself from the Creator enough to have something of his own — a first, limited independence given specifically so a person can *want to resemble* the Creator, not to genuinely oppose Him. Until a screen exists, "you don't even have an eye" — nothing to scrutinize with.

### The Instruction Given for Now

Without understanding, feeling, or intellect to draw on, a person's actual work at this stage is simply this: invest in the society, in the hope that it will awaken in him a change of desire in return. As Rav Laitman puts it, quoting the general principle: **"whatever is in your hand and power to do, that do"** — like a child being taught to walk, making the effort without yet grasping what is happening.

## Part 5 — Still, Vegetative, Animate, Speaking: Degrees Against the Host

Drawing on an article from **Shamati** on the four discernments — still, vegetative, animate, speaking — Rav Laitman maps them onto degrees of independence from "the host" (the Creator), each level marked by how far it can act against the nature the host built into it:

| Degree | Authority of Its Own? | Bound to the Environment? | Feels Others? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Still** | None — fulfills only the host's will | Fully bound | No |
| **Vegetative** | Some — can act slightly against the collective norm | Bound; no plant acts against all others; one life form for all vegetation | No |
| **Animate** | Yes — a distinct quality/feeling of its own, can act to bestow | Not enslaved to environment; has its own life | No — no sense of others |
| **Speaking** | Yes, and works against the host by nature | Not enslaved to environment | Yes — shares the sorrow and comfort of the public; draws from past and future, not only the present |

The higher the degree, the more a person appears to act **against** the nature the Creator built into him — self-love — yet this apparent opposition is itself given "so that a person will want to resemble" the Creator. Independence is not a rebellion against the Creator's purpose but the very mechanism by which the purpose (resemblance to Him) can be reached.

## Part 6 — Closing Workshop: What the Group Took From It

In the closing round, students named several takeaways: that the lesson, though delivered over twenty years earlier, still contains more than can be summarized simply; that the still-vegetative-animate-speaking framework had come up before and remains difficult to fully absorb; that a person occupies only one state at a time — he can be a thief, then sorry for it, then a thief again, each one total in itself; and that all of this depends on society, since "the society will give me an additional desire" the way an outside "injection" would.

Several tied this back to practice inside **the ten**: every action performed with a friend, every exercise, every scrutiny of "why did I do this, where do I want this to lead" is itself part of the laboratory the group provides. As one participant put it, the daily work is simply to do "whatever is in your hand to do" — like a baby — trusting that understanding will be revealed afterward, not before.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Every sin, without exception, traces back to one root: the will to receive.** The distinction between thief and robber is only the degree to which that will is corrected, not a difference in kind.
2. **At the end of correction, even the root that produced sin must enter holiness** — otherwise the Creator's original plan to do good remains unfulfilled. Nothing is discarded; everything is eventually corrected.
3. **A person cannot change his state by sheer will in the moment.** Whatever will to receive is revealed determines the act; change happens only through the intervention of the environment and the light, never through self-command alone.
4. **The environment functions like an "injection"** — it cannot be self-administered. Society awakens an additional desire in a person that he could not manufacture on his own.
5. **Prayer is the gap between one's current desire and the desire the environment awakens** — this gap, not either desire in isolation, is what draws the correcting light.
6. **States cascade deterministically: light acts on desire, producing the next desire.** A person cannot occupy two states — such as sinning and repenting — at once.
7. **Before a screen is acquired, there is no independent "self" to blame or credit** — a person's task is only to invest effort in the group, like a baby learning to walk, trusting the light to do the rest.
8. **The four degrees — still, vegetative, animate, speaking — mark increasing (and intended) independence from the Creator's built-in nature,** culminating in the speaking degree's capacity to feel and care for others.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Will to Receive** | The root desire imprinted in all created beings; the source of every sin. Its correction — receiving in order to bestow — is the entire aim of the Work. |
| **Thief vs. Robber** | Two degrees of the uncorrected will to receive. A thief is stopped by the shame of being seen; a robber's will to receive is too strong to be stopped even when seen. |
| **In Order to Bestow / In Order to Receive** | The corrected intention (bestowal) versus the natural, uncorrected intention (self-benefit) behind any action. |
| **Bread of Shame** | The discomfort of receiving without earning; the reason a restriction on simple reception was put in place, requiring correction toward bestowal. |
| **Klipot / Sitra Achra** | "Shells" / "the other side" — the uncorrected discernments of the will to receive that cannot yet be corrected before the end of correction. |
| **Angel of Death Becomes a Holy Angel** | Zohar's image for the end of correction: even the force behind sin and death is ultimately corrected into holiness. |
| **Prayer (in the Work)** | The felt gap (delta) between one's own desire and the additional desire for change awakened by the environment; this gap draws the correcting light. |
| **Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif)** | The light drawn in proportion to the gap a person captures; it corrects the will to receive to the extent it is drawn. |
| **Cascading States** | The deterministic process by which light acting on a desire produces the next desire, which the light then acts on again, and so on. |
| **Screen (Masach)** | What allows a person to separate himself from the Creator enough to possess a measure of independence — the beginning of a "self" capable of scrutiny. |
| **Still / Vegetative / Animate / Speaking** | Four ascending discernments of independence from the Creator's built-in nature, from Shamati; the speaking degree alone feels and cares for others. |
| **Shamati** | A collection of Rabash's notes recording teachings of Baal HaSulam, referenced here for the four-discernment framework. |
| **Repentance from Fear vs. from Love** | Fear-based repentance still leaves sins as "mistakes"; repentance from love transforms sins retroactively into merits. |

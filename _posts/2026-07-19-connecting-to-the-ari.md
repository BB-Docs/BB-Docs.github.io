### Opening

This is another sitting for the Ari's memorial day, built around excerpts selected from the Ari's own writings and from Baal HaSulam's tributes to him. The live question running underneath the reading is simple: what does it actually mean to "connect" to a soul like the Ari's, and what exactly did the Ari do that no one before or after him could do? Rav Laitman's framing and the Q&A that follows the readings work through why this particular soul was singular, how the Ari's revelation and the Baal Shem Tov's dissemination were two different, necessary jobs, and why the whole point of studying either of them collapses into one thing: the connection between people.

## Part 1 — Why This Soul, Why This Day

### The Long Descent of the Light

Rav Laitman opened by tracing how the upper light comes from the Creator, cascading through concealments and "garments" until it can reach the created being — who must be the opposite of the Creator in order to discover Him fully, while still carrying a spark of connection to Him. He described how the will to receive, driven by that spark, develops the way a baby's need for its caregiver develops — first as instinct, then as something aware, emotional, and intellectual — until, in some people, it becomes an explicit demand to know "where are we from, why are we here, what for."

> "Those people who have inside of them such a lack, such a soul, are the ones that later on tell us and explain to us whom we are connected to and who we truly are and who is managing us."

### The Ari's Place in That Line

Of the many such people across history, Rav Laitman named Rav Yitzchak Luria Ashkenazi — the Ari — as the most important, describing him as a soul with no equal in the entire human species, one that "worked towards correction to an extent that no one else was able to reach." Today, he said, is the memorial day of the Ari's passing, and studying him is itself the way to draw close to that soul:

> "We need to remember him and to connect with him by studying. Because by this we also draw upon ourselves an opportunity to connect with that very special soul that there was none other like it in the entire human species."

## Part 2 — Questions Raised Between the Readings

### How Do We Actually Connect to the Ari's Soul?

Following the reading of the first excerpt (the opening of the Ari's *Tree of Life*), the discussion turned to how a person today makes contact with a soul from another century. The answer given kept it deliberately unmystical:

> "This is also something that he had passed on to his students, and he demanded it from them that they will be connected... if we want to connect to the Ari, it means that we have to connect through the same rules of the wisdom of Kabbalah that are the same for everyone — again, a friend as yourself. We eventually have to keep it to the fullest."

### Who Did the Ari Actually Write For?

Asked who the Ari's writing was really meant for, given that only five or six students ever studied with him directly, Rav Laitman was direct:

> "The Ari wrote his things for everyone... When a Kabbalist writes and he opens up whatever he wrote, it's for everyone... The purpose of humanity... is equal for everyone. For the white, the black, the yellow, the red — it doesn't matter what race, nothing really matters. So long as the person would want to know what does he exist for, who is managing him, and to whom should he come closer."

### Was His Small Group the Source of His Revelation?

A follow-up question asked whether the handful of students around the Ari served as some kind of vessel for what he revealed. Rav Laitman rejected this:

> "No, I don't think that he drew it thanks to his group. He was so high, he took from them a certain deficiency, nevertheless, by being connected with them. But it's not that the group somehow took part in his writing. No — it was such a soul that was truly disconnected from everyone."

### What Should We Actually Take From His Exalted Height?

Asked what practical lesson follows from a soul that high and that singular, the answer turned it back toward ordinary study:

> "We should be impressed, inspired by the Ari, by what he did, and to learn something from him... The most important thing we need to learn from him is the general system. And when we study that, we already become connected to those entrances, the gates that bring us the upper light that is influencing us as surrounding light."

## Part 3 — Two Roles: The Ari Revealed, the Baal Shem Tov Disseminated

### Why the Ari's Innovation Could Land At All

Asked what was genuinely new about the Ari's revelation compared to what came before him, Rav Laitman tied it to the growth of the human ego across generations:

> "The novelty, what was new, is that humanity keeps growing in its ego, and according to the degree of the ego it becomes more and more in opposition to the upper light, the Creator... They reach such a state where they already begin to feel — even though the light is very small — that, nevertheless, specifically the force of the darkness helps, and it emphasizes the light out of it."

He added that this is not a credit to the generation's merit but to its growing demand: "It's not because we are so worthy of it, but rather out of our ego that is growing and demanding it." He also noted that a Kabbalist's revelation works on all of humanity even when almost no one understands the words directly, because it acts as **surrounding light** — "the light that we are connected with... does pass through us to them, because we're all connected in a single body called Adam."

### Revealer and Disseminator

Rav Laitman then drew the now-familiar distinction between the Ari's role and that of the Baal Shem Tov roughly two centuries later:

> "One of them acted in order to reveal — to reveal scientifically, let's say — what is happening throughout the whole of reality, the system of reality, the forces that work in reality, the Creator's attitude to the created beings, systematically. This is what the Ari did. And the Baal Shem Tov clothed this in all kinds of stories, in forms by which he was able to bring the words of the Ari closer to the common people... The Ari revealed, and the Baal Shem Tov disseminated."

He noted that the Baal Shem Tov himself wrote almost nothing directly — his contribution was entirely in publicizing the method to ordinary people, against real opposition, and that this legacy persists even among people today who "don't really study the method of the Ari" but who still carry, at least in part, the pull toward "love your friend as yourself."

## Part 4 — Individual Work, Group Work, and Corporeal Life

### No Kabbalist Worked Alone

Asked whether one of the excerpts implied that individual work in front of the Creator is enough on its own, Rav Laitman closed that door firmly:

> "We have no Kabbalists that did not create a group around him — not in order to teach them, that group, but so as together with them, through the connection with them, to advance together, because the spiritual advancement can be done only in a group."

### Good Connection as the Actual Subject Matter

The closing exchange widened this into a claim about corporeal life generally: that all spiritual life, and increasingly all corporeal success as well, depends on the quality of connection between people.

> "We are the last generation. We will feel more and more to what extent we depend on each other. And without achieving a good connection between us, we will not succeed in anything in this corporeal life also... The correct, good connection between people — it has to be the main study of ours, and also of the whole of humanity following us."

Rav Laitman was careful to frame this as prior to any political or ideological division: "It's not an issue of left or right, or all kinds of different political parties or movements — there is only one thing that the upper nature... is demanding of us: to be connected in a good way, reciprocally, to each other."

### What Corporeal Work Is Actually For

Asked directly what the purpose of spiritual work in the corporeal world is, the answer connected individual effort to the upper system itself:

> "The corporeal world was given to us because here, through our own forces, by ourselves, we can determine good connections, good relations of connection between us... The more we are in equivalence of form with the general, overall nature, to that extent we can influence it, and that way, from the lower one, we are able to influence the upper one — this is called raising man."

He closed by describing this as a two-directional meeting: either "the upper world will truly descend down to our world," or, put the other way, "our world rises up to the degree of the upper world."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. Connecting to a great soul like the Ari's happens through the same universal rule as any spiritual connection — "through the ten" and through love of a friend — not through any special technique tied to his memory specifically.

2. The Ari's writing was addressed to everyone, not to the handful of students who actually studied with him; a Kabbalist's revelation is understood as inherently universal in scope.

3. The Ari's group did not generate his revelation — his soul is described as singular and disconnected from everyone, though he still drew something from being connected to his students.

4. The Ari's revelation and the Baal Shem Tov's dissemination were two distinct, necessary functions: one uncovered the system of reality "scientifically," the other clothed it in accessible stories. Both were required for the teaching to reach ordinary people.

5. A Kabbalist's revelation affects all of humanity as surrounding light even when almost no one understands it directly, because everyone is connected within a single collective soul (Adam).

6. No Kabbalist has ever worked purely alone — spiritual advancement is described as achievable only through a group, never through solitary effort in front of the Creator.

7. The lesson's closing claim widens outward: good connection between people is presented as the determining factor not only for spiritual attainment but for success in corporeal life generally, prior to and independent of any political framing.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Ari (Rav Yitzchak Luria Ashkenazi) | 16th-century Kabbalist, author of *Etz Chaim* ("Tree of Life"); the soul this lesson's memorial day commemorates. |
| Baal HaSulam | Rabbi Yehuda Ashlag, author of the Sulam commentary on the Zohar; quoted throughout this lesson's excerpts praising the Ari. |
| Baal Shem Tov | 18th-century founder of the Hasidic movement; disseminated the Ari's revealed system in accessible story form rather than adding new revelation of his own. |
| RAMAK | Rabbi Moshe Cordovero, a major Kabbalist prior to the Ari, whose disciples are described in the excerpts as standing before the Ari "as disciples before the Rav." |
| Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif) | The light of a Kabbalist's revelation that reaches and affects all of humanity even when almost no one understands the words directly, since everyone is connected within a single collective soul. |
| Adam (HaRishon) | The single collective soul containing all souls; the reason a revelation given to a handful of students still passes to everyone. |
| Equivalence of Form | The alignment of a person's giving with the Creator's; named in this lesson as the mechanism by which the lower one can influence the upper one ("raising man"). |
| Sefer HaBrit HaShalem | "The Book of the Complete Covenant" — a source cited in one of the lesson's excerpts on the opening of the gates of wisdom in this era. |
| Point in the Heart / The Ten | The group ("the ten") named as the concrete, practical vehicle through which connection to a great soul, and to the Creator, is actually built. |

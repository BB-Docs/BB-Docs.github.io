### Opening

The article's question is blunt: why should the right line outweigh the left, when the work is supposed to need both? Rav Laitman's answer runs through the whole session — a person has to already be standing on the right (wholeness, the qualities of the Creator) before the left (deficiency, the qualities of the created being) can safely be introduced at all, because deficiency without a prior foothold in wholeness just causes a person to fall rather than advance. The Q&A that follows tests this claim against hard cases: good deeds done without the goal in mind, global aid and dissemination, what actually changes when a person crosses the barrier, and where free choice can be found at all before that point.

## Part 1 — Three Lines, and Why the Right Must Come First

### Right, Left, and a Middle That Belongs to No One

Rav Laitman opened by placing the right and left lines against nature itself:

> "The middle line doesn't belong to anything that exists in nature. In nature we have the qualities of the Creator and the qualities of the created being — only two. The third is in how the created being makes himself in the image of the Creator."

The right line carries the qualities of the Creator — bestowal, Hesed, wholeness. The left carries the qualities of the created being — reception, deficiency. The middle line is not a natural given; it is built entirely by a person's own work of combining the other two.

### A Person Cannot Live on Deficiency Alone

Before any of this can function, Rav Laitman insisted a person needs a starting foothold in wholeness, not lack:

> "A person cannot live in a deficiency, because to begin with, he exists in his will to receive, and his will to receive must feel wholeness."

Before the barrier, this wholeness is simply health, functioning, being intact. Beyond the barrier, wholeness becomes the feeling of Hesed — delight in mercy, the state of having some grip on bestowal.

### The Point in the Heart Versus the Rest of the Heart

Asked how wholeness and deficiency can coexist in one person, Rav Laitman located both inside the same heart, not as sequential states but as two simultaneous parts:

> "The person himself already includes the point in the heart, where the qualities of the Creator exist — there it is wholeness. And the rest of the heart, besides the point in the heart, is the left; it's the deficiencies."

He connected this to the verse "you shall be whole (Tamim) before the Lord your God":

> "Tamim in Hebrew doesn't mean innocent, like a baby. It means whole, complete. It's by making his deficiencies also work within wholeness."

## Part 2 — Deficiency Must Be Measured, Not Avoided

### Revealed in the Right Amount

Deficiency isn't simply suppressed or indulged — it has to be calibrated:

> "How much deficiency can be revealed to me, so that through it I will advance rather than fall? This has to be measured — weighed."

### Awakened on Purpose

Rav Laitman described deliberately awakening a deficiency in order to see one's own state and build past it:

> "We awaken the deficiency within us artificially, in order to see our state and build on it a higher degree — a more advanced state, new scrutiny, new ideas, new corrections. This is how it is."

The immediate result of doing this is a felt mixture of "discomfort and helplessness" (the left) — but it is specifically from that state that a person "later reaches advancement on the right," since the left lets a person see, bit by bit, how to attach the deficiency to the right rather than be undone by it.

### Breaking a False Wholeness

He added a caution about complacency: for most people, the "wholeness" they settle into is not real adhesion with the Creator but a comfortable descent into what he called beastliness — leaning on one's current, unexamined state. Breaking that false wholeness on purpose, in a controlled way, is itself part of the work, not a failure of it.

## Part 3 — Right, Trouble, and Prayer: The Mechanism of the Three Lines

Rav Laitman laid out a working sequence rather than a static description:

> "First we equip ourselves with the right — with wholeness, with the recognition of the goal. Later, according to the measure of our connection and grip on the goal, we awaken the trouble, as he calls it — which means our state is truly not good, the opposite of the goal, and we need to feel in it trouble. Trouble means to feel the narrow pressure of being in a state which is incomplete. By scrutinizing it, a feeling is formed in the heart which we call a prayer... and through this trouble, through the prayer, we reach the right that we had before."

He was careful to note that the feeling of prayer itself is not something a person invents directly — it forms "from the correction of the right, when I hold onto the goal, and from the revelation of the left in a controlled manner, so it won't burden me too much." When this happens correctly, it awakens the middle line precisely, "in such a way that the upper one will give me a correction and an ascent." This, he said, is what the three lines actually mean in the work of the lower one toward the Upper One.

## Part 4 — Why Holding the Goal Must Come Before Any Action

A participant pressed the obvious objection: if wholeness is already "the right," why would a person need to leave it for lack at all?

### Holding the Goal First

Rav Laitman's answer kept coming back to a single requirement — the goal has to be held first, before any action, thought, or plan:

> "We have to keep our eye on the final goal: what do I want to reach, whom do I want to belong to, what do I want to see myself as at the end... This is called the right — holding onto the qualities of the Creator, His stature, His qualities. After I have a grip on the right, I'm permitted to turn and perform any act, any thought, whatever it is. Otherwise my action, my thought won't be purposeful — it will be just a beast inside this world."

Without the goal held first, he said, whatever comes after is "already beastly," no matter how good the deeds look:

> "If I do not first take the goal, then whatever I do later doesn't matter — it is already beastly, even if I do very good deeds. But if it's not connected with the goal, no good result will emerge from that, because ultimately the general mechanism of the Creator's Providence over our world will corrupt my action."

### The Baal HaSulam / Russia Example

Asked what Baal HaSulam writes about Russia, Rav Laitman used it as a case study in exactly this failure:

> "To begin with, a few people wanted to be good, truly — and immediately it turned into a dictatorship. In the same article about the last generation, he writes about Russian communism, that there is nothing worse than it, and in almost the same words he describes the last generation. So what's the difference between them? We wanted to be good for ourselves, or we wanted to be good because we're connected to the Creator. If it is just for others, it's a shell. If it is in order to get closer to the Creator, it's Kedusha, holiness."

The deed can look identical from outside; what separates a shell (Klipa) from holiness (Kedusha) is only the goal behind it, never the visible action itself.

## Part 5 — Kedusha and Klipa: Same Deed, Different Intention

### The World of Falsehood

Rav Laitman described good deeds done without the goal as producing what he called a world of falsehood:

> "We make good deeds, but the intention is hidden — hidden from them as well as from others — and they think that just as the actions are good, the inner intention is also good. This is called the world of falsehood."

### Stealing Ten Shekels and Saving the Planet Are the Same Shell

He pushed the point further with a deliberately provocative comparison — a petty theft and a global humanitarian cause are, internally, the same kind of act if neither is connected to the goal:

> "Stealing ten shekels, or being concerned about animal rights, or people's rights — it doesn't matter, it's the same thing. The problem is that when he steals ten shekels, his thought is revealed, so it's just a small shell. It's not even a shell, because a shell is what is nicely covered. The will to receive, which is covered with good deeds, with good externality — this is called a shell."

He warned that this is precisely how the shell "shines" and draws a person in: it delivers real pleasure, the same kind of pleasure a person could get from holiness, which is what makes bleeding-heart causes so convincing from the inside even when they are, in his account, disconnected from the actual goal.

### Society's Standard Is Not the Goal's Standard

Asked whether this makes ordinary human decency worthless, Rav Laitman drew a firm line between the two measures:

> "There cannot be a human society where if you steal ten shekels, it's good — this is not acceptable in any society. But whatever is customary is acceptable in humanity. In humanity, it's customary that a good attitude toward each other — that's holiness, that's good. We don't measure according to the goal; we measure according to the deed. And the more the deed is close to bestowal, we call it a good deed. But in truth, it causes more harm."

The paradox he leaves standing: ordinary kindness remains genuinely good and necessary by human standards, while still, by the standard of the goal, potentially reinforcing a disconnected shell if the intention behind it never turns toward the Creator.

## Part 6 — Necessity, the Small Group, and Dissemination

### Necessity Is Neither Praised Nor Denounced

On material needs — food, basic survival — Rav Laitman drew a distinction between meeting necessity and getting entangled in it:

> "We need to distribute to everyone, let's say a thousand calories a day — you don't need to make any intentions with it, let the beast lead. That's it. But after you've distributed to them, it's not on you to give more to the person; let him develop the way he is."

Even necessity, though, is meant to ultimately point toward adhesion with the Creator — otherwise, in the long run, it too "will be revealed as bad and will not bring any benefit," since (he noted) the world already produces enough food for far more people than currently exist; the problem is distribution, not scarcity, and mass redistribution schemes divorced from the goal tend to produce new harms rather than solve the old one.

### A Small Group's Actual Job

Asked about the group's own role relative to the whole world, Rav Laitman was explicit that direct global engagement is not the group's task:

> "We ourselves are a small group. But in principle, if we're talking about the global work, we should be in it... The only thing we can do is awaken the Surrounding Light — that it will awaken more and more in the whole of humanity, and it will affect these relationships."

### Two Teachings That Only Look Contradictory

He reconciled two apparently opposite instructions found at the end of the Introduction to the Book of Zohar — that in the last generation a person must "go and bestow to the whole world," and elsewhere, that the group's work is to "sit and study and bestow... not to distribute food, but to distribute the wisdom of Kabbalah":

> "We shouldn't see these things as contradictory, opposite each other, but rather as individual work and the work of the public. The general work should truly be like that — I teach ordinary people what they need to do, and they, through their own mutual bestowal to the whole world, awaken Surrounding Light upon themselves."

The individual's task is inner correction; the group's public task is dissemination that awakens the Surrounding Light in others — the two are not competing projects but two levels of the same one.

## Part 7 — Crossing the Barrier and Recognizing Concealment

### Restriction, Not Habit

Asked what actually changes when a person crosses the Machsom, Rav Laitman defined it as a change in what a person is permitted to do with the vessels of reception, not a change in daily behavior:

> "A person who crosses the barrier — it is considered that he has the power to restrict his vessels of reception and bring them into action only according to how he can work with them above reason. Above reason means to take upon himself the governance of the Upper One with a whole heart."

Pressed on whether this means changing habits, he was direct:

> "It doesn't mean to change one's habits... Your attitude toward people. It depends on which people — certainly people who are in the spiritual process. The attitude changes each time, as much as one understands and comes to know the spiritual process. Beyond the barrier, there is truly an inversion in the recognition of the path and the goal."

### Discovering Concealment Comes Before Anything Else

On how to connect to the Creator while still in concealment, Rav Laitman insisted the first move is recognizing the concealment honestly, not assuming revelation has already begun:

> "First and foremost, determine that you are truly in concealment, and not that it seems to you that you're already in revelation — because if there's revelation, there's no work."

Once that recognition is genuine, the next step isn't a separate calculation — the state itself supplies it:

> "If a person doesn't discover that he's in concealment, he has nothing to work with. When he reveals the concealment, then from that he can know what steps he can take — or he prays, meaning the feeling itself of the state he's in brings him to this inner prayer. The fact that you're asking what you need to do in concealment means that you don't feel that you're in concealment... After you discover your true state each time, you no longer have a question of what you need to do — the state itself dictates what to do."

## Part 8 — Free Choice Before the Barrier: Two Contradictory States

Asked where free choice could possibly exist before the barrier, when it can feel like only society and circumstance act on a person, Rav Laitman answered by pointing to something a person can actually observe in themselves:

> "I see, sometimes, I'm in a state where I feel the world is good, there's an upper force, there's a future, and thank God it doesn't end with me being here — there's some more life and purpose. I have this feeling — maybe it comes for a few moments, once a month, every few months, or a year. But when I'm in it, it exists. Relative to that, there are other feelings where I might be, most of the time — let's say ninety-nine percent — two opposite feelings, that there's no hope, the world has no purpose, and no one knows how to live here or why we're here at all. Do you see that these are two contradictory states? From these two states, you can start working."

He traced how the search for a stable "good state" eventually reveals its own limit and turns into something else:

> "I begin to discover gradually that my nature is made in such a way that it doesn't let me truly be happy. So I gradually go on to discover why the world is so bad for me, until I discover that no one brought me to Kabbalah but rather the search — the fact that I was searching how it would be good for me — until I come to a state where I can begin to understand that there's a purpose to this revelation of evil: to lead me to the real good. But to this real good I can come only in a very special way, on a very narrow path, and in a not-so-simple method."

He closed the point with what amounts to the session's central claim about suffering itself:

> "There is no such thing as bad in the world. The 'bad' is that you're being aimed more correctly toward the good — this deviation, this guidance, this correction of your direction, this push you're being given — that's what you feel as bad, no more than that... Meaning, without the revelation of evil, you wouldn't be able to be connected to the goal and advance."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. The right line (wholeness, the qualities of the Creator) must be established before the left line (deficiency, the qualities of the created being) is introduced — deficiency without a prior foothold in wholeness causes a person to fall rather than advance.

2. Wholeness and deficiency coexist in one person at once: the point in the heart carries the Creator's qualities (the right), and the rest of the heart carries deficiency (the left) — they are not sequential stages but simultaneous parts of the same subject.

3. Deficiency must be measured and even deliberately awakened in a controlled way, since it is specifically through the discomfort of the left that a person learns to attach deficiency to the right and advance.

4. The three lines function as a working sequence: equip with the right (the goal), awaken trouble (deficiency felt as pressure), let it form into prayer on the middle line — this is what draws correction and ascent from the Upper One.

5. The goal must be held before any action or it becomes "beastly" — the same external deed (a small theft, a global humanitarian cause) is the identical shell internally if it isn't connected to the goal; only the intention behind an act, not its visible form, separates Kedusha from Klipa.

6. Ordinary human decency remains genuinely good and necessary by society's own standard, even while, by the standard of the goal, it can still reinforce a disconnected "shell" if the underlying intention never turns toward the Creator.

7. Meeting basic material necessity requires no spiritual intention of its own ("let the beast lead"), but even necessity must ultimately point toward adhesion with the Creator, or in the long run it produces new harm rather than resolving the old one.

8. The group's actual task in the world is not direct global engagement but awakening the Surrounding Light through study and mutual bestowal — reconciling the seemingly opposed instructions to "bestow to the whole world" and to "distribute the wisdom of Kabbalah, not food."

9. Crossing the barrier means gaining the power to restrict the vessels of reception and act above reason — a change in attitude toward people in the spiritual process, not a change in ordinary habits.

10. Recognizing that one is in concealment — rather than assuming revelation has already arrived — must come first; once genuinely recognized, a person's true state itself dictates the next step, rather than leaving a separate question of "what to do."

11. Free choice before the barrier is found in noticing two contradictory felt states (rare clarity about purpose versus prevailing confusion or hopelessness) and beginning to work with that contradiction — and suffering itself is reframed as correction rather than genuine harm: "there is no such thing as bad in the world."

| Left Line | The line of deficiency and reception — carries the qualities of the created being; without a prior foothold in the right, it causes a fall rather than advancement. |
| Middle Line | Belongs to nothing that exists in nature; built entirely by a person's own work of combining right and left, "in the image of the Creator." |
| Tamim | Hebrew for "whole/complete" (not "innocent, like a baby") — the state of making even one's deficiencies work within an overall wholeness. |
| Machsom (Barrier) | The point at which a person gains the power to restrict the vessels of reception and act above reason, under the governance of the Upper One. |

| Trouble / Prayer | The felt pressure of an incomplete state, scrutinized until it forms into a prayer/demand on the middle line — the mechanism that draws correction from above. |

### Opening

After the group reads the article together, Rav Laitman turns to a separately recorded lesson from February 11, 2002. He opens with the article's own claim — that the Creator "created the evil inclination" and "created the Torah as a spice" to correct it — and asks what that actually means in practice: what is Torah in this world, and what, concretely, does a person do to draw the force that corrects him? From there the lecture and Q&A move through how correction unfolds across five worlds using the same set of desires, why the entire effort of the Work is really the act of asking rather than of doing, how ordinary corporeal actions can or cannot be tied to that goal, what a person is actually building during sleep, and finally circle back to the article's own closing question — if everything becomes revealed, what is left for faith to do?

---

## Part 1 — The Evil Inclination and the Spice of Torah

### Precision Without Comfort

Rav Laitman opens by insisting that Kabbalah requires a person to be simple and exact rather than confused and layered in interpretation — and that this bluntness is itself difficult to tolerate. He recounts telling teachers at the Mofet school, plainly, that their desire is a "beastly desire," and describes their reaction as shock and rejection.

**Root and branch:** Baal HaSulam's method names a spiritual root by whatever branch in this world corresponds to it exactly — "coupling," "kissing," "embryo," "womb" — regardless of how crude or pleasant the word sounds to us. From the Creator's perspective of wholeness, nothing is inferior to anything else; the sense of high and low, pleasant and unpleasant, belongs only to our own uncorrected perception.

> If we are disgusted by any form in our life, it is a sign that these vessels with which we see and feel this phenomenon are not corrected.

### The Spice of Torah

The evil inclination is the will to receive; the "spice" the Creator created to correct it is a force from above — **the light that reforms**. Any action that can cause this reforming light to come down is called an **action of mitzvah** (commandment). A mitzvah, in this sense, is not a ritual category but a personal correction: the reforming light corrects a specific quality in the person, gradually replacing the intention to receive with an intention to bestow. "There's nothing more — this is the whole Torah."

## Part 2 — Five Worlds, One Set of Desires, Deepening Correction

### The Same 613 Desires, Correction After Correction

Reality divides into five degrees, or worlds, from the lowest, least deserved state to the highest, most perfect one. In every world, the same 613 desires of the soul are corrected again — but at a deeper layer each time. Correcting the coarsest layer brings a person to the light of **Nefesh**; going deeper draws a greater light and a "greater Torah," bringing the light of **Ruach** in the world of Yetzira; deeper still leads through Beria, Atzilut, and Assiya, until reaching **Ein Sof**, the end of correction. All the light that completes this process, across all degrees, is called **Messiah**.

### Before All the Degrees: This World

Prior to any of these worlds is a state called "this world" — a desire that does not yet have a screen. This is where a person begins from the point in the heart, working in single or double concealment. Rav Laitman describes this stage as a genuine but strange kind of correction: a person's desire for spirituality grows at the same time that his awareness of how far he is from it also grows — both movements happening together, with the reforming light acting on him throughout.

### What Counts as an Action of Mitzvah Here

Before a person can distinguish his own 613 desires directly, every effort — studying, working in the kitchen, working on love of friends, preparing any kind of deficiency that will draw the reforming light — counts as an action of mitzvah. Once a person begins to actually perceive his desires and the connections between them, distinct forms of corrective action become distinguishable (described using terms like tefillin, tallit, the 248 positive and 365 negative commandments, or in the language of Kabbalah, Sefirot and reshimot above or below the chazeh) — but Rav Laitman is clear these are simply different names for the same underlying work: correcting each desire, one at a time.

## Part 3 — Preparation for Prayer Is Itself the Work

### The Question

A student asks whether there is a difference in attitude required when reading Rabash's articles ("the rungs of the ladder") versus studying TES (Talmud Eser Sefirot) — how should the thinking or the requests differ between the two?

### Prayer Before Prayer

Rav Laitman answers using the concept of "prayer before prayer" (from Rabbi Noam Elimelech): even the preparation for prayer is itself a request — a desire for a desire for a desire — asking the Creator to prepare the person so that he will be able to pray at all.

> What's the difference between our work and the whole world? The whole world thinks they can do something by themselves.

Against this self-reliant approach (which he associates with the drive to control everything, as in "the Americans... take control"), the entire labor of the Work is to gather up whatever difficulty, confusion, or suffering arises and consolidate it into a single, correct request — not to accomplish the goal oneself, but to change so that the Creator does it.

### Mapping the Two Kinds of Study

- **Reading the article beforehand** functions as preparation for prayer — arousing the desire and awakening the greatness of the Creator so a person is ready.
- **Studying TES itself** is the prayer proper — the actual demand for the reforming light, made continuously from the beginning of the study to its end.

Study must be done together with friends; done alone, it is "just reading wisdom, not Torah." TES specifically carries a concealed light that is closer and more direct than texts wrapped in worldly imagery (like the Gemara's language of ox, cow, and damages) — special individuals may draw from it more easily, which is precisely why the group's shared, sustained demand for that light matters so much.

## Part 4 — Any Corporeal Action Can Be Tied to the Goal — Within Limits

### Organizing the Group's Work

Asked how to organize group activities (kitchen duty, or making holy items like tefillin, tallit, or mezuzot) around the aim of adhesion, Rav Laitman affirms that every action in this world can and should carry the yearning to reach adhesion — with one condition.

> You can't say, "Now I'll eat pig with cream, so that I will have the demand as I study to adhere to the Creator." ... It's not the matter of pork ... but opposite the root and branch, you can't do it.

An action can only be tied to the spiritual goal if it is "kosher" — if it genuinely corresponds, as branch to root, to something in the spiritual structure. This is why some actions are commanded and others are not.

### Making Holy Items vs. an Ordinary Trade

Comparing someone making tefillin to a shoemaker at his trade, Rav Laitman says the surface of the action is not what matters — what matters is whether the person searches for a connection to the Creator through what he does. A person working with a coarser, more mundane will to receive (an ordinary trade) may in fact benefit more from this search, because his prayer and preparation become more urgently needed.

**The example of greatest coarseness:** the Zivug (marital union) is named as the root of all pleasure in this world, the point of greatest coarseness — offered not as a practical instruction but to illustrate how far the root-and-branch principle extends, and how difficult it is to act "for the sake of heaven" under concealment even in the most intense desires. Real ability to act for the Creator's sake, Rav Laitman notes, depends on the opening of the eyes to His greatness — until then, a person can only make the effort "to the extent one can imagine."

## Part 5 — Preparation as "Play," and Measuring Invisible Growth

### Why the Preparation Stage Feels Worthless

Rav Laitman describes the current stage — "writing on a board with chalk" — as having no worth in itself; its only value is as preparation, just as a child's reading of "nonsense" is worthless in itself but is exactly how a child grows toward adulthood. He recalls a friend who worked in game development at the Weizmann Institute, where psychologists and teachers studied how play trains a person naturally and beneficially for life. Even a trick played among friends — convincing someone something exists when it doesn't — produces a real desire in the person who is fooled; that desire, not the reality of the object, is what matters.

### Can Progress Be Felt During Preparation?

Asked whether any development can be felt during this stage, Rav Laitman answers with an image rather than a metric: a mother with a ten-year-old who has developmental issues (behaving like a one-year-old) and a two-year-old who has already surpassed him — sharp, alert, engaged with the table and its objects, while the older child sits "like a golem." The comparison suggests that real growth shows itself in the vessel's sharpness and readiness, not in the passage of time alone.

## Part 6 — What a Dream Actually Is

### The Question

A student asks what it means, from the article, that "this world is a dream."

### Departure of the Mind

Rav Laitman explains that a dream is what happens when the Mochin (the current, conscious mind) departs because a person is adhering to a higher degree. Physically lying down — head level with the feet, without the light of Chochmah — mirrors this: the mind steps back so that something can be received from the higher degree instead.

### The Nightly Process

Through the bedtime Kriyat Shema, a person hands himself to the higher partzufim, **Aba ve Ima** (father and mother), who work through the night to prepare new vessels for him. In the morning — Kriyat Shema again, the sacrifices, the Amidah — those vessels are filled with light; this filling is what constitutes "the next day," the next degree.

> It's a great effort to be in a dream, to annul oneself, to devote oneself. "I deposit my spirit in your hands."

This nightly self-annulment is not passive rest but a serious, almost strenuous act — Rav Laitman describes it as something a person should ideally "sweat through," a genuine willingness to give oneself over completely so as to be built up for the next degree.

## Part 7 — Closing Question: If Everything Is Revealed, Why Still Need Faith?

In the final exchange, a student presses on the article's last line — if revelation removes concealment entirely, what work is left for faith to do? Rav Laitman does not resolve the question in this transcript, responding only that it needs further consideration ("I don't know... we need to see").

This unresolved question echoes the article's own teaching: the Zohar's account of Jacob's vow was that even after being answered — "then the Lord shall be a God unto me" — he would still build his house on the stone he had placed as a headstone, meaning he would remain in faith even in a state of complete revelation (Gadlut), not only in smallness (Katnut).

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Anything that draws the reforming light is a mitzvah** — prayer, TES study, kitchen work, reading the article beforehand. The "spice of Torah" is this reforming force itself, not a fixed set of rituals.
2. **The same 613 desires are corrected repeatedly, at ever-deeper layers, across five worlds** — each pass requiring a stronger light (Nefesh, Ruach, and beyond), until the process completes in what is called Messiah.
3. **The entire labor of the Work is the request itself.** A person cannot correct himself by his own effort; even the desire to pray correctly must first be prayed for ("prayer before prayer").
4. **Study without a group is just wisdom, not Torah.** Real engagement with TES requires friends who together sustain the demand for the reforming light.
5. **A corporeal action can be tied to the spiritual goal only if it is a genuine "kosher" branch of a real root** — the value is in the searching for connection, not in how spiritually the action presents itself on the surface.
6. **Sleep is a serious act of self-annulment**, not passive escape — a nightly handing-over of oneself to Aba ve Ima so that new vessels can be prepared and filled with light the next morning.
7. **Growth during the preparation stage isn't directly measurable**, but real development is still visible over time — in the sharpness and readiness of the vessel, not simply in how much time has passed.

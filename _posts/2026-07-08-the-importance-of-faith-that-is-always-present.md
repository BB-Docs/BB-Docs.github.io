## How to Use This List

Terms are given in the order they appear in the article. Each entry shows the term, its plain meaning, and the sentence from the article where it appears — so you can see it in context rather than as an isolated definition.

---

| Term | Definition | As Used in the Article |
|------|------------|--------------------------|
| **The Zohar** | The foundational text of Kabbalah, structured as mystical commentary on the Torah; this article opens by quoting it. | "The Zohar asks (Vayetze, Item 75)..." |
| **Vayetze** | The Torah portion ("And He Went Out") that tells the story of Jacob's dream and vow — the biblical source this article interprets. | "The Zohar asks (Vayetze, Item 75)..." |
| **Kelim** (singular: **Kli**) | Vessels — the desires or capacities through which a person can receive delight and pleasure; must be "corrected" (aimed at bestowal) to receive without shame. | "...they do not have the Kelim [vessels] to feel any taste in it." |
| **Kedusha** | Holiness; the quality of bestowal, opposite to the Klipot. | "...a frail light of sparks of Kedusha [holiness] that fell among the Klipot." |
| **Klipot** (singular: **Klipa**) | Shells or peels; the impure, self-serving forces/desires that conceal holiness and hold only the will to receive for oneself. | "...fell among the Klipot [shells/peels]. This is all of their vitality." |
| **Torah and Mitzvot** | The commandments and good deeds prescribed by Jewish law; in the work, understood as the vehicle for drawing the corrective ("reforming") force. | "...the primary delight and pleasure are clothed in Torah and Mitzvot [commandments/good deeds]." |
| **Tzimtzum** | Restriction; the primordial act of concealment that hid the delight and pleasure in Torah and Mitzvot so a person could develop the intention to bestow rather than simply receive. | "...the correction of Tzimtzum [restriction] took place, namely the concealment on the delight and pleasure..." |
| **Bread of Shame** | The shame a person would feel receiving pleasure without earning it; avoided once a person receives only in order to please the Creator, not himself. | "...there will not be any bread of shame in it because he will not be receiving the delight and pleasure for his own benefit." |
| **Equivalence of Form** | The state in which a person's giving mirrors the Creator's giving — bestowing rather than receiving for oneself; the goal of spiritual labor. | "...the creatures must delight the Creator, too. This is called 'equivalence of form.'" |
| **Will to Receive (for Oneself)** | The innate, self-serving desire a person is born with — the raw material that must be corrected, not eliminated. | "...man is created with a will to receive pleasure for himself, called 'will to receive for himself.'" |
| **Desire to Bestow** | The new vessel (Kli) a person must acquire in place of pure self-interest — receiving or acting in order to give to the Creator. | "...he must cancel this will to receive and acquire a new Kli [vessel], called 'desire to bestow.'" |
| **Spice of Torah** | The corrective power carried by Torah study, described by the sages as the antidote created alongside the evil inclination. | "'I have created the evil inclination; I have created for it the spice of Torah.'" |
| **Tuma'a** | Impurity; the state of the Klipot, rooted in the will to receive that separates a person from the Creator. | "...we must understand the meaning of the existence of Tuma'a [impurity] and Klipot." |
| **ABYA** | Acronym for the four worlds Atzilut, Beria, Yetzira, Assiya; here referring to the "impure worlds" system in which the will to receive operates when uncorrected. | "...which He placed (the will to receive) in the system of the impure worlds ABYA." |
| **Life of Lives** | A name for the Creator as the source of all vitality; the Klipot are described as cut off from this source. | "...separates them from the Life of Lives, and they are far from Him from one end to the other." |
| **Malchut** | Literally "kingdom"; in this article, identified with the "stone" Jacob placed under his head, and with faith itself — the vessel of reception when corrected toward bestowal. | "It is known that a stone is called Malchut, and Malchut is called 'faith.'" |
| **Faith Above Reason** | Placing trust in the Creator's guidance above and prior to one's own logical understanding — described through Jacob placing the stone (understanding) under his head (faith). | "This is called 'faith above reason.'" |
| **Matzeva / Matzav** | Matzeva ("headstone") derives from Matzav ("state") — Jacob's headstone symbolizes the state he is building: a life structured on faith above reason. | "Matzeva [headstone] comes from the word Matzav [state]..." |
| **Shechina** | The Divine Presence; "greeting the Shechina" describes the moment a person is rewarded with revelation after having built on faith. | "...if he is rewarded with greeting the Shechina [Divinity]..." |
| **Katnut** | Smallness or "infancy" — a state of diminished spiritual perception, contrasted with Gadlut. | "It follows that faith applies both in Katnut [infancy/smallness] and in Gadlut [adulthood/greatness]." |
| **Gadlut** | Greatness or "adulthood" — a state of expanded spiritual perception and revelation; the article's point is that faith is still required even here. | "...for the vow was that even in Gadlut he would not move from faith." |
| **This World Is Like a Dream** | The article's central metaphor: just as a dream's meaning depends on how it is interpreted, a person's actions in this world "solve" (determine the outcome of) the Creator's promise of good. | "This world is like a dream. That is, however we solve it, so it exists." |
| **"All the Dreams Follow the Mouth"** | A talmudic saying (Berachot 55) meaning a dream's meaning is shaped by its interpretation — used here to explain how a person's choices "solve" what happens to them. | "...as our sages said (Berachot 55), 'All the dreams follow the mouth.'" |

### Opening

This session is a short preparation talk rather than a reading of a written article — Rav Laitman opens the gathering with prayer and framing before sending the group into a silent workshop. The question underneath the talk: what actually makes a group of individuals into a single force capable of "annulling the will to receive called evil," and why can a person not simply do this work alone?

---

## Part 1 — Gathering as One Man in One Heart

### Opening Intention

The lesson opens not with teaching but with a shared intention: the group has gathered "as one man in one heart with one desire," aiming to open their hearts and pray together for the "reforming light" to act in the lesson. The request is made on behalf of the ten, the friends, and the whole world:

> "May we succeed in praying for the lesson, opening our hearts, speaking from the heart. The main thing is that we do this together — for our goal to be realized and for us to make the Creator glad."

### Bestowing Contentment, Not Just Receiving

The stated aim of the prayer is to **bestow contentment upon the Creator** — not simply to ask for help reaching the goal, but to pray that all the friends succeed in reaching it, so that the upper force itself is made glad.

---

## Part 2 — Why a Society Is Needed to Annul the Will to Receive

### One Great Force

The talk states directly why individuals need a society at all: alone, a person cannot overcome his own nature.

> "We need a society that will form a great force so we can work together on annulling the will to receive called evil, as it hinders the achievement of the goal for which man was created."

For this reason, the society must consist of individuals who **unanimously agree** that they must achieve the goal. Once that agreement exists, the individuals become one great force — "where one can fight against oneself, since each one is incorporated in everyone else."

### Annulling Before Others, Not Judging Them

To be genuinely integrated into one another, each person must **annul himself before the others** — practically, this means seeing the friends' merits and not their faults. The talk gives the failure mode directly: a person who believes he is even a little higher than his friends can no longer unite with them.

---

## Part 3 — Preparing for the Workshop: Welding and the Sesame Seed

### From Preparation to Preparation

The group is described as coming together "in the preparation, really in the preparation to the preparation" — each person's individual point of desire is called very unique but very small, and the work of the gathering is to weld these small points together, with the hope that "the Creator, that the light, will do this welding to us together."

The talk notes the surrounding calendar as part of why the week has felt unusually dense: an eve of a holiday, a holiday itself, and a post-holiday day in close succession — "it's from preparation to preparation, it's all very thick." Each meeting, in this stretch, is treated as another opportunity to generate the group's embrace.

### The Common Vessel

> "We all yearn to reach one desire, to love the friends, in order to discover the Creator. Each one places his sesame seed, and that's how we create the common vessel."

The image offered is that no one person's contribution is large — each person's "sesame seed" is small — but together, placed into the same vessel, they form something the individual could not create alone.

### Watching Out for the Friends

A small concrete story is offered as an example of this care in practice: arriving that morning, the speaker heard the head of the ten on the phone, waking up a friend who had overslept — "our friends are constantly, constantly taking care of us." The lesson drawn from it is simple: "I need to care for the friends. Let's try to achieve that."

---

## Part 4 — Adhering to the Society

### No Influence Without Attachment

The talk closes on a single point, repeated twice for emphasis:

> "It is impossible to receive the influence of the society if he is not attached to the society — meaning, if he does not appreciate them. To the extent that he does, he can receive from them the influence without any work, simply by adhering to the society."

The implication is practical rather than abstract: appreciation of the group is not a nice sentiment on top of the work — it is the actual mechanism by which a person receives anything from the group at all.

### Closing Prayer

The gathering moves into a silent workshop with a short, repeated prayer:

> "Creator, help us adhere to the friends, and through them to You."

---

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **A society is needed because a person cannot annul his own will to receive alone.** United individuals become "one great force," able to work against their own nature specifically because each is incorporated in the others.
2. **Annulling oneself before the friends means seeing their merits, not their faults.** Believing oneself even slightly above the group breaks the possibility of uniting with it.
3. **Small individual contributions form one common vessel.** The "sesame seed" image frames each person's desire as small but essential to a collective result no individual could produce alone.
4. **Attachment to the society is the actual mechanism of receiving from it.** Appreciation is not sentiment — the talk states plainly that influence cannot be received without it.
5. **Caring for the friends is concrete, not abstract** — illustrated by the head of the ten personally waking a friend who had overslept.
6. **The goal of prayer in the gathering is to bestow contentment on the Creator**, not only to ask for personal or group advancement.

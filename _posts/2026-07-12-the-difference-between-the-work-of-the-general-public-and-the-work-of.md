### Opening

The lesson works from a strange detail in the Zohar: a beast is born with the same full strength it will ever have, while a person is not "appointed a force from above" until he is circumcised — and only afterward slowly acquires the lights of Nefesh, Ruach, Neshama, and Haya. Rav Laitman's lecture and the Q&A that follows take up the question this puzzle is really asking: what actually separates a "beast" from a "man" in the work, what does it mean to be "born" as a man, and what determines whether — and how fast — a person moves from working for himself (Lo Lishma) to working for the Creator (Lishma)?

---

## Part 1 — Beast and Man: What the Screen Determines

### Beast Is Not an Insult, It's a Stage

Rav Laitman opens his remarks on the article by stressing that "beast" describes a real, universal stage rather than a condemnation:

> "What is a beast? A beast doesn't have a purpose, the purpose of creation in mind. It doesn't ascend. As it was born, so it dies, and its entire role is to serve man. That is the degree of the beast. And the whole world is in that degree."

He includes observant people in this category: someone who studies Torah and keeps every commandment in detail, but never once asks whether the Creator benefits from it, is still — by this definition — working as a beast, Lo Lishma. The difference between all the degrees of "beast" (he compares this to the vast range of species in the animal world, from lice to large animals) and the degree of "man" is a single factor:

> "The difference is the screen. That's what determines it. It's either towards me, my intentions... or there is fear regarding how much and how I can bestow upon Him, meaning the fear isn't directed towards me, inside of me, but outside of me."

### Why a Jew Studies Lishma and a Gentile Doesn't

Rav Laitman recalls being taught, early in his own study, that "the Gentile studies Lo Lishma" while a Jew studies Lishma — and reframes this not as an ethnic claim but a functional one: "Yehudi" (Jew) describes someone who has reached a degree of *Yehud*, unity with the Creator, so by definition his study is measured by that unity. The real issue, he says, "is how people interpret Lo Lishma and Lishma" — not the label itself.

## Part 2 — Crossing the Barrier and the Period of Preparation

### What "Crossing the Barrier" Means

A student asks about the article's claim that a person "born" as a man immediately sees things going backward, not forward. Rav Laitman first clarifies what birth and "crossing the barrier" actually mean:

> "Only after a person crosses the barrier does he actually begin to become a man... that means that he's acquired an intention, maybe the smallest, but it is in order to bestow, and that is crossing the barrier."

He offers the image of a highway barrier that has to be raised before a driver can pass — in spiritual terms, acquiring even the smallest measure of intention to bestow is what "raises the barrier."

### The Period of Preparation Is Not Yet Birth

Everything before that point — including the state the group is currently in — is preparation, not birth. Rav Laitman describes this period as beginning when "the point in the heart" is revealed: alongside the ordinary heart's desire for worldly pleasure, a new desire for spirituality appears, but it is still "egoistic, clothed in order to receive." A person in this stage is tossed between looking at life from the ordinary heart and looking at it from the point in the heart:

> "Then instead of these pleasures, I go towards pleasures of the next world. I want to receive from them... But it's still not birth. The birth starts with the acquisition of the screen, the actual acquisition of it."

He adds that preparation "has many stages... but they cannot be divided in a very clear way," and that real birth — after the barrier — is itself still only the beginning: what follows is described through the image of "the seed entering the womb," then the "nine months of pregnancy," before the four degrees (Nefesh, Ruach, Neshama, Haya) are reached in sequence.

### What Changes Is Not in Our Hands

Asked what determines the pace of this process, Rav Laitman distinguishes two things that are commonly confused:

> "The pace of it depends on us. The pace of it depends on our awareness, our state. That depends on us. But the transition between the states, that doesn't depend on us."

A person's awareness changes how a given state is *felt* — whether a difficulty lands as a meaningful "pipe" leading somewhere, or as something closer to death — but the states themselves arrive according to a person's root in the world of Atzilut, scanned by the descending surrounding light.

## Part 3 — The Grace of Holiness: Wanting Without Yet Having a Screen

### Why It Can't Be Explained

A student asks how a person can intend Lishma before crossing the barrier at all, if intending Lishma is itself supposed to require the barrier. Rav Laitman's answer names a distinct, in-between experience — the "grace of holiness":

> "Intending Lishma before the barrier, it's not possible. But there is what we call [the] grace of sanctity — that I want it. Why? Because it's graceful... it's impossible to explain. Why can't I explain? Because it's the surrounding light. It doesn't enter my vessels. It doesn't give me knowledge... I want it. It attracts me. But why? Why really?... It's all light. The surrounding light provides us with that. That's why it's called surrounding."

He describes this grace as a mixture — not simply 99% Lo Lishma and 1% Lishma, since there is no screen yet to make that kind of split real — but as something entirely "on account of the upper one," who holds a person in that state before he has any independent capacity to use it.

### Fear of This World vs. Fear of the World to Come

The same question thread leads to a distinction between two kinds of fear. Rav Laitman locates the root of fear itself in a person's "empty vessels" — fear of not filling them (fear of this world) versus fear tied to the reward of the world to come:

> "This world means that you are operating with intentions to receive. The world to come means that [you] are operating with intentions to bestow." And, citing Baal HaSulam: "A person attains both this world and the next world while he is still alive in this life. They are simply two different states — without a screen and with a screen."

He notes that the ordinary religious idea of "reward in the world to come" is technically correct but is usually understood only in the crude, this-worldly sense — most people can recite that there's no reward for a mitzvah in this world without ever asking what "this world" and "the world to come" actually mean in the work.

### Gradual, Not All at Once

Rav Laitman lays out the sequence a person's self-concern is supposed to travel through: first, concern only for oneself in this world (in various forms and degrees); then a shift toward wanting pleasures of "the next world" instead; then, once a point in the heart is present, catching a small amount of the Creator's own nature — bestowal — inside the pleasure the surrounding light provides. That catch, however small, is itself the grace of holiness: "For the time being, it's all in Lo Lishma, but it is already the grace of sanctity."

## Part 4 — Study as a Means, Not an End

### Why Kabbalah Specifically

Asked whether the study material itself needs to change after the barrier, Rav Laitman first restates why any of this study matters at all:

> "I actually don't need Kabbalah for its own sake. I need Kabbalah because it's a means for the surrounding light... surrounding light brings the screen, brings the intention of Lishma."

He says any book, in principle, could serve this purpose if it brought a person to Lishma — "give me any other book, a children's book, it doesn't matter" — but that the language and books of Kabbalah specifically arouse the greatest surrounding light of all the holy books, because they were written from, and speak directly about, the spiritual degrees themselves.

### The "Magic Book" and the Cat

Once a person has some screen, however small, study changes character. Rav Laitman uses an image of a book whose pages "come alive" as the reader is present in them:

> "It's as if you're in a theater where what you read... the page comes alive with moving figures and scenes... you immediately live the reality you're reading about."

He adds that even now, before any screen, the intention brought to study still draws surrounding light — a person simply doesn't feel it, the way, in a story he tells about being with a friend (Krupnik) occupied with music "of the next world," a cat lying nearby stayed completely indifferent, unaffected, because it existed on a different degree entirely.

### The Discipline Baal HaSulam Recommends

Asked why Baal HaSulam advises reviewing definitions "a thousand times" and knowing them by heart, Rav Laitman explains this is meant to retrain the mind to interpret ordinary words spiritually rather than physically — his example is the word "circle": not a geometric shape, but a quality without corners, without separate distinctions. Repetition is what pushes the mind toward that internal reading rather than the literal one.

## Part 5 — Malchut and Keter: Working With Both Poles at Once

### Detachment and Demand, Together

A student raises what looks like a contradiction in the instruction to "observe from the side" while studying: doesn't real demand require descending fully into one's own lack, not just observing it from a distance? Rav Laitman answers that both are required simultaneously:

> "There is both aviyut and zakut together, coarseness and purity. The two extremes, Malchut and Keter. Malchut draws the light, Keter receives the light."

A person needs to hold, at once, an awareness of the exaltedness of what he is trying to reach (drawing him out of himself, toward equivalence of form) and full contact with his own coarseness and need (which is what actually draws the light). He describes the two points gradually becoming "one, even though they're opposites," through sustained work — but declines to fully resolve, in this session, exactly how "Malchut like Keter" is used before a screen exists, saying the group will have more experience with it "in another week and a half."

### Contradiction Is a Sign of Progress, Not Failure

The final question of the Q&A raises a related worry: if a person feels satisfaction one moment and total lack of advancement the next, is that a sign of failure? Rav Laitman answers by pointing to Rabash's teaching directly:

> "Rabash writes that every single discernment that we have contains within it two opposite polar states... The more you scrutinize them, one opposite the other, the greater they become, the farther apart they appear — it means that you've truly discerned these things and you're ready to progress. And if you don't notice this... you haven't examined any point in your work."

He adds that ordinary people instinctively run from states of contradiction and confusion, but the work asks the opposite: to wait for them, even seek them out, since "a new lack means progress."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **"Beast" and "man" are stages, not insults.** Anyone — however observant — who works without ever asking whether the Creator benefits is still working as a beast (Lo Lishma). The screen is what changes the category, not the quantity of Torah and mitzvot performed.
2. **Crossing the barrier means acquiring even the smallest intention to bestow.** Everything before that is preparation, including the ordinary swings between self-concern and a newly emerging point in the heart.
3. **Pace is ours; the transition itself is not.** Awareness and attitude change how a state feels and how quickly a person moves through it, but the sequence of states themselves is governed by a person's root in the world of Atzilut.
4. **The "grace of holiness" is an unexplainable pull toward Lishma that arrives before any screen exists.** It cannot be reasoned into being because it comes from surrounding, not inner, light — it can only be wanted, not manufactured.
5. **Fear of this world and fear of the next world both stem from empty vessels** — the real distinction between them is whether one is oriented toward receiving for oneself or toward bestowing.
6. **Kabbalah is a means, not an end in itself** — valuable specifically because its language and books draw the greatest surrounding light, which is what actually builds a screen.
7. **Contradiction between two opposite states in the same moment is the design, not a malfunction.** Per Rabash, the sharper the contradiction a person notices, the more real discernment — and readiness to progress — is taking place.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Beast (in the Work)** | A person or state working without any intention toward the Creator's benefit — Lo Lishma — regardless of how much Torah or mitzvot are performed externally. |
| **Man (in the Work)** | A person or state working with even the smallest intention to bestow upon the Creator; begins at "crossing the barrier." |
| **Screen (Masach)** | The acquired capacity to receive in order to bestow. Its presence or absence is what distinguishes "man" from "beast" in the work. |
| **Crossing the Barrier** | The transition point at which a person acquires the smallest possible intention to bestow, ending the period of preparation and beginning real "birth" as a man. |
| **Period of Preparation** | The stage before crossing the barrier, marked by the point in the heart and shifting, unstable attention between self-concern and spiritual desire. |
| **Point in the Heart** | The initial spiritual desire revealed within a person, alongside the ordinary heart's desire for worldly pleasure. |
| **Grace of Holiness** | An unexplainable pull toward wanting Lishma that arrives via surrounding light before a screen exists; cannot be reasoned into being. |
| **Lo Lishma / Lishma** | Not for Her sake (self-motivated work) / For Her sake (work aimed at the Creator's benefit). The path runs from Lo Lishma to Lishma. |
| **Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif)** | Light that shines on a person from outside the vessel rather than filling it directly; the actual mechanism by which Kabbalah study builds a screen and draws a person toward Lishma. |
| **Fear of This World / World to Come** | Fear rooted in empty vessels: fear of not receiving for oneself (this world) versus the fear and reward associated with intentions to bestow (the world to come). |
| **Malchut / Keter** | The two poles a person must hold together in the work: Malchut (coarseness, need, what draws the light) and Keter (purity, exaltedness, what receives the light). |
| **Aviyut / Zakut** | Coarseness and purity — the two qualities of the vessel that must be worked with simultaneously, corresponding to Malchut and Keter. |
| **Two Opposite Polar States** | Rabash's principle that every discernment in the work contains two contradictory poles; the sharper the contradiction perceived, the more real the discernment and the closer a person is to progress. |
| **World of Atzilut** | The world from which the surrounding light descends according to a person's root, determining (independent of the person) the sequence and timing of spiritual transitions. |

### Opening: Three Men in the Field

The article opens with a puzzle from the Zohar. The word "field" appears in connection with three very different figures:

- **Esav** — "a skillful hunter, a man of the field" (interpreted by the Zohar as robbing and killing people).
- **Isaac** — "And Isaac went out to stroll in the field" (a holy act of correction).
- **Jacob** — Isaac blesses Jacob saying "the scent of my son is as the scent of the field that the Lord has blessed."

**The central question:** How can the same word — field — describe both the holiness of Isaac and Jacob, and the destruction of Esav? What is "the field" in the Work?

---

## Part 1 — What Is the Field? Malchut and the Will to Receive

### The Purpose of Creation

The Creator created the world with a single aim: to do good to His creations. For this purpose He created something new — a desire to receive delight and pleasure. The measure of pleasure depends on the measure of yearning; greater yearning = greater pleasure.

This desire to receive is called **Malchut** — the vessel for receiving the lights. However, because of the bread of shame (the shame a receiver feels when receiving without earning), a correction was required: creation must learn to receive with an intention to bestow rather than to receive for itself.

**What is "the field"?** The field = Malchut = the will to receive. It is the space of choice — the place where a person decides whether to correct the will to receive into working in order to bestow (holiness), or to leave it working in order to receive for itself (the shell).

### Two Paths for the Field

**Corrected field (Kedushah):** The will to receive is used only with an intention to bestow. Isaac strolled in the field to correct it. Jacob's blessing — "the scent of the field that the Lord has blessed" — means he had already achieved this correction.

**Uncorrected field (Shell):** The will to receive operates for its own sake. This is Esav's field — robbing people and killing them, using the field for pure self-interest.

The correction of the field is called "correcting the world with the kingdom of Shaddai" — making Malchut (reception) take on the quality of Yesod (bestowal), the righteous foundation.

## Part 2 — The Two Flaws of Esav: Mind and Heart

### Skillful Hunter vs. Man of the Field

The Zohar describes Esav with two phrases: "a skillful hunter" and "a man of the field." Drawing from Nimrod — also called a mighty hunter — the Zohar teaches that hunting means misleading people's minds to rebel against the Creator.

Rabash identifies a precise distinction between the two phrases, corresponding to two elements of the Work:

**Skillful Hunter → The Mind → Faith.**
Nimrod hunted the minds of people. In the Work, the mind refers to faith above reason. A "skillful hunter" in the Work is one who damages faith — who rebels against the Creator, who refuses to believe above his own understanding.

**Man of the Field → The Heart → Desire.**
The heart refers to the desires — what a person is willing to do based on what reward or pleasure he expects. A "man of the field" in the Work is one whose heart works only for self-love: he will act only when he sees a worthwhile return for his effort.

**The principle:** Where Esav damaged the mind (faith), he also damaged the heart (desire). Both are needed for spiritual work. Both are the target of the evil inclination.

---

## Part 3 — Two Forms of Labor: Ox and Donkey

### Why Two Forces Are Needed

Because a restriction and concealment were placed on Malchut — so that man cannot see or feel who receives his work — a person must begin the work in the form of faith above reason. Within reason, the concealment hides everything. This is where the battle of choice begins.

The body does not want to do anything unless it sees what is done with its work — who benefits, what the reward is. Yet this is precisely what is hidden. Two kinds of labor are therefore required:

---

#### 1. As an Ox to the Burden — The Mind (Faith Above Reason)

An ox works by coercion — it obeys its owner regardless of its own desire. In the Work, a person must not ask the body if it wants to accept the burden of Torah and commandments. He must force it and believe in the sages that this is the path of truth — even though the body sees nothing and feels nothing worthwhile.

**This is the labor of the mind:** accepting the yoke of heaven above reason, believing that the Creator receives his work, even when this cannot be felt or seen.

---

#### 2. As a Donkey to the Load — The Heart (Equality Without Reward)

A donkey can carry a load even for an uncertain reward — but if told to carry with no reward at all, it wants to throw off the burden every moment. In the Work, a person is asked to work with no expectation of reward. The body experiences this as an unbearable load.

**This is the labor of the heart:** working for the sake of the Creator without any expectation of personal benefit. The body resists this at every moment. A person must walk in this direction despite the body's constant objection.

---

### Summary of the Two Labors

| | Labor | Element | Challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ox to the burden** | Faith above reason | Mind | Body cannot see who receives the work; must be forced to believe |
| **Donkey to the load** | Working without reward | Heart | Body wants to throw off any load with no benefit to itself |

**Key teaching (Baal HaSulam):** The true root of the difficulty with faith above reason is not intellectual — it is the will to receive. A person claims it is hard to go above reason, but in truth, what drives this difficulty is self-love. The beast within man wants to work only for itself. This is what makes faith feel impossible.

## Part 4 — Self-Deception: Esav's Real Error

### Performing the Action Without Correcting the Intention

Esav said he was in the field to pray — like Isaac. Externally, his behavior looked identical to Isaac's holy work of correcting Malchut. The problem was not in his actions. The problem was in his intention.

In the Work, actions are visible and can be checked. Intention is invisible — it lives in the heart. This is precisely where self-deception becomes possible. A person can perform holy actions while his heart is entirely for his own sake, and neither he nor anyone around him can easily detect this.

### Esav as Adam HaRishon — Theft of Authority

The sages said that Adam HaRishon was a thief — he ate from the tree of knowledge and thereby took something out of the singular authority (the Creator's authority) and placed it under his own authority. He wanted to receive everything for his own sake.

Esav did the same. He entered the field — externally correcting Malchut — but his intention robbed from the singular authority of the Creator. Everything he did was for himself. This is the meaning of "robbing people": he robbed the quality of man within himself and reduced himself to the level of a beast.

**"The wicked in their lives are called dead"** — because Esav works only for himself, separated from the life of lives, the Creator, he is spiritually dead even while physically alive.

### The Hillel and Shammai Story — Faith in the Sages

> A non-Jew came to Shammai and said: "Convert me, but teach me only the written Torah — I don't believe in the oral Torah." Shammai refused. He came to Hillel. On the first day, Hillel taught him Aleph, Bet, Gimel, Dalet. The next day, Hillel reversed the letters. The man said: "But yesterday you said it differently!" Hillel replied: "Do you not trust me? Then trust me about the oral Torah too."

**The lesson:** Even knowing which letter is Aleph requires trusting a teacher. Without faith in the sages, there is no starting point at all. Knowledge itself rests on trust. A person who claims he can accept the written Torah but not the oral has not understood that his acceptance of the written depends on the same faith he is refusing.

## Part 5 — The Path: Lo Lishmah to Lishmah

### The Practical Instruction

A person who begins the holy work must be careful not to deceive himself while performing actions. He must be honest with himself: his work is lo lishmah (not yet for the Creator's sake). He should tell his body openly:

> *"I am engaging in Torah and Mitzvot lo lishmah, and by this I want to aim lishmah."*

He believes in the words of the sages: *"One should always engage in Torah and Mitzvot lo lishmah, and from lo lishmah one will come to lishmah."* The light in Torah reforms a person — the Ohr Makif (surrounding light) drawn through study gradually corrects the intention from self-benefit to bestowal.

### Why Honesty About Lo Lishmah Matters

Esav's error was not that he was lo lishmah — every person begins there. His error was **self-deception**: claiming to be in the field for the Creator's sake (like Isaac) while inwardly working entirely for himself.

The correct path acknowledges: I am starting from where I am. I am not yet lishmah. I trust that the light of Torah will reform me if I engage faithfully and honestly. This is not a diminishment — it is the honest beginning of the whole correction.

**Rabash's note on the article:** Although Rabash's articles seem simple on the surface — as though he is just spinning yarns — there is enormous depth concealed within his special style. He opens things up while appearing to say everything plainly. Gradually, through study, the depth reveals itself.

---

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **The field = Malchut = the will to receive.** It is the space of choice. Every person must enter the field and decide what to do with their desire.
2. **Two corrections are needed: mind (faith above reason) and heart (working without reward).** Both are the constant target of the evil inclination.
3. **Esav's failure was not in his actions but in his intention.** He entered the field outwardly like Isaac but inwardly worked for himself — this is the most subtle form of self-deception.
4. **The root of difficulty with faith is not intellectual.** It is the will to receive — the beast within — that makes faith above reason feel impossible.
5. **Faith in the sages is not blind** — it is the same trust on which all knowledge rests. Without it, there is no starting point for anything.
6. **The honest path begins with lo lishmah.** Acknowledge where you are. Use the Torah to draw the reforming light. Trust that it will bring you to lishmah.

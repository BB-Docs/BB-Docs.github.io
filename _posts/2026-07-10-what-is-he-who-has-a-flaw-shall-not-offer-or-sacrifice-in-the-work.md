### Opening

The article's puzzle is stark: the Zohar disqualifies anyone with a blemish from serving in holiness, since "one who is flawed has no faith in him." Taken literally, this seems to leave a person born with a defect no choice at all — either he is healed first, or he is barred from the work of faith. Rav Laitman's recorded lecture turns this from an abstract question into a practical one: given that everyone works with some flaw — some organs that refuse to agree to bestowal — how does a person actually advance despite it? The lecture and Q&A that follow trace the answer through several threads: that advancement comes only through the group, never through private shortcuts; that a person must prepare in advance for the "dead" periods when nothing seems to move; that everything in the work depends on intention rather than action; and that real progress shows up as a growing indifference to externality the more a person's internal world develops.

---

## Part 1 — The Purpose of Creation and the Necessity of Choice

### Why the Path Must Begin From the Opposite of Perfection

Rav Laitman opens the recorded lesson by restating the thought of creation: the Creator's desire to do good to His creations in a complete, eternal way. To reach that state, a created being must travel the entire distance from imperfection to the Creator's own degree. Because of this, the created being's starting nature is evil — "the opposite of the Creator's nature" — and the Torah was given only to expose that evil so it can be corrected: "I have created the evil inclination, I have created the Torah as its spice."

### Choice Is Choosing a Society

The one place a person is genuinely free, Rav Laitman explains, is in deciding which society to place himself in — one whose entire purpose is to advance toward the Creator, or one aimed at other goals. The Creator does not simply leave a person to fend for himself; He brings him to a good society and says, in effect, "take this for yourself" — so that no one can later claim he was never given the opportunity to choose.

> The initial point, which is the opposite of perfection, is necessary. We are born [into it] and only gradually discover its true form... The path, too, depends on the structure of the soul... Only the path depends on us.

### There Is No Private Shortcut

Rav Laitman illustrates this with a story: an ultra-Orthodox man had repeatedly asked to meet with him privately for personal guidance, assuming there must be some special individual instruction beyond what is taught in the group. Rav Laitman told him plainly he had nothing extra to give — the same questions asked privately are answered in the group itself. The man, despite years of study and real knowledge of the material, could not understand how to advance until he grasped that **the entire work is work in the society** — not in private conversation, but in the ordinary actions of the group: dissemination, gatherings, meals, Shabbat. "It's not the actions themselves that do so, but the fact that we're working on expanding and strengthening the society, both in breadth and in height — that is the entire means."

## Part 2 — Preparing for the "Dead" Periods

### Sleepwalking Through the Work

Asked about states where a person can do nothing but "sit down like a lump," go through the motions at work and at home like a sleepwalker, Rav Laitman confirms these dead periods exist and can persist "until it dries you out completely." The remedy is not to wait for such states to pass on their own, but to prepare for them in advance.

### Preparation as the Whole of "Our Time"

Drawing on his own pre-Kabbalah experience running a large dental clinic, Rav Laitman describes overcoming his own impatience only by rehearsing in advance how he would behave under pressure — talking himself through it until he could hold on for an hour, then a day. He connects this directly to spiritual work: "our present time is also called the time of preparation." A person in a good state should use it to study, and to plan in advance that if even a small descent comes, he will immediately attach it to his current state of ascent.

> Where do the ascents come from? They come from joining the descents to your constant state, to your fixed state.

## Part 3 — Strengthening the Group Without Fear of "Noise"

### Demand in Proportion to How Much You Want to Be Influenced

Asked how much to strengthen the group, Rav Laitman answers: to the degree you want the group to influence you. If a person recognizes his own weakness, he should demand accordingly from his friends — scrutinize, push, provoke response.

### Disagreement Is Better Than Sleepiness

Rav Laitman explicitly rejects the instinct to keep a group calm and orderly at all costs: "It's better to make some noise than to sit quietly... there's nothing worse than this general state of sleepiness." A person who jumps up, shouts, or disagrees is where change comes from. The work requires patience with routine, but also room for outbursts.

## Part 4 — What Is Intention?

### Action Can Look Identical While Intention Differs Entirely

Rav Laitman defines intention through several corporeal examples. A surgeon and a murderer perform the same physical action — cutting into a body — but the intention makes them opposites. A friend who struck him with a heavy medical book to relieve a painful swelling looked, from the outside, like an assault; the intention made it healing. Politicians' public actions on television are only the visible layer; "the intentions are behind the screen," in their private thinking and speech.

### Action and Intention Are Fused Only Below the Human Level

Among the still, vegetative, and animate levels of nature, action and intention are one and the same — even predatory animals that "deceive" and stalk prey do so by instinct, not by planning. Only at the speaking (human) level do action and intention become genuinely separable. Rav Laitman applies this directly to the group's own work: a person can be the best worker in the kitchen or in dissemination, but if his intention is to become the manager, "he's not going to gain anything spiritual from that" — though the surrounding environment may still gradually work on and change his intention.

## Part 5 — Connecting the Heart, and Why Intention Precedes Action

### First Connect the Innermost Point, Then Act

Asked how to even examine one's own intention, Rav Laitman describes a specific order of operations: first connect the innermost point of the heart to the Creator alone — "it's as though I place Him inside my heart" — and only then turn outward to society, family, and daily life. This connection determines the value of everything else: to the extent worldly disturbances threaten it and a person resists those disturbances to preserve the connection, the whole world becomes "worthwhile" as a means.

> That's called the whole Torah on one foot.

### Intention Without Action Is "Like an Angel"

A student asks why intention is called the main thing at all if action is also necessary. Rav Laitman answers that intention determines the nature of the action, but intention without action never changes or develops a person — "it's like an angel," not a human being. The sequence is: bring the Creator into the heart (the intention/Rosh), then go out into life carrying that point; disturbances will test it, and overcoming them while preserving the connection is what grows the point into full stature — described in the lecture as growing "into ten sefirot," through the structure of a partzuf.

### Goal, Then Intention, Then Action

Asked about the relationship between intention and purpose, Rav Laitman states the order plainly: first there must be a clear goal; from the goal comes the intention; only then comes the action. Intention itself is already a kind of action — the establishment of the connection to the Creator.

### Mesirut Nefesh: "One Who Puts Himself to Death Over It"

Responding to a question about the saying that Torah exists only in "one who puts himself to death in a tent" over it, Rav Laitman explains this refers to the death of the will to receive for oneself — putting to death the intention to receive so that a person aligns with the light itself and it "resides within him." This is identified as **mesirut nefesh** (devotion/self-sacrifice): surrendering one's own will, performing a restriction on it. He advises translating every concept encountered — whether from law, Midrash, Aggadah, the Five Books, or ordinary speech — into the single, consistent language of Sefirot and Partzufim, so as not to become confused by mixing frameworks.

## Part 6 — Internality and Externality

### Why the Group Looks Unremarkable From Outside

Asked how a person in darkness can draw inspiration from the group when even that inspiration seems to "pass through him," Rav Laitman first observes that Bnei Baruch, seen from outside, "doesn't appear to be anything special." He retells the story of the prophet who gathered people who felt themselves empty and took them into the wilderness precisely so he could later fill them — the closer a person comes to the Creator, the emptier and more insignificant he sees himself by comparison, not the reverse.

### Rav Laitman's Own Story

He recalls his own skepticism before meeting his teacher — having been told a real Kabbalist would make him "tremble," and finding this claim absurd for someone as technically minded as himself, until he actually met simple-looking people connected to Kabbalah and understood what he was sensing in them.

### Today's Internality Becomes Tomorrow's Externality — Then Is Discarded

Rav Laitman describes advancement as a continuous process of discovering deeper internality while discarding what had been internal the day before, now treated as mere external garment. People who instead chase externality — fashion, self-presentation, constant reinvention — move in the opposite direction. He illustrates this with a friend from Jerusalem, a Satmar Hasid "born that way," who went through a crisis and stopped keeping certain outward religious practices — not from irreligion, but because deepening internality naturally reduces sensitivity to externality.

### A Person in Darkness Is Like a Corpse — But Is Still Being Revived

Answering a question about a person who feels nothing but emptiness, Rav Laitman describes this state as being "like a corpse" even while physically present in the group — the person cannot feel that anything is affecting him, "yet in the meantime they're performing resuscitation on you." The instruction is to give oneself over entirely to those doing the caring — the group — "disconnect your own head and let them do whatever they want with your body," as the small one in spirituality carries out what the great one determines, rather than directing itself.

---

## Part 7 — Closing Round: What the Group Takes From the Lesson

In the closing sharing, students name what stood out: that advancement comes only through intention, not action; that today's internality becomes tomorrow's externality and is then shed; that a person's whole reception must aim at the Creator alone, with no concern for intention regarding anything else; and that action without intention is "like an angel" — the act itself is what grows the point established by the intention. One student recaps the sequence Rav Laitman gave: connect to the Creator from the deepest point in the heart (restriction), then go out and act, expecting disturbances that call for renewed connection each time. Another repeats Rav Laitman's closing instruction about the group's mood: don't fear noise or disagreement — oppressive quiet in the group is worse than open conflict.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **A flaw in the work does not disqualify a person from faith** — everyone works with organs that resist bestowal; the task is to ask the Creator for the power of faith, not to wait for the flaw to disappear first.
2. **There is no private shortcut to advancement.** The entire work happens through the group — its ordinary activities of dissemination, gatherings, and meals — not through individual guidance from a teacher.
3. **Descents and "dead" periods should be prepared for in advance**, not merely endured; ascents come specifically from joining a descent to one's fixed, constant state.
4. **A healthy group tolerates — even needs — disagreement and "noise."** Oppressive calm and sleepiness are worse than open demand and conflict among friends.
5. **Intention, not action, is what determines everything.** Identical actions (a surgeon's cut, a public gesture) can be opposite in meaning depending on the intention behind them; this separability exists only at the human level.
6. **The working order is goal → intention → action.** Intention begins with connecting the heart's innermost point to the Creator alone; action then grows that connection by surviving life's disturbances.
7. **Growth in internality shows up as declining concern for externality** — a principle illustrated through the group's own unimpressive outward appearance and a friend's changing outward practice as his internal work deepened.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Flaw / Blemish (in the Work)** | Not a physical defect but organs that do not agree to work of bestowal; results from lack of faith in the Creator's greatness, not from personal sin. |
| **Mesirut Nefesh** | Devotion / self-sacrifice — "putting to death" the will to receive for oneself so a person can align with and hold the light of Torah. |
| **Intention** | The inner attitude of the heart toward someone or something; in the Work, what determines the spiritual value of an action, as distinct from the action itself. |
| **Action Without Intention** | Described as "like an angel" — it does not change or develop a person, since growth comes from intention overcoming disturbance. |
| **Rosh / Partzuf / Ten Sefirot** | The structure through which a connection (intention) established at the "head" extends outward into action, incorporating other desires as it grows into full stature. |
| **Restriction** | The act of disconnecting from all other concerns in order to find and hold connection with the Creator from the innermost point of the heart. |
| **Internality vs. Externality** | Internality is the depth of a person's genuine spiritual work; externality is outward appearance and self-presentation. Growth in the former reduces attachment to the latter. |
| **The Group / Society (Bnei Baruch)** | The sole means of advancement in the Work; described as outwardly unremarkable but the actual mechanism of correction through its shared activities. |
| **"Like a Corpse"** | The felt state of a person in deep darkness or descent — unable to feel that anything is affecting him, even while the group continues to work on and "revive" him. |
| **Lo Lishma / Lishma** | Not for Her sake / for Her sake — referenced implicitly through the discussion of intention as the axis distinguishing self-serving from Creator-directed work. |

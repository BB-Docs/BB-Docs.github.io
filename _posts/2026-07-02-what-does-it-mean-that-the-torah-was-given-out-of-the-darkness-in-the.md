### Opening

The lesson has two movements. The first reads Rabash's article on why the Torah is described as having been "given out of the darkness" — why the nations of the world could not receive it that way, while Israel could, and what "darkness" actually means in the Work. The second movement turns to a 2002 lesson on the same article and opens into a live workshop: students ask Rav Laitman how the 613 parts of the soul are corrected, where free choice actually comes from if everything about a person is otherwise given from above, and why a person who feels he is regressing may in fact be advancing.

---

## Part 1 — Darkness as the Doorway to Torah

### What Is Darkness in the Work?

The article opens with a passage from the Midrash: the nations of the world did not receive the Torah because it was given out of darkness, but Israel did receive it — "As I hear the voice out of the darkness... the Lord will shine upon you, and His glory will be seen upon you."

**Darkness**, in the Work, is not simply unhappiness. It is what a person feels when he wants to draw near the Creator — to work with **vessels of bestowal** (called "Israel" in the Work) — but cannot, because his body, extended from the **vessels of reception** (called "the nations of the world"), disagrees. He senses that without vessels of bestowal he will never receive the upper light, the light of doing good to His creations. That felt lack is darkness.

> "There is no light without a vessel, no filling without a lack."

This lack itself is what creates the need for the light of Torah: *"I have created the evil inclination, I have created the spice of the Torah as a spice."* The Torah is given specifically to the deficient — to those who feel the darkness.

### Why the Nations of the World Could Not Receive It

The "nations of the world" in this article are not literal peoples — they are the part of a person that wants only the vessels of reception, with no desire to leave that control. They want Torah in order to *add* pleasure to what they already have from this world:

> "Give, give — give us the wealth of this world, and give us the wealth of the next world."

Because they don't feel darkness — for them, the vessels of reception already feel like light — they have no genuine deficiency calling for the Torah, and so, in the words of the verse, "the darkness will cover the earth" for them.

### What the Torah Actually Does

For the one who does feel the darkness, the Torah accomplishes two things:

| Stage | What Happens | Verse |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Reform** | The light in the Torah reforms the person; the restriction on the vessels of reception is lifted and replaced with vessels of bestowal | "And the Lord will shine upon you" |
| **2. Revelation** | Once a person can work *Lishma* (for Her sake) with those corrected vessels, the secrets of the Torah are shown to him | "And His glory will be seen upon you" |

At that point, the teaching concludes, "Israel, the light, and the Creator are one."

## Part 2 — Much Learning or Much Work: Where Intention Actually Lives

### The Talmudic Question

The article turns to a well-known debate: Rabbi Tarfon says "much work" is greater; Rabbi Akiva says "much learning" is greater — and the sages sided with Rabbi Akiva, "since learning leads to work."

Rabash reads this in the Work as two parts of Torah itself, not two categories of people:

- **"Work"** = the *revealed* part of Torah — the rules and measures of the mitzvot (how large a tallit, how the mitzvot of Shabbat are kept) — visible actions a person can simply be told to do, and can be coerced into doing.
- **"Learning"** = the *concealed* part of Torah — the aim placed on those actions, which lives in the heart and cannot be forced.

**Baal HaSulam's reading of "which God created to do":** creation means existence from absence — the will to receive. "To do" is the labor the created being must perform on that will: placing on it the aim to bestow. The revealed mitzvot can be kept by coercion; the concealed aim cannot.

### Why the Sages Chose "Learning"

A person can force his body to perform the 613 mitzvot in action even while his heart disagrees. But he cannot force his heart to *want* to bestow — that is against his given nature. Real progress happens like this:

1. Before real effort, a person assumes he *could* work entirely for the Creator's sake whenever he chose — he simply hasn't gotten around to it yet, out of "negligence."
2. After genuinely laboring toward bestowal and seeing no progress — even falling into despair — he discovers something new: his nature truly cannot do this on its own. *"This recognition is called wicked."*
3. Only at that point does he need, and can he use, the light of Torah as "a spice" to reform the evil inclination he has now actually recognized in himself.

**Conclusion:** actions of bestowal are impossible without the light of Torah. "Learning" — the part of Torah that works on the heart's aim — is what leads to genuine "work," so the sages ruled in its favor.

## Part 3 — The 613 Parts: Torah, Light, and the Vessel

Moving into the 2002 lesson on the same article, Rav Laitman lays out the mechanics behind "much learning leads to work":

- The soul (*neshama*) has **613 parts**, all initially corrupted. Correcting each one is called observing the **613 Eitin** ("counsels").
- As each part is corrected, light is deposited against it — this is called being rewarded with the **613 Pakudin** ("deposits"), together with 7 rabbinic additions making **620** parts in total.
- The Torah itself is the general light (one name for the Creator) divided into these 613 parts to match the vessel of the soul; the mitzvot are the corrections of each part.

**On attaining the Creator:** a student asks why the goal is called *adhesion (dvikut)* to the Creator rather than simply "correction of the soul." Rav Laitman's answer: correction of the vessel is only a *means* — a state must be reached, and that state is dvikut, resembling the Creator (*Adam*, from the root "to resemble"). A person never attains the Creator directly, only his own corrected vessels: *"You appreciate only according to the measure of correction of your vessels."* Knowing the Creator's qualities — merciful, gracious — means having corrected oneself to feel those qualities, not observing them from outside.

## Part 4 — Free Choice: The Only Thing Not Sealed From Above

A student asks the practical question underneath all of this: what action can a person actually take, if everything about the vessel — its cracks, its records, the light that reaches it — is "stamped and sealed" from above?

### Why the Soul Had to Be Shattered

If the soul had descended whole, nothing about a person's state could be attributed to his own choice — everything, including the desire itself, would simply come from above. The shattering of the general soul into many parts creates, on the level of each individual part, a narrow but real space for choice:

> On my own plane, I cannot appeal what I am given. But I can add to it — by being impressed by an adjacent vessel, a friend.

### What Can Actually Be Taken From a Friend

Not a friend's inner thoughts or desires — a person cannot draw on those directly. What can be taken is an impression from a friend's **external form**: how he studies, how diligently he works, what he is visibly striving for.

> "Look only at the good things. At the bad things, shut your eyes... judge a friend to the scale of merit."

This is not a moral prohibition — it is practical advice for a person's own advancement: judging a friend favorably draws in the friend's yearning for the Creator as an added desire, which invites more light. Two connected vessels, drawn together this way, receive illumination as if for two souls at once.

This — connecting with the point of desire in another broken soul — is described as the *only* place free choice genuinely operates, and it is the reason the group ("Jews," from *Ever*, "to cross over") exists at all: without a nearby person to be impressed by, this single avenue of choice has nothing to work with.

## Part 5 — Reading the Left Line: Why Feeling Worse Can Mean You're Growing

### Darkness as a Sign of New Vessels

A student raises the article's claim that a person can labor and feel he is *regressing*. Rav Laitman explains this as entering the **left line**: a new, larger vessel is being revealed, still empty, before it can be filled with correction and light. The emptiness itself is the sign that growth is underway — not failure.

Conversely, a person who feels only satisfaction — "everything is good, I have the best group, the best books, thank God" — without any friction, is a warning sign, not an achievement: it means he is not exerting enough internally, or is not truly connected to the friends in a way that exposes new lack. **"Envy" of a friend's diligence** (in the sense of wanting to reach what he has, not diminish him) is named as the missing ingredient — "envy among the wise increases wisdom."

### Being Happy in the Darkness

Answering how a person is supposed to stay in "uplifted spirit" while also honestly feeling darkness, Rav Laitman distinguishes the feeling itself from what it signals: a person can be genuinely in a bad, dark state and still be happy about what that state is building toward — this addition from the future is what is meant by the *light of faith*. It does not erase or sweeten the darkness being felt right now; it sits alongside it.

## Part 6 — Internal vs. External Mitzvot: What Counts as "Action"?

### The Question

The article states that one who studies Torah without observing the 613 mitzvot in actual practice is only studying "wisdom," like an external teaching — not Torah. A student asks: does that mean physical acts like putting on tefillin are what's really being discussed?

### Rabash's Distinction

Rav Laitman clarifies that the "action" in question is not the physical mitzvah performed with the body — that is a *practical* mitzvah tied to one's circumstances in this world (a Kohen's laws, a woman's laws, laws that depended on the Temple). Physical observance alone, done out of habit without engaging the will, corrects only what is called the **still (inanimate) level of sanctity** — real, but unable by itself to produce spiritual growth.

The actual "action" the article means is the **internal correction of the 613 desires of the soul** — installing a screen on the will to receive so it can work in order to bestow. This inner correction must be pursued regardless of a person's external circumstances, because it covers all 620 parts of the soul that every person carries, independent of which physical mitzvot happen to apply to their situation in this life.

### A Real, Acknowledged Tension

A student observes that people who begin studying the internal dimension of Torah often visibly start to look down on external, practical observance. Rav Laitman does not deny this — he affirms it is a known, real phenomenon, not something to explain away, and says it needs to be studied honestly rather than judged as simply good or bad. A person's state at any moment — described even as being a "thief," "robber," or "righteous" — is determined entirely by the measure of light currently shining on him, and states shift over time as a person's work continues.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Darkness is the precondition for receiving the Torah, not an obstacle to it.** It is the felt gap between wanting vessels of bestowal and being unable to attain them — and that gap is what creates the need the Torah answers.
2. **Wanting more of what you already have is not the same as feeling darkness.** The nations of the world seek to add pleasure to reception; Israel seeks release from reception's control. Only the second is answered by Torah.
3. **"Learning leads to work" because the heart cannot be coerced.** Actions can be forced; the aim to bestow cannot — it requires the light drawn through learning.
4. **Recognizing your own evil inclination requires real, failed effort first.** Before genuine labor, a person assumes he could bestow anytime he chose; only after laboring and despairing does he discover this is not so.
5. **Free choice exists in exactly one place: being impressed by a friend's external effort.** Everything else about a person's state is given from above; only the choice to draw a desire from someone nearby is genuinely his own.
6. **Feeling like you're regressing can be a sign of a new vessel being revealed (the left line).** Constant, friction-free satisfaction, by contrast, can mean a person has stopped growing.
7. **Internal correction of the 613 desires — not physical observance alone — is the "work" the article means**, though the two are not in true opposition; the tension between them is real and worth examining honestly rather than denying.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Darkness (in the Work)** | The felt lack of vessels of bestowal — wanting to bestow but being unable to, because the body/will to receive disagrees. The precondition for needing and receiving the Torah. |
| **Vessels of Bestowal / Vessels of Reception** | "Israel" and "the nations of the world" respectively, in the internal sense — the desire to give vs. the desire to receive for oneself. |
| **613 Eitin / 613 Pakudin** | The 613 "counsels" for correcting each part of the soul, and the 613 "deposits" of light received once each part is corrected; together with 7 rabbinic mitzvot, 620 parts in total. |
| **Lishma / Lo Lishma** | For Her (the Creator's) sake / not for Her sake. Working Lishma with corrected vessels is the condition for the secrets of Torah to be revealed. |
| **"Which God Created to Do"** | Baal HaSulam's reading: "created" = existence from absence, the will to receive; "to do" = the created being's task of placing the aim to bestow upon that will. |
| **Dvikut (Adhesion)** | The actual spiritual goal — resembling the Creator — as distinct from "correction of the soul," which is only the means to reach it. |
| **Free Choice (in this lesson)** | The single point of genuine agency available to a person: being impressed by a friend's external effort and drawing an added desire for the Creator from him. |
| **Judging to the Scale of Merit** | Deliberately viewing a friend's good qualities and overlooking flaws, in order to draw benefit and desire from the friend — not a moral rule but practical advice for one's own advancement. |
| **Left Line / Right Line** | Left: honestly feeling one's actual emptiness or lack (a sign a new vessel is opening). Right: standing in faith and gratitude. Constant right line without left can mean stalled growth. |
| **Still (Inanimate) Level of Sanctity** | Correction achieved through habitual, external observance of mitzvot alone — real but incapable of producing further growth by itself. |
| **The 613 Desires of the Soul** | The internal object of correction — distinct from the practical mitzvot tied to one's circumstances in this world, and common to every soul regardless of external circumstance. |
| **Light of Faith** | The addition felt from a hoped-for future even while a present state remains genuinely dark — allows a person to be "happy in the darkness" without denying it. |

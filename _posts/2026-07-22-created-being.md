### Opening

The session opens mid-thought, already inside the hardest question in the whole system: if a person starts out as nothing but a deficiency utterly bound to the Creator's will, with no independence at all, how can anything genuinely separate — a "created being" — ever come to exist? Rav Laitman works through the answer piece by piece: how a screen (Masach) is what actually produces a created being, who counts as "Israel" versus "the nations of the world" within that process, what angels actually are, why shame persists even in bestowal, and what contentment means once the "I" is gone. The Q&A then turns to an even harder question: if everything — worlds, souls, even our own bodies — behaves like a fixed, predetermined system, where is free choice actually located?

## Part 1 — From Sheer Deficiency to a Screen: How a Created Being Comes to Exist

### No Independence, Only a Deficiency

Rav Laitman started from the vessel's original condition — pure lack, with no separateness in it at all:

> "Of course there's no independence in that vessel yet, only a deficiency for the presence of the Creator... it's just the Creator is nature in general, and this vessel is a replica, or a deficiency that comes from that. So there's no created being yet."

A created being, he specified, means something more demanding than a deficiency: "an independent force that works outside the Creator's plan, outside the desire of the Creator" — and because everything outside the Creator is by definition opposite Him, this independence is inherently "against" Him, not neutral.

### Two Opposite Qualities, Compared

If there is only one force, one desire, how could a second, truly separate force ever emerge from it? Rav Laitman's answer was that the Creator built in the comparison itself:

> "The Creator created, as if, a state where two qualities exist at once — his quality, and the opposite of his quality, at once. And then, by comparing these two qualities, comes out the created being — the result, the outcome of the lack of feeling the Creator and the feeling of the Creator."

Determining, through that difference, one's own relation to the Creator's presence is what produces a created being — "and that created being is called Masah, the screen, intention."

### Three Things That Must Exist Together

He was precise that the deficiency involved has to be a specific kind, not just any lack — the deficiency to feel the Creator's presence, and the deficiency to determine one's own attitude toward Him:

> "So there are three things here: there's deficiency, and pleasure, and what we call determining the relationship — the relation between them, all of this situation, circumstance, and a screen between them. When these three things exist together, then we can talk about the existence of the created being. If one of them is lacking, this concept of created being cannot exist in reality."

## Part 2 — Israel and the Nations of the World

### A Desire That Agrees to Feel Opposite

Rav Laitman brought in a Midrash to explain why some desires correct themselves and others don't:

> "The Midrash says that the Creator turned to each of the desires, and no one agreed to receive the force of correction — only a unique desire that felt itself as opposite from the Creator. That he wants... His desire is called Israel, after [Yashar-El — straight to the Creator]."

Why not the nations of the world instead? Because, he said, "the nations of the world are desires that don't feel opposite — their desire, the presence of the Creator — leads them to yearn for [correction]," while a desire that does feel that break processes itself "to the point that they reach the true desires, called desires for holiness."

### Who Is "Israel," Who Is "Goy"

Rav Laitman drew the line squarely on this felt sense of brokenness, not on any external marker:

> "Israel is one who feels that he's broken, that he's opposite from the Creator, and begins to understand — in that status, opposite the Creator — that equivalence of form has to be his goal... The one who doesn't feel broken, that it demands correction — what is that? It's called Goy, a nation, or Gentile. He doesn't need to [correct]. He says, 'Why do we need it?'"

He carried the process one step further: once the desire called Israel corrects itself and "receives Torah," that correction is said to expand outward — "the correction spreads to the rest of [the desires]... called nations" — who then also begin to want that same correction.

## Part 3 — What Angels Actually Are

A participant asked what angels are, given that "we" are supposedly working between ourselves and the Creator. Rav Laitman defined angels functionally, not as separate beings:

> "Angels are assistants by which the desire becomes a correct desire, and receives forces to correct itself, to demand fulfillment — to fulfill itself — because the desire itself has no possibility to make any action besides feeling itself... The desire cannot do more; the feeling itself works."

He was explicit that angels are not entities born outside the will-to-receive-and-light system, but forces generated from within it:

> "These angels are not something that's born outside of the will to receive and the light. Rather, using the will to receive and the light purposefully — that is called angels... temporary, particular, specific forces, incidental forces that are used in a certain state in order to advance."

Both restraining and advancing forces are needed together, he added, precisely so that progress toward the goal stays controlled: "there are seemingly good angels and bad angels, opposite each other, so that by both of them you can always determine the precise progress toward the goal." Put simply: "the inner and outer environment of the created being, how it works on him, is called angels."

## Part 4 — Shame and the Vessel of Reception

A participant pushed on a specific line from the article: if the intention has already been corrected to bestowal, why does any shame remain?

### The Vessel Never Disappears

Rav Laitman confirmed that even a corrected intention doesn't erase the underlying vessel:

> "He writes that the created being is ashamed even while using the intention of bestowal. He's still ashamed that the original vessel is the will to receive... So first he has to correct his own vessels of bestowal."

### The King's Allegory (Page 99, ¶2)

Asked to look at the specific passage, the group turned to an allegory about a king preparing a meal for someone he loves:

> "By this you'll understand the allegory — that the king says that he wants to make a meal for his lover, and he said, 'Go and correct for me,' meaning to do the action of receiving in order to bestow. And he went and corrected. And then, when the light came by way of the desire to bestow to the lower one, [he received it] — but the vessel he's working with is the desire to receive, and not to bestow."

The unresolved shame, Rav Laitman confirmed, is exactly the gap between "the layperson's vessels" and vessels that are already higher: correcting the intention alone does not, by itself, close that gap.

## Part 5 — Contentment: When the "I" Disappears

### Not My Desire — His Desire to Give

Asked what "contentment" actually means, Rav Laitman located it entirely outside personal sensation:

> "The vessel we attain is a deficiency for the Creator — the initial deficiency for which He created us, the deficiency of the cow that wants to feed the calf. It's not my desire to receive in order to bestow; it's His desire to give to me. That desire I attain, and in it I live. And then, when I fulfill this desire, then my feeling in that desire is called contentment."

He pressed the point further — contentment is not even personal at all:

> "It's not even mine, it's not my desire, it's not an individual sensation. I don't feel myself in it — I live in Him, I feel Him. The 'I' disappears... This is a totally different vessel, not the vessel of the created being."

### Why Everyone's Experience Still Differs

If contentment erases the "I," why does the felt yearning differ from person to person? Rav Laitman's answer kept the paradox intact rather than resolving it away:

> "There's no answer to that — why are they different, why are we different. Everyone feels himself even after he is included with everyone, because of his special point of existence from absence. Each one finds himself in the Creator, in the thought of creation... Of course we're all together and we're all one vessel, but this individuality, this singularity, remains."

## Part 6 — Is Any of This Free? The Case for a Fixed System

### The Biologist and the Black Box

Shifting into the second half of the session, Rav Laitman used an analogy from research and computing to frame how "choice" looks from outside a system:

> "When you investigate some cell and how it works, you put some acid in it, and it starts behaving in a certain way... It's like when we work with a computer — you say, 'I do this, it does that.' You just don't know how it's supposed to behave. If you knew the whole system, you would have no doubt how it's going to behave... The expert doesn't look at the character of the computer. Any system is a black box — I don't care what happens inside. I know there's ins and outs. If there's some mishap — put something in and nothing comes out, or not what should come out — that needs a correction. That's called an illness, or a mishap, or a malfunction."

He extended this directly to human beings: "same thing with us, in any body — biological, mechanical, electrical. It doesn't matter. If you know exactly what its inner system is, there's no problem knowing how it's going to behave. Just input and output."

### Worlds, Souls, and a "Fixed Match"

Pressed on whether the system itself has any choice, Rav Laitman was blunt:

> "No. So when we're talking about all the worlds — even the souls — that seem to have free choice, it's not free choice, because of the light and vessel and what happens between them. There's no [hacks] here; it's all known in advance. It's a fixed match, so to speak. There's a parameter being determined above them — the feeling of the Emanator. And the feeling of the Emanator is something that is premeditated from above."

## Part 7 — Above Reason: Where Real Choice Actually Lives

### A Free Parameter That Isn't About the Creator

If the whole system is predetermined, Rav Laitman still located one exception — not with respect to the Creator, but with respect to the created being itself:

> "If we investigate all our actions and everything that works on us, you're not going to find any free parameter. And it can't be, according to reason, that the will to receive, in some situation, can determine that it wants to rise above it. Rather, this free parameter — determining this desire can truly be free — not with respect to the Creator, but with respect to the created being. This is called above reason. It doesn't exist in this world; it only exists in the spiritual world."

Before the Machsom, he added, "we are simply beasts... prepared for free choice, bit by bit" — but the same quality of freedom, once it becomes real above reason, is "called Israel, the measure of man, not a beast."

### Two Contradictory Feelings to Work From

Asked where anyone could even locate choice before the barrier, Rav Laitman pointed to something observable within a person, rather than an abstract claim:

> "Sometimes I'm in a state where I feel the world is good, there's an upper force, there's a future... I have this feeling — maybe it comes once a month, or once a year. Relative to that, there are other feelings, most of the time, that there's no hope, the world has no purpose. Do you see that these are two contradictory states? From these two states, you can start working."

### Living Inside the Contradiction

He tied the whole Q&A together with the paradox of "if I am not for myself, who will be for me; and if I am only for myself, what am I":

> "If I'm not for myself, then who am I? If there's none else besides Him, there's no longer any choice. If I'm not for me, then who? Then there is choice... They're not hiding this from you; they're preparing you for the true choice, which is something you don't know yet. It'll come."

He closed with a warning about knowing this intellectually without living it: a person whose "knowledge is greater than his deeds... becomes dumb, stupid, because he's just following some slogan he heard," disconnected from what is actually wise.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. A created being is not the same as a deficiency — it requires a screen (Masach): the Creator built in two opposite qualities at once, and comparing them is what produces genuine, determined separateness.

2. The deficiency involved must be specific — to feel the Creator's presence and to determine one's relation to Him — not just any lack; three things (deficiency, pleasure, and a determined relationship) must exist together for a created being to exist at all.

3. Israel and "the nations of the world" are defined by felt brokenness, not by any external marker: Israel is the desire that feels itself opposite the Creator and takes equivalence of form as its goal; the nations of the world are desires that don't feel that break and so don't yearn for correction.

4. Angels are not independent beings but temporary, specific forces generated from within the will-to-receive-and-light system — both restraining and advancing forces are needed together to keep a person's progress controlled.

5. Correcting the intention to bestowal does not by itself remove shame, because the underlying vessel remains a vessel of reception — full correction requires reworking one's own ("layperson's") vessels, illustrated by the king's allegory of a meal prepared for his beloved.

6. Contentment means losing the sense of an individual "I" inside the Creator's own desire to give — a wholly different vessel than that of the created being — even though each person's distinct "point of existence from absence" still keeps their experience singular.

7. From the outside, any system — a cell, a computer, a human body, even whole worlds and souls — behaves as a fixed, predetermined system with no free choice; genuine freedom ("above reason") exists only in the spiritual world, as the will to receive's capacity to choose, in a given state, to want to rise above itself.

8. Real free choice must be lived as a felt contradiction rather than resolved intellectually — behaving as though one chooses freely at one's own degree while recognizing the larger system is determined; knowing this without living it ("knowledge greater than deeds") is what Rav Laitman calls foolishness, not wisdom.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Masach (Screen) | The comparison of two opposite qualities that produces a genuinely determined, separate created being; here identified directly with "intention." |
| Created Being | An independent force with a determined relation to the Creator, arising only from a unique deficiency, pleasure, and a screen existing together — not from deficiency alone. |
| Yashar-El (Israel) | The desire that feels itself opposite the Creator and agrees to receive the force of correction — traced to "straight to the Creator." |
| Nations of the World | Desires that do not feel themselves opposite/broken from the Creator, and so do not yearn for correction. |
| Angels | Temporary, specific forces generated from within the will-to-receive-and-light system (not separate beings) that restrain or advance a person's progress toward the goal. |
| Vessels of Bestowal (layperson's vs. higher) | The hierarchy of corrected vessels; correcting one's intention alone doesn't remove the shame of the underlying vessel of reception — the layperson's vessels must be corrected first. |
| Contentment | Feeling the Emanator's own desire to give, once the sense of an individual "I" has disappeared into it. |
| Above Reason | The one genuine free choice available to a created being — not with respect to the Creator, but with respect to itself — exercised only in the spiritual world. |

### Opening

After re-reading the article on counting and blessing (covered in the companion guide for the morning's first session), Rav Laitman moves into his own lecture, opening with concealment: the Creator hides Himself so the created being can do independent work and grow real vessels. From there, a student raises a pointed challenge that drives the rest of the session: if pleasure and reception are the problem, why doesn't Kabbalah simply recommend renouncing worldly pleasure altogether, the way ascetic paths do? The lesson's answer reshapes the entire relationship between pleasure, intention, and correction.

---

## Part 1 — Concealment, Screens, and the Building of Degrees

Rav Laitman opens by explaining why the Creator's state is hidden: if a person were not required to do the whole process through his own labor, he would never acquire real vessels of wholeness and eternity. A person's senses simply don't register the state he is actually in — like a rich brother's money sitting untouched in a poor brother's bank account (recalling the article's own image).

**The mechanism of ascent:** rather than only the Creator hiding from the person, a person can build his own screen — hiding his will to receive from the light so he is affected only by the purpose of creation, not by raw pleasure. To the extent a person builds such a screen, the Creator correspondingly reveals more of Himself. This alternating concealment and revelation between the person and the Creator is what builds, degree by degree, the vessels that eventually allow full closeness to the Creator.

### Two Stages of Correction

- First, a person acquires a screen over the light of Chassidim (mercy) — the vessels of bestowal, called "Israel," yearning purely for connection with the Creator.
- Only afterward does a person begin working with vessels of reception (called "the nations of the world") — taking those desires and binding them to the vessels of bestowal, correcting them at every degree.

## Part 2 — The Central Question: Why Not Simply Renounce Pleasure?

### The Yogi Challenge

A student poses a sharp, practical challenge: why not follow the ascetic path — enter a kind of shell, cancel all reception, take only minimal bread and water, and gradually work only in order to bestow? This is how most religions and Eastern methods operate, and it sounds entirely reasonable.

### Why Kabbalah's Answer Is Different

Rav Laitman's answer is direct: **Kabbalah does not even count worldly pleasures as "pleasure" in its own terms** — the wisdom of Kabbalah begins beyond the machsom (barrier), addressing only spiritual pleasure: the light, and whether it is received in order to receive or in order to bestow. Whether a person eats simply or feasts, fasts or dines in restaurants, is irrelevant to spiritual standing — what matters entirely is the intention accompanying the action, not its magnitude.

> *"You cannot measure according to what a person receives, the way that he relates to things... All the work is in the thought."*

### The Danger of Ascetic Practice

Rav Laitman warns that renouncing worldly pleasure on principle is actually a trap: a person who deprives himself starts to feel he needs nothing — not even the Creator — and becomes proud of his "holiness," which halts spiritual progress rather than advancing it. He recalls a man in Bnei Brak who wore shoes without soles to suffer in secret: *"It doesn't correct. Besides the light, nothing."* Judaism's actual practice, by contrast, expects a person to marry, raise a family, eat, drink, and lack nothing — abstention is treated as a special, separate corrective measure, not a general spiritual virtue.

### Historical Examples

Kabbalists themselves lived across the full range of circumstance — some poor, some very wealthy (the Ari was a merchant; Rabash lived simply for years and later "wouldn't spare any pleasure"). External lifestyle carried no bearing on spiritual attainment; only intention did.

## Part 3 — What Actually Changes Across a Person's Degrees

### The Skeleton and the Flesh

Rav Laitman offers an image: a person's original ten Sefirot (his root from the soul of Adam HaRishon) are like an unchanging skeleton — what changes from degree to degree is the "flesh" clothing it: the desire and impression a person takes from a shared spiritual state, the way different guests at the same ball each enjoy it according to their own nature. At every degree, a person acquires new vessels of bestowal and reception appropriate to that degree, works with them, and moves on.

### Envy, Lust, and Honor as Tools

Rav Laitman explains (citing Baal HaSulam's introduction to *Panim Meirot U'Masbirot*) that the three tendencies of envy, lust, and honor are what move a person from bodily to human pleasures, and can — used correctly — accelerate spiritual development as well:

- **Destructive envy:** wanting someone else to *not* have something good.
- **Constructive envy:** seeing someone's good state and wanting that same good for oneself — which motivates advancement, "so I'll be even greater."

The same three forces, redirected, can eventually operate toward the Creator as well ("the glory of heaven").

## Part 4 — Concealment, Transgression, and Two Kinds of Love

### Double Concealment and Regular Concealment

Rav Laitman describes two forms of error prior to full revelation: acting with malice under double concealment (with no sense of the surrounding light at all), versus mistaken intention (*shgaga*) once the surrounding light begins to illuminate a person a little.

### Reward and Punishment vs. Eternal Love

Beyond the machsom, Rav Laitman distinguishes two further degrees:

| Degree | Description |
|---|---|
| **Reward and Punishment** | Acting in order to bestow, but still out of a form of fear — fear "at the expense of the Creator" rather than oneself, but fear nonetheless; actions still calculated against consequence. |
| **Eternal Love** | Knowing the Creator loves without any calculation or limitation; reward and punishment disappear as categories, and the driving structure of the work changes entirely — a state the lesson admits is very difficult to describe or fully understand from the current degree. |

---

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Concealment exists so a person can build real vessels through his own labor** — as a person builds a screen, the Creator correspondingly reveals more of Himself, degree by degree.
2. **Kabbalah does not ask a person to renounce worldly pleasure.** Unlike ascetic methods, it holds that pleasure's size or presence is irrelevant — only the accompanying intention matters.
3. **Ascetic self-denial is treated as a real spiritual danger, not a virtue** — it breeds pride and a false sense of needing nothing, which halts rather than advances progress.
4. **A person's original "ten Sefirot" (root) never change** — what changes from degree to degree is the "flesh," the desire and impression clothing that root at each new stage.
5. **Envy, lust, and honor can be used constructively** — wanting the same good you see in another, rather than wanting them not to have it, is a legitimate engine of advancement.
6. **There are stages beyond stages:** even beyond the machsom, "reward and punishment" (fear-based bestowal) precedes the far harder-to-describe state of "eternal love," where calculation itself disappears.
7. **Historical Kabbalists lived in every kind of material circumstance** — wealth or poverty carried no bearing on spiritual attainment; only the intention behind one's actions did.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Screen (Masach)** | The faculty a person builds to hide the will to receive from raw pleasure, allowing reception only for the purpose of creation; building it draws corresponding revelation from the Creator. |
| **Vessels of Bestowal ("Israel") / Vessels of Reception ("Nations of the World")** | The two categories of desire a person must correct at every degree — first bestowal, then reception bound to it. |
| **Machsom (Barrier)** | The threshold past which Kabbalah's actual subject matter (spiritual pleasure, light received in order to bestow) begins; worldly pleasure is outside its concern entirely. |
| **Original Ten Sefirot** | A person's unchanging spiritual root, likened to a skeleton; the "flesh" (desire/impression) clothing it changes from degree to degree. |
| **Constructive vs. Destructive Envy** | Destructive: wanting another not to have good. Constructive: wanting the same good you see in another — an engine of advancement when directed correctly. |
| **Double Concealment / Shgaga** | Acting with malice under total concealment, versus mistaken intention once the surrounding light begins to illuminate a person slightly. |
| **Reward and Punishment (as a spiritual degree)** | Bestowal still structured around fear of consequence (even fear "for the Creator's sake"), preceding the higher state of eternal love. |
| **Eternal Love** | A degree beyond reward and punishment in which the Creator's love is known to be unconditional; the categories of calculation that structure ordinary work disappear. |

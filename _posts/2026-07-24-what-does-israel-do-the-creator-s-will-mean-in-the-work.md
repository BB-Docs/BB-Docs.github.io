### Opening

After a short personal reflection carried over from the prior session, the group turns to an archival lesson: Rav Laitman's own July 7, 2002 teaching on Rabash's article about what it means for "Israel" to do the Creator's will. The lesson opens by drawing a hard line between the "landlord" (Baal Bait) — someone who works straightforwardly for reward, according to his own nature — and the "real worker," who acquires a screen and works against nature, aiming only to bestow. From there the teaching turns practical and personal: how does a person check whether he's actually progressing toward "in order to bestow," without either despairing at how much evil keeps surfacing, or turning that struggle into a source of pride? The live Q&A that follows takes this into two concrete directions: what can a person actually *do* for a friend, even with mixed motives, and could society's role — the recognition of evil, the sense of the goal's importance — simply be purchased?

## Part 1 — The Landlord and the Real Worker

The lesson opens with a distinction Rav Laitman draws directly from the article:

> "A landlord is someone who goes straight, directly according to his nature, according to the will to receive that the Creator created in him. Openly, before his eyes, he works and demands reward, and works according to reward and punishment, and understands that the way he works is all well and good. To this extent, we know it is possible to reveal very small pleasures in the will to receive — only what can be received in order to receive. In the end, the pleasures are called corporeal or animal pleasures, called that because of the intention to receive. This is how the entire world runs after these pleasures, knowing that according to the effort, it will have reward according to its labor."

Against this stands the "real worker":

> "Those who enter into real work do not work according to the will to receive and the pleasure opposite it, but go to acquire a screen that separates the will to receive from pleasure, and work according to the principle of how much it is possible to bestow — in a way opposite to the initial nature that exists in reality."

The practical difference: the landlord earns more the harder he works his will to receive. The real worker, developing a will to bestow, has to examine his results through the eyes of bestowal — not through the eyes of reception.

## Part 2 — You Can't Check One Vessel Against Another

Rav Laitman names a confusion almost everyone hits: a person is in a group, a study, a method, building a vessel of bestowal — but keeps measuring that vessel against his old standard, the will to receive.

> "I am in a society, a method, a study, a guidance in which I'm going to build a vessel of the will to bestow. But I check this vessel by how much I receive according to my initial nature, the will to receive — and then, of course, I find I have nothing. Even worse, every single day I feel myself becoming emptier."

His response is that this isn't a sign of failure — it's simply the wrong ruler:

> "This is correct — because we build one vessel and check its filling according to a second vessel. It won't work."

He ties this back to a working definition of spirituality itself:

> "Spirituality is not that I will have additional pleasures. Spirituality is called 'in order to bestow.' That's the definition of what spirituality is."

## Part 3 — Why Feeling Worse Is the Reward

Rav Laitman reframes the growing sense of one's own ego as good news, not bad:

> "The more he discovers that his nature is worse — that in his will to receive he is more egoistic each time and farther from the will to bestow — he should see that a vessel is being revealed to him, and that is his reward. He should be happy about it."

He backs this with a quote from Baal HaSulam:

> "As Baal HaSulam says in the introduction to the Study of the Ten Sefirot — only the heroes whose patience stood by them defeated the guards who stand at the entrance, and so entered the king's palace, only through patience: continuing to discover the evil, continuing to feel that it's unpleasant, that one is weak — but seeing this each time as a good result, an addition to the vessels of bestowal."

But he's careful about *how* this revealed weakness should be held. It shouldn't become something sweetened, and it shouldn't become a badge of pride either:

> "The whole path really needs to be such that in our society we value the revelation of evil — not that the evil itself should become sweet or good for us. No. Otherwise it would no longer be evil. Rather, the revelation of weakness should be, for us, a sign of goodness. We should not take pride in it — otherwise we turn it once again into a shell, a Klipa. And we should not rejoice in it either, the way one might enjoy suffering, as a kind of masochism — that too is a shell. It simply needs to be an appreciation of what is happening now: that new stages toward 'in order to bestow' are being revealed to me."

## Part 4 — Living by Signs You Don't Yet Understand

Rav Laitman is candid that "in order to bestow" remains, for now, mostly a phrase rather than a felt reality:

> "We do not know what 'in order to bestow' is, and in general, what we are heading toward. We do not know. But according to what is being revealed to me on the path, I see that I am heading toward something that is the opposite of nature. I don't know how it operates on me — they say the surrounding light operates on me — I also don't know how it suddenly changes, or why it even comes to me, through what efforts. I don't really know. But you see that it's happening."

What can be tracked, he says, are the signs themselves — and the signs are simply the evil being revealed:

> "What matters is that you see signs along the way — these signs are the revelation of the evil within you. You already feel the will to receive as evil, and that's something you begin to suffer from — to be ashamed of, so to speak."

He draws a careful distinction between being happy about the *fact* that truth is being revealed, and wanting to stay comfortably stuck in a low state:

> "If you look at all these things as signs given to you — an instruction manual that says this is how it should be — then you can already be happy about it. Not happy that you're confused, or despairing, or lacking strength, but that the state itself, the truth, is being revealed to you. But if you're happy about the state itself in the sense that you don't want to continue, then you're a fool sitting with folded hands, eating his own flesh. It's not that I'm dissatisfied with this place — I'm grateful this state has come to me. From here, I can continue."

And he grounds the whole discussion in why any of this is even intelligible before "in order to bestow" is actually attained:

> "Although I'm still in the will to receive, I already understand what it means to bestow, in contrast to it. Why do I understand? Because I'm already beginning to see that I suffer from the will to receive in some way — and that's only because, in contrast to it, the light has been revealed. The difference between the qualities of the light and the qualities of my will to receive evokes in me the feeling of shame, the feeling of suffering."

## Part 5 — Q&A: What Can I Actually Do for a Friend?

A student asked what concrete actions — even artificial ones — a person can take toward a friend that resemble bestowal. Rav Laitman's answer stayed deliberately small and practical:

> "If, let's say, I like flowers, you could bring me flowers. If I can't get up to study, you wake me up, you bring me to study. If you know it will be good for me to feel some kind of reminder during the day, a warm reminder — then call me, remind me. Anything that can be beneficial for the sake of the goal."

He didn't treat mixed or self-interested motives as disqualifying:

> "The fact that I now want to achieve the goal through my egoism — it's correct. So what? If I want to use the egoism, what else can I use? What else do I have at my disposal? Only that. I should not be ashamed of it. I should operate as I am, with all my power in the right direction, as much as I understand it. And this will be revealed to me bit by bit — both the direction, and the power of my ego, and its willingness to go or not. This is called Lo Lishma."

To explain how real annulment before a friend becomes possible, he reached for examples of total dependency:

> "If I'm in some elite commando unit, and we truly depend on each other for life or death, then I have no choice, and I don't have a problem annulling myself before the others. This was true for me at several times in my life, in states where my life depended on other people — I annulled before them, and I even felt joy in it. Even in a hospital, let's say — someone comes to me and says, 'I'm not going to put you to sleep, I'm going to cut you open, I'm going to do some work in your body, correct you, and you'll be healthy, I'll save your life' — I kiss his hands, I pay him money, I want him to just go ahead and do it. Do I have a problem with it? I want to be his friend — he's bringing me to life. So my ego is working full volume just to connect to him. I search for how I can do good to him — so long as he'll save me."

He added a smaller, everyday version of the same instinct: when his daughter calls and asks him for something, in that moment he is simply "Daddy" — nothing else about him matters.

The point, applied to the group: this same inner awareness — "without him, I'm not going to make it" — needs to be cultivated toward a friend regarding the spiritual goal. Even where a person privately believes a friend is mistaken about something, and has no rational way to bridge that gap, he still has to annul himself before the friend, because the importance of the goal outweighs his individual ego.

## Part 6 — Q&A: Could You Just Pay People to Encourage You?

A second student pushed the logic further: if society is the biggest factor in a person's progress, and progress specifically comes through the recognition of evil, could both simply be bought? Could you hire people to shout that the Creator is great — or, alternatively, hire people to constantly berate you as "a piece of garbage, a little creature of reception," since the will to receive is just impressed by what it sees and hears, regardless of motive?

Rav Laitman's answer distinguished sharply between the two halves of the question. On the first — paid encouragement — he said it would, in principle, actually work:

> "All in all, I'm now in my will to receive, in my 'beast' — and my beast is impressed by what it hears, what it sees. If I could buy, with money, a million people who would constantly shout that the work of the Creator is important, and act before me as though they were truly worshipping — I pay them, I know in advance they're doing it only for the money — I would still be influenced by them. They give me strength, they give me an impression, an upliftment — I would truly go and acquire spirituality, and they'd earn their paycheck."

That, he said, is essentially what a group already does — the only difference is who's in it:

> "This is what we do in a society. Otherwise, we'd need around us the group of Rabbi Shimon, or people like that. And here they say, no — take ordinary people, just like you, who want to go toward the goal and make this effort together."

But on the second half of the question — paying people to shame or shout at you — he drew a firm line:

> "The recognition of evil should be the result of the influence of the surrounding light — not the result of someone shouting at you. If I yell at you that you're bad, you won't feel that you're bad — it won't help. What society does is stimulate you, provoke you to become more sensitive to the surrounding light, so that you draw more of it, discover more of it — and you should feel the results only in your own effort toward it. Shouting 'bad, bad, bad' at someone sometimes helps a little, in a human way, but our advancement should never come from negation, only from the positive side — from being drawn to the goal. And when it shines, according to the measure of my focus on the goal, I see the evil in me. From friends, I only need to receive the awareness of the importance of the Creator. That is all. This will never bring about real love of friends, or the correct result."

Closing out the session, one participant summarized the takeaway in almost the same words: the revelation of evil should be the result of the surrounding light, not of someone shouting at you — from friends, all that's genuinely needed is the shared awareness of the goal's importance. (The recording cuts off shortly after, mid-transition to another example from the article.)

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. Two paths of labor: the "landlord" (Baal Bait) works straightforwardly for reward, according to his own nature; the real worker acquires a screen and works against nature, measuring progress by the will to bestow rather than the will to receive.

2. A vessel being built toward bestowal can't be checked against the standard of the will to receive — feeling emptier while building it isn't failure, it's simply the wrong ruler being applied.

3. Spirituality isn't extra pleasure; it's defined precisely as "in order to bestow." Discovering one's own worsening nature is itself the reward, since it signals a vessel is being revealed.

4. The right response to revealed evil is neither pride (which turns it into a shell) nor taking pleasure in the suffering itself (also a shell) — just plain appreciation that a new stage toward bestowal is being uncovered.

5. A person can't know in advance what "in order to bestow" truly is, or how the surrounding light works on them — only the signs along the way, each new revelation of evil, confirm the direction.

6. Concrete, even egoistically motivated, actions toward a friend (Lo Lishma) are legitimate steps; real annulment of ego before another person follows naturally once the stakes of dependence are truly felt, as in the commando-unit and surgery examples.

7. The recognition of evil has to come from the surrounding light drawing a person toward the goal — not from being shamed or shouted at by others. From friends, all that's genuinely needed is a shared, reinforced sense of the goal's importance.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Baal Bait (Baalei Batim) | "Homeowner(s)" — Rabash's term for a person who works straightforwardly according to his own nature, openly seeking reward and punishment; contrasted here with the "real worker" who acquires a screen and works against nature, in order to bestow. |
| Screen (Masach) | The acquired capacity to receive in order to bestow; described here as what separates the will to receive from pleasure, letting a person work by the principle of how much he can bestow rather than how much he can receive. |
| Lo Lishma | "Not for Her sake" — working toward the goal while still using one's own egoistic motivation; treated in this lesson as a legitimate, even necessary, starting point rather than something to be ashamed of. |
| Klipa / Klipot | Shell(s) — here used to describe two traps in how a person relates to revealed evil: taking pride in overcoming it, or taking pleasure in the suffering itself; both turn the revelation back into a shell. |
| Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif) | The light not yet inside a person's vessel that nonetheless works on him from outside; identified here as the true source of the recognition of evil, as opposed to criticism or shouting from other people. |
| Machsom | The barrier crossed once a person's entire vessel of the will to receive has been revealed and regretted, and he cries out for a screen to be different. |
| Baal HaSulam | Rabbi Yehuda Ashlag, author of the Sulam commentary on the Zohar and founder of this teaching lineage; quoted here from the Introduction to the Study of the Ten Sefirot on patience defeating the "guards at the entrance." |
| Rabash | Rabbi Baruch Shalom Ashlag, author of the article studied in this lesson ("What Does 'Israel Do the Creator's Will' Mean in the Work," Article No. 32, 1990). |

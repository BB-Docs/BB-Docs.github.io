### Opening

After the group reads the Rabash article on Jacob's vow and the importance of faith, Rav Laitman turns to a recorded lesson from February 11, 2002. A student's opening question sets the direction for the whole session: if the Creator "created the evil inclination and also created the Torah as a spice" to correct it, what actually *is* Torah in this world — what is a person supposed to do with it, moment to moment, to draw the light that corrects? The lecture and Q&A that follow work through this in stages: why Kabbalah demands radical simplicity, how ordinary "actions of mitzvah" draw the reforming light, why study must be inseparable from a genuine demand/prayer, how root-and-branch determines which actions can be aimed at the goal, and what a dream and true sleep actually are in the Work.

---

## Part 1 — Kabbalah Demands Simplicity, Not Complication

### Why Straightforward Language Feels Unpleasant

Rav Laitman opens by naming a basic difficulty: Kabbalah requires a person to be simple and unconfused, and this itself is hard, because in our world we dress every concept in layers of clothing and blur our own understanding. Kabbalah's directness — calling things exactly what they are — makes people uncomfortable precisely because it strips away the clothing we're used to.

He illustrates this with a story: telling a class of teachers plainly that "your desire is a beastly desire" caused them to react as if he were uncivilized and disrespectful — "their jaw dropped" — and they walked away.

### Root and Branch Requires Precision, Not Politeness

Kabbalah's terminology has to be maximally precise, even when the precision sounds crude — Rav Laitman cites Baal HaSulam's insistence that if a root is called "coupling," "kissing," or "embryo," the corresponding branch in this world must be named exactly that, with no substitution. Words can feel pleasant or unpleasant to us, but the Creator has no such reaction:

> The Creator doesn't have any pleasant or unpleasant things from the state of wholeness. You see the lowest, most gross things in this world in complete wholeness, and nothing is inferior to the other thing.

**The diagnostic:** if a person is disgusted by some phenomenon, that disgust is a sign that the vessels with which he perceives it are not yet corrected — not a fact about the thing itself.

## Part 2 — The Spice of Torah and the Five Worlds

### What "Torah as a Spice" Actually Means

A student's opening question — what is Torah in this world, if the Creator "created the evil inclination and the spice of Torah" to fix it — is answered directly: the "spice" is a force, a light, that descends and reforms a person when he performs an "action of mitzvah." An action of mitzvah is any action that draws this reforming light down; a mitzvah, in this sense, is a personal correction of some particular quality in a person, not fundamentally different in kind from one action to the next, only in scale and depth.

### Five Degrees, Five Depths of the Same 613 Desires

Rav Laitman describes the same 613 desires of the soul as existing at five successive depths, corresponding to the five worlds:

| World | Light Received | What Deepens |
|---|---|---|
| Asiyah (root/coarseness) | Nefesh | Correction of desires at the surface |
| Yetzirah | Ruach | A greater light draws a deeper layer of the same desires |
| Beriah | Neshama | Deeper still |
| Atzilut | Chaya | Deeper still |
| Adam Kadmon / Ein Sof | Yechida | The end of correction — the light that completes it is called Messiah |

Correcting only the surface layer yields the light of Nefesh; going deeper into the same 613 desires requires drawing a correspondingly greater light, and so on through all five degrees. This progression is called the Naranhai (Nefesh-Ruach-Neshama-Chaya-Yechida) of the soul.

### Before All Degrees: The Point in the Heart

Prior to entering this ladder, a person is in a state Rav Laitman calls "this world" — the point in the heart exists but has not yet developed into an organized structure of discernible desires. A person at this stage still performs "actions of mitzvah" (reading articles, working in the kitchen, working on love of friends, preparing all kinds of deficiencies) even though he cannot yet distinguish which of his 613 desires each action is correcting. Rav Laitman compares this to an embryo passing through its stages without knowing how much has been completed — until, without being told, the process concludes and he is ready to "enter as an embryo" into the actual work of Asiyah of Kedusha.

## Part 3 — Prayer Is the Actual Content of the Study

### The World Believes It Can Act Alone

Asked about the difference between preparing for prayer and prayer itself, Rav Laitman draws out the deeper point: the defining difference between spiritual work and how "the whole world" operates is that the world believes it can accomplish things by its own effort. In the Work, by contrast, all of a person's labor, mind, and self-control has to be directed at gathering up his states — the confusion, difficulty, and suffering he encounters — and consolidating them into a single, correct request. The demand itself is the effort; nothing is actually accomplished by the person "doing" on his own.

### Preparation for Study Is Itself Prayer

Rav Laitman states plainly that when a person prepares himself before study, that preparation already *is* the prayer — there is no separate, lesser phase before the "real" prayer begins. What he needs to be asking, while reading the article and while studying more directly, is for the reforming light to come and correct him. The article's role is to awaken that demand from multiple angles — the Creator's greatness, one's own relationship to Him — so the person carries it forward into deeper study.

> Whatever we can do is that during the study, from the beginning of it till the end, you will just demand and demand and demand. And the study itself, if you can focus on it, great. If not, also not that bad — as long as the demand, the prayer, is burning in you.

### Study Without Friends Is Just Wisdom

Rav Laitman is direct that this kind of study has to happen together with friends — otherwise, he says, "you're just reading wisdom, not Torah." Asked online what books to recommend, he says it isn't about which book: opening any text can "do something" to a person only if there is a pledge and a prayer bound up with the act of opening it, sustained through to the end of the study.

## Part 4 — Root and Branch: What Actions Can Be Aimed at the Goal

### Every Kosher Action Can Be Tied to the Goal

Rav Laitman states that any action in this world, so long as it is kosher, can be aimed toward the yearning for adhesion — there is no category of permitted action too small or unrelated to be tied to the goal. But the connection runs through the actual root-and-branch correspondence, not through sentiment:

> You can't say, "Now I'll eat pig with cream, so that I will have the demand as I study to adhere to the Creator." ... It's not the matter of pork, poor pigs in this world — but opposite the root and branch, you can't do it.

### Sacred-Seeming Work vs. Ordinary Work

A student asks whether making explicitly holy items (tefillin, mezuzot, a tallit) connects a person to the root more directly than ordinary labor, like preparing food in the kitchen. Rav Laitman's answer separates the object from the person's inner search: two people can each perform a different action — one directly tied to holy objects, the other, like a shoemaker, doing an entirely mundane trade — and if the shoemaker searches for a connection to the Creator through his coarser will to receive, his preparation and prayer can actually be *more* beneficial, precisely because he is working with coarser material.

### The Coarsest Act Still Follows the Same Principle

Asked where the greatest coarseness in an action can be found, Rav Laitman points to the act of coupling (Zivug) — called the root of all pleasures in this world. Even there, the same principle of root and branch applies: a person accustoms himself to acting for the Creator's sake to the extent he is able, understanding that under concealment he cannot fully act for the sake of heaven, since it is the opening of the eyes to the Creator's greatness that ultimately grants the power to do so — but the effort toward it is still required and still counts.

## Part 5 — What Is a Dream? What Is Real Sleep?

### A Dream Is the Departure of the Mochin

Responding to a student's question about what a dream means in the article (Jacob's dream), Rav Laitman explains that a dream, spiritually, is the departure of the Mochin (the mind/light) — when the current mind departs, a person adheres to a higher degree and receives what belongs there. This is described through a nightly cycle: after Kriat Shema before sleep, "Abba Ve'ima" (father and mother) prepare vessels through the night; with the light of day, those vessels are acquired and corrected through prayer, forming the next day's actual spiritual status.

### Ordinary Dreams vs. the Ascent of the Soul

Rav Laitman is dismissive of the content of ordinary dreams — "our dreams are beastly," and there is nothing to do with them but, as he puts it, take a cold shower. The ascent of the soul during genuine spiritual "sleep" is a different matter entirely: it requires total self-annulment before the higher one, a willingness to give up everything, described as an extreme tension through every part of oneself.

> This is a great effort. And that is called sleep... you're sweating in that dream.

After this annulment, a person is ready to add more of himself into the next, greater degree of annulment — each subsequent degree requiring a greater self-annulment than the one before it.

### Closing: An Unresolved Question on Faith and Revelation

In the brief closing exchange, a student raises the article's closing question directly: if everything about the Creator becomes revealed, what is left for faith to do? Rav Laitman affirms that faith is a force — the force of the Creator's greatness — that helps a person work in order to bestow; the exchange trails off without fully resolving what remains of that force once revelation is complete, leaving the open question the article itself ends on.

### Closing Round: What the Group Took From the Lesson

In brief closing remarks, participants and Rav Laitman restated the session's core points:

- The difference between our work and the world's approach is that the world believes it can act alone, while our work is built entirely on request and prayer.
- Preparation for prayer must itself be a genuine prayer — asking to actually want to pray — not a lesser, separate step.
- Disgust at anything in this world is a sign that one's own vessels for perceiving it are not corrected.
- Any action — kitchen duty included — that can draw the reforming light is an "action of mitzvah."
- Study without prayer is just wisdom; the demand for the reforming light must run from the start of study to its end.
- Real sleep, in the spiritual sense, is a state of total effort and self-annulment, not passivity.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Kabbalah demands radical simplicity.** Its precision can feel crude or unpleasant, but that discomfort is diagnostic — it reveals which of a person's own vessels are still uncorrected, not a flaw in the teaching.
2. **"The spice of Torah" is the reforming light drawn by actions of mitzvah.** Any action that draws this light — study, kitchen work, love of friends — counts, regardless of how humble it looks.
3. **The same 613 desires are corrected at five successive depths** (Nefesh/Asiyah through Yechida/Ein Sof), each requiring a correspondingly greater light to reach a deeper layer of the same desires.
4. **Before entering that ladder, a person works "subconsciously"** — performing actions of mitzvah without yet being able to discern which desire each one corrects, like an embryo completing unseen stages.
5. **Preparation for prayer already is prayer** — there is no separate, lesser phase. The entire value of study lies in a sustained demand for the reforming light, from beginning to end.
6. **Root and branch determines which actions can be tied to the spiritual goal** — any kosher action qualifies, and coarser material (a trade, ordinary food, even the coarsest physical acts) does not disqualify the effort; what matters is the search for connection within it.
7. **A dream is the departure of the Mochin** — adhesion to a higher degree through self-annulment — sharply distinct from the meaningless content of ordinary dreams, which deserve no analysis at all.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Spice of Torah** | The reforming light the Creator provided to correct the evil inclination; drawn through "actions of mitzvah." |
| **Action of Mitzvah** | Any action — study, kitchen work, care for friends — that draws the reforming light and corrects a specific quality in a person. |
| **Naranhai** | The five progressive levels of the soul (Nefesh, Ruach, Neshama, Chaya, Yechida), corresponding to five worlds and five depths of correcting the same 613 desires. |
| **The Point in the Heart** | The earliest, undeveloped spiritual desire in a person, prior to any discernible structure of individual desires. |
| **Mochin** | Lit. "mind"/light of a degree; a dream is described as the departure of the Mochin during adhesion to a higher degree. |
| **Abba Ve'ima (Father and Mother)** | The upper configuration that, in the nightly cycle described, prepares a person's vessels for the following day. |
| **Root and Branch** | The correspondence between a spiritual root and its expression ("branch") in this world; determines precisely which actions can be aimed at a spiritual goal. |
| **Zivug (Coupling)** | Cited as the root of all pleasures in this world — the coarsest possible act to which the same root-and-branch principle still applies. |
| **Demand / Prayer During Study** | The sustained request for the reforming light that must run through an entire study session; without it, study is "just wisdom, not Torah." |
| **Messiah** | The light that completes the correction of all five degrees, described here as ending the whole process of correction. |
| **Concealment** | The state under which a person cannot yet fully act for the Creator's sake, since only the revealed greatness of the Creator can grant the power to do so completely. |

### Opening

The lesson opens with a firm prohibition: a person is forbidden from pointing out another friend's deficiencies, even gently ("come on, what's with you? Why aren't you moving forward?"). The central question the lesson works through: if dwelling on deficiency is the wrong path, where does real spiritual growth actually come from, and how does a person distinguish between a deficiency that drags him down and one that genuinely moves him forward?

---

## Part 1 — Why Pointing Out Deficiency Is the Wrong Path

### Advice from the Shell, Not from Holiness

Rav Laitman states plainly that a person never moves forward *because of* a deficiency — only backward, into deterioration. The idea that a person should dig into his deficiencies and suffer his way toward spirituality is, in his words, "advice from the Klippa, not from Kedusha." A person should never argue with or turn toward the will to receive at all — as though it doesn't exist. Instead, engaging the desire to bestow, recognizing the Creator's greatness, and gratitude are what actually build the deficiencies, strengths, and obstacles a person needs. **The light builds the deficiency; the deficiency does not enlarge itself from within.**

### A Strict Time Limit on Self-Criticism

If a person insists on self-examination, Rav Laitman offers a concrete boundary: at most half an hour a day, and only on condition that the other twenty-three and a half hours are spent solidly in the right line (gratitude, recognition of the Creator's greatness). A group, especially, should never turn collectively toward lamenting its own shortcomings.

> *"We should cry out for Him to reveal Himself, to draw us toward Him... not go to the other side... and say, why aren't you beating me so that I'll leave you?"*

---

## Part 2 — "If You See the Donkey of Your Enemy"

### The Goal Outranks Every Personal Calculation

Rav Laitman draws a direct practical lesson from the verse about helping even an enemy's fallen donkey get back to work: helping a person return toward the goal takes precedence over every other consideration — whether you love or hate him, whether you have unresolved history with him. **"I need to erase the entire past and do only what is necessary to get that donkey back on its feet."** Bringing personal, "animalistic" calculations into spiritual work is described as destructive to the whole endeavor.

- Personal dislike ("I don't like his nose") is one thing.
- Helping someone continue in the work of the Creator is a separate, non-negotiable matter — especially at the exact point where a person feels dislike, since that is where the real work lies.

---

## Part 3 — Two Kinds of Deficiency

### Ordinary Deficiency vs. the Deficiency for the Creator

A question is raised: isn't deficiency itself always negative — so why would anyone need it? Rav Laitman distinguishes two entirely different things:

| Kind of Deficiency | Description |
|---|---|
| **Ordinary deficiency (feeling bad)** | Arises because something is lacking; a person never grows from this kind, though it can later be redirected (the way of Esav, per the lesson's example). |
| **Deficiency for the Creator** | The suffering of love, a yearning to adhere to Him, arising from recognizing His greatness — a wholly different origin and quality. |

**Rabbi Nachman of Breslov** is cited approvingly for always demanding joy — the point being that people are commonly absorbed in petty personal losses ("our little toys") without realizing how much this absorption actually holds them back from the far greater progress available through engaging the Creator's greatness directly.

### The Instruction: Stay in Kedusha

The main instruction offered is never to leave the "domain of holiness" — meaning never to forget that the Creator acts in everything and that a person exists within Him. A person may awaken a small measure of his own disturbances and even work with them, but the key is to remain adhered throughout, turning every added disturbance into fuel for greater adhesion rather than separating from that adhesion to deal with the disturbance on its own.

---

## Part 4 — Where a Useful Deficiency Actually Comes From

Asked whether the desire for spirituality itself counts as a deficiency, Rav Laitman is precise: a useful deficiency for spirituality must be **built by the descending light**, not self-generated ("awakened from your own navel"). A self-generated deficiency leads nowhere — either back to satisfying an ordinary corporeal want, or to a person simply consuming himself in unproductive self-examination.

**Why this matters so much:** he calls this a stumbling block for the overwhelming majority of students — people who fall into "looking at themselves in a black mirror" with melancholy they cannot escape. Even a person who keeps working despite this can still reach the machsom eventually, but the lesson estimates such a person prolongs the process by decades, because the shells (Klipot) stretch a single eternal point into an experience of time by pulling attention into past and future.

---

## Part 5 — Envy and Inspiration as Legitimate Fuel

### Getting Inspired by Others, Not Comparing Down

Rav Laitman describes deliberately looking for people and states that inspire him — people who travel far to attend, work hard all day, or grasp the material more deeply than he does — and allowing himself to envy their strength or understanding as fuel to keep advancing. He gives the example of a mother of several children whose desire for spirituality is so strong she is almost afraid to voice it — an inspiring example of natural instinct being subordinated to spiritual yearning.

### Distancing from Negativity — "Drilling a Hole in the Boat"

The opposite case — a person who is consistently negative or indifferent — calls for real distance: **"why should I catch those viruses from him?"** A negative, unwilling presence in the group is compared to drilling a hole in the boat. This is distinguished carefully from someone who is simply weak or struggling but still wants support: in that case, the instruction is the opposite — to actively help, even physically insisting a struggling friend come to the lesson, rescuing him "on the battlefield" rather than leaving him exposed.

### Distinguishing the Two Cases

> *"If a person is in a bad mood and doesn't want to come out of it, but instead tries to pull me into it as well, then I really need to make a calculation: am I capable of bringing him out of it or not."*

If the friend cannot be helped and risks pulling others down, the group should draw on more support (the lesson compares this to needing several soldiers to carry one wounded man) rather than any single person exhausting himself pointlessly.

---

## Part 6 — What Does "the Greatness of the Creator" Actually Mean?

### Great Means Stronger Than Me

Asked directly what "greatness of the Creator" means, Rav Laitman answers simply: greatness means someone stronger than oneself — not an abstract theological claim, but something a person discovers, sometimes through being "given a few blows." He points to concrete avenues for building this recognition: seeing how completely the Creator organizes every thought and desire, down to their smallest development — and even points to the wonder of studying something like a single living cell or a genome as a way of building genuine appreciation for the intricacy of creation (referencing the older tradition that Kabbalists first mastered the seven sciences before Kabbalah, to build sufficient capacity for this kind of wonder).

### Free Choice: The One Point Where He Is "Absent"

The lesson closes on a sharp formulation: examine every system, thought, feeling, decision, and action, and the Creator is present and operating in all of them — except one single point: **a person's attitude toward Him.** That is the only place free choice actually exists. Seeking within oneself for where the Creator is "not present" leads precisely to this one point — not because He is truly absent, but because this is the one place a person's own determination is asked for.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Dwelling on deficiency is advice from the shell, not holiness.** Growth comes only from engaging the Creator's greatness, not from digging into one's own lack.
2. **"If you see the donkey of your enemy" means the goal outranks every personal grudge or preference** — especially at the exact point of personal dislike, which is where the real work is.
3. **There are two entirely different kinds of deficiency** — the ordinary kind (feeling bad, which never produces growth) and the deficiency for the Creator (yearning built by the light itself).
4. **A useful deficiency must be built by the descending light, not self-generated** — self-manufactured deficiency is described as a stumbling block for the overwhelming majority of students.
5. **Envy of others' spiritual exertion is legitimate fuel**, while sustained exposure to genuine negativity in the group should be avoided — but a struggling, still-willing friend should be actively helped, not abandoned.
6. **"Greatness of the Creator" means, concretely, someone stronger than oneself** — recognized through the intricacy of creation and the way one's whole inner life is governed from outside oneself.
7. **Free choice exists in exactly one place: a person's attitude toward the Creator** — everything else in a person's inner and outer life is, in this account, already determined.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Deficiency Built by the Light** | The genuine, productive form of lack — yearning for adhesion that arises from the descending light itself, not self-generated. |
| **Ordinary Deficiency** | Feeling bad due to lack; does not produce spiritual growth and can trap a person in unproductive self-examination. |
| **Right Line / Left Line** | Right line: gratitude, recognition of the Creator's greatness, wholeness. Left line: self-criticism, disturbance, deficiency — to be engaged only briefly and from a position of strength. |
| **"The Donkey of Your Enemy"** | Torah image used to teach that helping a person return to spiritual work outranks any personal grudge or history. |
| **Drilling a Hole in the Boat** | Image for sustained exposure to a negative, unwilling group member; grounds for deliberate distance. |
| **Kedusha (Holiness) / Klippa (Shell)** | Two opposed sources of "advice" — Kedusha directs toward the Creator's greatness; Klippa directs toward dwelling in deficiency. |
| **Ahavat Aliyah** | Rendered in the lesson as "raising the love" — the practice of adding disturbances to one's adhesion rather than separating from adhesion to deal with them. |
| **Greatness of the Creator** | Concretely defined as "someone stronger than I am" — recognized through the intricacy of creation and the extent to which one's inner life is governed from outside oneself. |
| **Free Choice** | The single point where the Creator is experienced as "absent" — a person's own attitude toward Him; the only place genuine choice exists. |

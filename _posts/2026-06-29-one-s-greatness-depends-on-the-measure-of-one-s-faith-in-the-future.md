### Opening

The lesson opens with a grammatical puzzle from the Zohar. Exodus 15:1 reads "Az Yashir Moshe" — "Then shall Moses sing" — using future tense to describe a song that, in the story, has already been sung; the verse "should have said, Sang." The Zohar insists the future tense is deliberate: Israel are destined to sing this same song again, in the future, at the final redemption. Working outward from there — through a question about why the song is credited to "Moses and the children of Israel" when the Zohar says the song itself belongs to Malchut, the queen, praising the King — the lesson develops a central teaching for the work: a person cannot sustain himself, in any sense, from purely negative truth. Wholeness in the present has to be built on confidence in a future not yet attained.

---

## Part 1 — The Zohar's Question: Why "Shall Sing" and Not "Sang"

### The Queen's Song

The Zohar asks why the verse uses future tense for a song already sung, and answers that it depends on the future — on the time when Israel are destined to praise this same singing again. It adds that "this song" is in female form (grammatically), meaning the song belongs to **Malchut** — the queen praising the King.

**Rabbi Yehuda's question:** If the song belongs to the queen, why does the verse credit it to "Moses and the children of Israel" rather than to Malchut herself? The Zohar's answer: *"Happy are Moses and Israel, for they know how to properly praise the king for the queen."* Moses and Israel are not claiming the song as their own — they know how to credit it correctly, to the queen, on her behalf.

### Moses, the Faithful Shepherd

This is connected to why Moses is called **the faithful shepherd**. Baal HaSulam interpreted that Moses's role was to instill faith in Israel — and faith itself is called Malchut, "kingdom" or "kingship." In other words, Moses instilled the fear of heaven, the kingdom of heaven, in the people. This is the meaning of "they believed in the Lord and in Moses His servant": Moses gave them faith in the Creator.

## Part 2 — Why a Person Needs Both a Lack and a Filling

### Living From Positivity, Not Negativity

One cannot live from negativity — only from positivity. Nourishment is what a person receives and enjoys receiving, and this flows from the purpose of creation itself: the Creator's desire to do good to His creations. A person therefore needs delight and pleasure — a **filling** — with which to satisfy his needs.

But a person also needs a **lack**. Without a lack, there is no place for the light of life to enter. A lack is called a **kli**, a vessel: a desire, a want, a felt absence. The size of the lack is measured by how strongly a person feels the necessity of satisfying it.

### Three Ways a Lack Gets Weakened

The lesson identifies three ways a person's desire for spiritual attainment can wane, even when the underlying need is real:

- **Direct discouragement.** A person tells friends what he is missing, and the friends convince him it's unattainable — weakening his strength to overcome obstacles. As the need starts to look impossible, the desire for it shrinks.
- **Overheard despair.** A person never voices his own need, but overhears friends who have already given up, and their despair affects him anyway — he loses the enthusiasm he had for reaching adhesion quickly.
- **Honest self-analysis that finds only the negative.** Without any outside discouragement, a person examines himself and finds that each time he tries to draw near to holiness, he seems to move backward rather than forward. Seeing only negativity and darkness, he loses his spirit of life — he feels spiritually dead, unable to make a single movement in the work, even though what he is seeing (the recognition of evil) is, in itself, true.

The problem in all three cases is the same: recognizing the truth about one's self-love is necessary, but truth alone is negative, and the body cannot be sustained by negativity. Provision for the body comes specifically from positivity.

## Part 3 — The Right Line: Wholeness Built Honestly

### Two Reasons to Walk the Right Line

A person must walk the **right line** — wholeness — for two reasons: to keep his desire from waning when he hears discouragement, and to receive the vitality that comes specifically from positivity.

### The Paradox

This raises a real difficulty. If a person honestly judges his own work and sees that he is immersed in self-love, how can he be told to feel wholeness — which, judged honestly, looks like a total lie?

### General and Particular Are Equal

The answer draws on a principle: **what applies to the collective applies, in the same way, to the individual.** Regarding the collective, Israel were given the belief in the coming of the Messiah: *"I believe in the coming of the Messiah, and though he may be delayed, I await his arrival still."* One must never give up and conclude that adhesion (**Dvekut**) with the Creator is unobtainable.

If a person believes in the general redemption, the same logic obligates him to believe that redemption will come to him personally. This means picturing, concretely, the goodness, pleasure, and joy he will feel once his needs are satisfied — which itself produces real emotional satisfaction and energy to keep working toward the goal. As the lesson puts it, a person must first know clearly what he is hoping for, so that once he pictures it, he can say wholeheartedly: *"Blessed is he who said, let there be the world"* — because the delight is truly worth receiving, for himself and for all creation.

This is connected to Rabbi Meir's teaching that one who engages in Torah **Lishmah** (for its own sake) is rewarded with a great many things — the whole world becomes worthwhile for him, and he becomes like an everlasting spring.

## Part 4 — Inner Light and Surrounding Light: The Merchant's Confidence

### Two Kinds of Light

Baal HaSulam distinguished between **inner light** (what a person actually receives in the present) and **surrounding light** (the light destined to shine in the future, not yet attained). The key point: surrounding light can shine into the present too — but only to the extent of a person's confidence that he will eventually attain it.

| | Inner Light (Ohr Pnimi) | Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif) |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | What is actually received right now | What is destined for the future, not yet attained |
| When it shines | Only in the present, and only to the extent already attained | Also in the present — in proportion to one's faith and confidence in the future |
| What it gives | Present filling, which by itself can feel like "nothing" | Joy and vitality drawn now from a future not yet seen |

### The Merchant Allegory

The lesson illustrates this with a story: merchandise flooded a market and became worthless — every merchant wanted to sell, but no one wanted to buy, for fear prices would drop further. One man bought it all at the lowest price. Friends mocked him, certain he had ruined himself. He answered:

> *"Now I am happier than ever... I will store it, and take it to market in three years, for by then this merchandise will not be found here, and I will get the price that I want."*

Calculated by what he has in the present, he has nothing to show. But he is genuinely joyful now, because of his certainty about the future. This is the allegory for surrounding light: it shines in the present specifically through one's confidence that the full reward is still coming, "though he may be delayed."

### The Surrounding Becomes Internal

By believing in personal redemption the way one believes in general redemption, a person draws the future nearer to the present — the surrounding light begins to actually clothe in the present, becoming internal. This happens gradually: faith accumulates "penny by penny," joining into a great amount, until a person is rewarded with permanent, whole faith — described as the attainment of the light of **Hassadim** (mercy) within the illumination of **Chochmah** (wisdom), as written in the Sulam Commentary.

---

## Part 5 — Resolving the Question, and a New One Left Open

### Singing Now About the Future

This resolves the opening puzzle. The Zohar's "shall sing," in future tense, points to the fact that the righteous are, in the present, singing about what they will receive in the future. To the extent a person pictures the delight and pleasure coming to him — and believes there really is a future in which everyone will be corrected — he can feel that joy now, and this present feeling is itself the praise he is able to give in the present. As the lesson states it: *"All that is certain to be collected is deemed collected."*

### Why "Female Form" — Returning to the Queen

The lesson then returns to the second half of the original question: why does the Zohar describe this singing as being in **female form**? Because it is Malchut — the queen — who is being praised, and Moses and Israel "know how to properly praise the king for the queen." This raises one further question, which the lesson poses but does not yet answer in this transcript: in ordinary, flesh-and-blood terms, a king needs and enjoys honor from his people — but why would the Creator need praise from creation at all?

*(The transcript ends at this open question.)*

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **A person cannot live from negativity alone.** Honest recognition of one's self-love is necessary, but by itself it provides no nourishment — sustenance for the work comes specifically from positivity.
2. **A lack (kli) is not fixed — it can be weakened.** Direct discouragement from friends, overheard despair, and even one's own honest self-analysis can all shrink the desire needed to keep pursuing a goal.
3. **Wholeness on the right line is not self-deception.** It is grounded in something real — surrounding light, which genuinely shines into the present in proportion to a person's confidence in the future.
4. **"General and particular are equal."** The belief in a general, future redemption (the coming of the Messiah) obligates a person to believe just as firmly in his own personal arrival at the goal, however delayed.
5. **Inner light is what's received now; surrounding light is drawn from the future.** The merchant allegory shows that real joy can be felt in the present purely from confidence in a future gain, even while the present, calculated on its own, shows nothing.
6. **Faith accumulates gradually — "penny by penny" — not all at once,** until it becomes permanent and whole, drawing the surrounding light to actually clothe in the present.
7. **The Zohar's "shall sing" describes praising now for a future not yet seen.** Singing in the present about what is still to come is itself the measure of praise available in the present.

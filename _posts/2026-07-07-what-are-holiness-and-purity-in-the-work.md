### Opening

After the group reads the Rabash article together, Rav Laitman turns to a separately recorded lesson from June 17, 2002. Its organizing question is practical: a person moves constantly between states of ascent and descent — feeling uplifted or feeling remote — and has to figure out what he is actually supposed to *do* in each one, since neither state is a reward or a punishment. The lecture and Q&A trace this through what counts as real work during ascent versus descent, how a group's study amplifies each person's inner light, why suffering is never the goal even when it serves a purpose, the real difference between "feeling bad" and "recognizing evil," and what the shells (klipot) actually are once the Creator's exclusive authority is taken seriously.

---

## Part 1 — Ascent and Descent Are Not Reward and Punishment

### Two States, Two Kinds of Work

Rav Laitman opens by stating the main point: there is work to do during ascent and different work to do during descent, and a person must find what is appropriate for him in each. Mostly the emphasis falls on ascent, because during descent "there is no one to obligate" — a person can be so shut down, internally and externally, that he simply cannot act.

**During ascent:** when a person feels uplifted and it seems he has reached a perfect state, he should not simply enjoy it as if he earned it. He should understand it was given to him from above — just as the descent is also given from above — specifically so he will feel the Creator's qualities a little, and from that, go deeper into the study: delve into the articles and letters he previously could not understand, and extract as much as possible while the illumination lasts.

**During descent:** a person is given a feeling of being remote from the Creator, in the dark, understanding and feeling nothing. Even so, the surrounding light still shines on him — that is precisely what lets him see how far he feels. The instruction is not to sit and study his own vessel (there is nothing to study there), but to act: physical, even mechanical work — in the group, in the kitchen, it doesn't matter what — done with hands and legs alone, without heart or mind engaged, still counts as a true spiritual action, because it is the maximum he is capable of in that state.

> Neither state is reward or punishment for suffering earlier. It's not the correct thought. The process is only in order to feel our own qualities and the qualities of the Creator, so we can advance.

**Why it matters how you relate to it:** if a person passes through these states without purposefully relating them to the purpose of creation, they will simply stop coming — perhaps repeating only once or twice, then arriving only every six months or a year, while time passes.

### Ascent and Descent Are Measured by Yearning, Not Circumstance

Rav Laitman makes clear that ascent and descent are not calculated by how close or far a person objectively stands, or even by "the awakening from above." They are calculated by **the awakening from below** — how much a person yearns to be close or distances himself. It is possible to yearn for the Creator more during a descent than during an ascent, in which case the descent is, functionally, the ascent.

He illustrates this with an example: enjoying an evening playing cards and having a beer with friends, with time passing pleasantly — in the moment, this might be called "an ascent," since it feels good. But waking up afterward and asking "what was I doing, where was I," and beginning to feel that state was actually a descent (because the Creator was absent from it), shows that ascent and descent are measured only by equivalence with the surrounding light — nothing else.

## Part 2 — Studying Together: The Amplifier Effect

### Preparing Before the Study

Rav Laitman notes that a person cannot simply expect to remember, mid-study, that he should feel connected to the whole group and to the Creator through the material. This requires preparation beforehand — worrying, before the study begins, about what he will actually get out of it. That concern is what conditions a person to think correctly once the study is underway.

### Same Words, Different Depths

The same Torah is read every day, yet each reading can reveal something that feels new. Rav Laitman explains this through the image of a child and a Kabbalist (Rabbi Shimon) reading the identical text: the letters and words are the same, but what each one attains from them is entirely different — a child sees only the story ("Abraham wanted to kill Isaac"), while a Kabbalist perceives the spiritual forces the same words are built from. All of reality, he notes, is the ten Sefirot — nothing more — and the difference between people is only the **depth** at which those same ten Sefirot are felt.

### The Group as an Amplifier

When Rav Laitman explains something during the lesson, his intent is to pass his own depth of perception on to the group, adding to the surrounding light everyone draws during study. He describes real group study as everyone intending, while studying, to pass what they grasp to each other — and when that happens, everyone's surrounding light combines and operates on the whole group at once, functioning like an amplifier of spiritual force.

**Every contribution counts, regardless of size:** even someone who understands far less still adds something the others do not have, because each person carries a different root of the soul. Rav Laitman states this applies to himself as well — he receives something from the group's questions and comments that he could not reach without them.

## Part 3 — Receiving Without Resistance, and the Case Against Suffering

### The Question of Resistance

A student (Shapira) asks about the resistance a person feels toward what happens to him, and whether resistance itself is somehow useful or revealing.

### First, Receive Without Judging

Rav Laitman answers that whatever comes should first be accepted as it is — observed and monitored, not immediately judged as good or bad — following the idea that "whatever the Lord does is all for the better," and that everything that comes is meant to correct a person, however severe it feels. Only afterward does one relate it consciously to being a correction; it is not that the body's resistance simply vanishes by deciding this — the resistance is a real, growing part of the vessel's development.

### No Commandment to Suffer

Rav Laitman states plainly that suffering is never required, "not even for a second," and that it is a mistake — attributed to the shells — to believe suffering itself produces spiritual attainment. Instead: **"there is a great commandment to be enjoyed."** The moment a person can shift toward joy, he should do so rather than waiting to first feel more suffering, since remaining in suffering does not, by itself, bring anyone closer to correction.

> If, upon feeling suffering or humiliation, you can shift to joy, do it — that is not levity. Returning again and again to suffering, without making the exit from it, is not the path.

### Suffering as a Reminder, Not the Goal

The Creator sometimes uses suffering — described as "a thorn" — to remind a person of the connection when he has drifted (as when absorbed entirely in ordinary life). But the concern is never the felt state itself; it is the danger of forgetting the Creator's existence altogether. Once suffering has served that reminder function, staying in it out of fear of losing the connection is, in Rav Laitman's words, exactly how "the shell" keeps a person captive — the true aim is adhesion through **shared joy**, not through shared pain, since "the pain cannot be shared."

### Building Internal and External Supporters

Because the point of connection is constantly lost and has to be found again, Rav Laitman recommends constructing both an internal practice (returning, in thought, to the point of connection as often as possible) and external supports — reminders placed around oneself. He cautions that a fixed, repeated external signal (like a gong every five minutes) quickly loses its effect once a person gets used to it, so the external supports need to keep changing to keep working.

## Part 4 — Recognition of Evil Is Not the Feeling of Evil

### Mushi's Question

A student (Mushi) points to the article's statement that a person should ask the Creator to feel how evil the will to receive is — and asks why this is the request, when the more familiar teaching is to ask instead for a sense of the Creator's greatness and importance.

### The Answer: Two Different Things

Rav Laitman distinguishes sharply between **feeling bad** and **recognizing evil**:

- **Feeling bad** is something even a beast experiences — it is simply the raw sensation of pain, without any understanding of who delivers it or why.
- **Recognizing evil** requires placing a point of awareness *outside* the body that is feeling the pain, so that the pain can be seen as showing something specific: that a particular quality within the person is what creates distance from the Creator. Recognition of evil is the recognition of *distancing from the Creator*, not a sensation located in an organ.

> "I feel bad" — who feels? My body feels the bad. And I receive this from the Creator. I put my eye outside the Creator and outside my body that feels bad — that is the recognition of evil.

He adds that this exit is only possible by the eye (the point of awareness) drawing closer to the Creator while the body remains where it is; from that vantage point, the person can observe how far the body is from the Creator, which cannot be assessed from inside the bad feeling itself.

### Why Ask for Importance, Not for the Evil Itself

Rav Laitman clarifies that a person should not directly pray to feel more of the bad — he uses the analogy of wanting the "recognition of evil" about smoking while still wanting to keep smoking and enjoying it: the actual process is that a growing sense of how damaging something is builds gradually, from repeated experience, until it becomes intolerable enough that a person is finally willing to leave it. What should be requested is the Creator's greatness and importance; recognition of evil is the byproduct that follows from seeing one's distance from that greatness, not a feeling to be requested directly while still immersed in the bad state.

## Part 5 — The Shells Are Your Own Uncorrected Desires

### What Exists Besides You and the Creator?

A student asks how the Creator acts through the shells (klipot), and how this system works. Rav Laitman's answer is direct: besides the desire to receive that the Creator created, and the light which is the Creator Himself, **nothing else exists.** The klipot are not some external force to be feared or kept at a distance — they are a person's own uncorrected desires, specifically those from after the shattering of the vessels that cannot be corrected until the final correction, and remain in the raw "will to receive" form.

### How the Light Shapes the Vessel

The light reverberates against the vessel (the kli) and this contact is what produces every state a person goes through — there is no other force capable of influencing the vessel. The light itself is described as simple and abstract, like a flashlight, but it passes through a constructed system called the **worlds**, which shapes it precisely to match what each specific part of a person's vessel needs corrected at that moment — delivering exactly the right measure to the right place.

### Why the World of Atzilut Can Be Studied, But Not the Soul Directly

Rav Laitman notes, as an aside he calls genuinely fascinating, that the world of Atzilut is constructed as something like a mirror image of the vessels below it — precisely calibrated to what each soul in the world of Biya needs. Because of this precise correspondence, the world of Atzilut can actually be studied to understand the souls, whereas the souls themselves cannot be studied directly; a person only comes to know himself through his own actions.

## Part 6 — Closing Round: What the Group Took From the Lesson

In the brief closing workshop, participants and Rav Laitman restated the session's core points:

- During study, a person should aim to feel himself as one with the whole group, connected to the Creator through the material studied — this is what should be longed for.
- Group study, with each person intending to pass understanding to the others, functions as a generator/amplifier of spiritual force that uplifts everyone.
- Ascent and descent are not measured by how a state feels, but by the effort exerted in relation to them — particularly, using states of ascent to reinforce oneself (through articles and study) in order to be prepared for the eventual descent.
- In descent, when intention, heart, and mind are unavailable, purely mechanical action (even manual work) still counts as genuine spiritual action, with real value.
- The consistent direction of work is to shift toward joy, since there is no commandment to suffer, and equivalence of form with the Creator is found in joy, not in suffering.
- A person needs to arrange internal and external "supporters" for himself — things that repeatedly bring him back to the point of connection with the Creator.
- Recognition of evil is not the feeling of evil; it is a discernment made from a point of observation, distinguishing this from the simple experience of pain.
- The same Torah yields different discernments to different people (and to the same person at different times) depending on the depth at which it is felt — the words are always old, but the depth revealed is new.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Ascent and descent are not reward and punishment.** Their only purpose is to let a person feel his own qualities and the Creator's qualities, in order to advance — and they are measured by the awakening from below (yearning), not by objective circumstance or feeling.
2. **Mechanical action during descent still counts as real spiritual work**, since it represents the maximum a person is capable of when heart and mind are unavailable to him.
3. **Group study amplifies each person's inner light.** When each participant intends to pass his understanding to the others, everyone's surrounding light combines — and every contributor adds something unique, regardless of how much or little he understands.
4. **There is no commandment to suffer.** Suffering can serve as a reminder of the connection to the Creator, but the actual aim is always adhesion through shared joy — remaining in suffering out of fear of losing the connection is described as the shells' trap.
5. **Recognition of evil is not the feeling of evil.** It requires a point of awareness placed outside the body's sensation, able to see how a specific quality creates distance from the Creator — not merely that something hurts.
6. **The shells (klipot) are one's own uncorrected desires, not an external force.** Besides the will to receive the Creator created, and the light that is the Creator Himself, nothing else exists.
7. **The same words can yield different depths of understanding** depending on how deeply a person can feel the ten Sefirot beneath them — this is why the same Torah study never stops revealing something new.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Ascent / Descent** | States given from above to let a person feel the Creator's qualities (ascent) or his own remoteness (descent); measured by the awakening from below (yearning), not by outward feeling or circumstance. |
| **Awakening from Above / Awakening from Below** | A state given by the Creator versus a person's own yearning and effort; ascent/descent are calculated according to the awakening from below, not the awakening from above. |
| **Surrounding Light (Ohr Makif)** | The illumination drawn during study that a person "adds to" by sharing his understanding with the group; combines across a group into an amplified spiritual force. |
| **Recognition of Evil** | A discernment, made from a point of awareness placed outside the body's own sensation, that identifies how a specific quality creates distance from the Creator — distinct from simply feeling bad. |
| **Point of Awareness (the "eye")** | The inner vantage point that must draw closer to the Creator, separate from the body's sensation, in order to observe how far the body is from the Creator. |
| **Klipot (Shells)** | A person's own uncorrected desires remaining from the shattering of the vessels, not correctable until the final correction; not an external force, since nothing exists besides the will to receive and the Creator's light. |
| **The Worlds** | The constructed system through which the Creator's light passes on its way to the vessel, shaping the light precisely to match what each part of the vessel needs corrected at a given moment. |
| **World of Atzilut** | The world constructed as a precise counterpart to the vessels below it; can be studied to understand the souls, whereas the souls themselves cannot be studied directly. |
| **Adhesion (Dvekut)** | The state of connection with the Creator sought through shared joy rather than shared suffering, since "the pain cannot be shared." |
| **Internal / External Supporters** | Practices and reminders a person deliberately builds for himself — inward returning of thought, and outward signals (which must vary, since fixed ones lose their effect) — to keep bringing him back to the point of connection with the Creator. |
| **Ten Sefirot** | The single underlying structure of all reality, worlds, and souls; different people (or the same person at different times) perceive the same text or reality at different depths of these same ten Sefirot. |

### Opening

The letter itself is Baal HaSulam writing from London to defend his work against slander and opposition back in Jerusalem. Rav Laitman uses it to open a wider question: great Kabbalists, in every generation, are misunderstood and resisted by their own contemporaries — so what, if anything, actually distinguishes a genuine spiritual teacher from someone who only appears to be one? The lesson moves from the historical case (the fractured, generations-long transmission of the Ari's writings down to Baal HaSulam) to a concrete answer, before closing with students reckoning, out loud, with what it means to have received this method at all.

## Part 1 — Why the Great Are Never Believed by Their Own Generation

### Baal HaSulam's Opposition, Then and Now

Rav Laitman opens by noting that Baal HaSulam faced real, sustained opposition from the Orthodox establishment of his day — the "sages of Jerusalem," whether or not they actually understood what he was doing, "greatly resisted what he was doing and what he was writing." He is careful to distinguish that situation from today's: "this generation is engaged in something different altogether — you don't have the same Torah, the same Orthodox public that existed then."

### "There Is No Prophet in His Own Hometown"

The explanation offered is structural, not personal. A person who has actually grown spiritually still looks, physically, like the same person he always was — "you think that it's the same person, because the inanimate doesn't grow." Someone at 20 and the same person decades later appear unchanged to an outside observer who only measures by ordinary, "inanimate" standards, so:

> "One cannot be a prophet in his hometown... the usual people, regular people... engage in their commandments day to day, and this is their liveliness, their spirit. They don't understand the other ways there are to live. This lifestyle of theirs doesn't allow them to think differently about how someone suddenly grows."

### The Humility of the Truly Great

This is paired with an observation about how the great Kabbalists themselves behave — Rav Laitman notes that "as much as I got to know them, they didn't describe any importance to themselves," tying this to the description of Moses as the humblest of his generation, felt by no one else. A person measuring himself against the Creator "always finds himself to be inferior, weak" — so outwardly, a Kabbalist can look unremarkable even while carrying deep attainment, and "the audience judges him... according to each one who judges others, he's judging his own flaws."

### Isolation as Protection, Not Punishment

Rav Laitman reframes the social isolation faced by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, Baal HaSulam, and the Ramchal as something the Creator arranges deliberately: "The Creator... protects His unique, beloved people... so they don't mix with the public... and can advance alone without any influence from the external group." Rabash is quoted making the same point about closeness itself being dangerous: the closer people get to the internality of Torah "seemingly, then you need to stay away from them more and more... so as not to gain from them all their corruptions."

Rav Laitman adds a personal recollection here — the years he spent accompanying Rabash to weddings and social functions Rabash found nearly unbearable: "I had to go with Rabash to weddings... sometimes a few weddings a week... I hated it intensely, it was very difficult for me, and I also saw how much he suffers from this."

## Part 2 — The Fractured Transmission from the Ari to Baal HaSulam

### Why the Ari's Own Writings Were in Disarray

Baal HaSulam's letter explains, and Rav Laitman underscores, that the confusion in the Ari's writings was not the Ari's fault: the Ari himself did not write or arrange his teaching in its full depth — his disciple Chaim Vital wrote it down while only thirty years old and, by his own account, not yet at the level needed to grasp it at its root.

### The Ari's Final Instruction

The letter quotes Chaim Vital's own record of the Ari's deathbed instruction, relayed through Rav Itzhak HaCohen: the Ari told the friends not to engage in the wisdom he taught at all, "for they did not understand it properly. Only Rav Chaim Vital shall engage in it, alone, in a whisper, and in hiding." Asked if there was any hope, the Ari's answer was conditional: "If you merit, I will come to you and teach you." Rav Laitman reads this literally as a test rather than a door closing outright — obedience to the instruction itself, not personal readiness, was what "meriting" required.

### A Chain of Partial Compilers

None of the students who scattered the Ari's writings across the next three generations — Rav Yaakov Tzemach, Rav Meir HaKohen, and Chaim Vital's own son Rav Shmuel Vital — ever held the complete set; 600 pages were reportedly stolen during Chaim Vital's lifetime, and some material was deliberately buried with him. Rav Laitman's summary of the result:

> "Each time the compiler had only a third of all the writings, which together make up one entity and one structure... since they had only a small portion of the writings, they did not understand the depth of the wisdom at that time, and they terribly confused the matters by not understanding how to arrange them."

The point stressed is that this was a failure of attainment, not of intellect — the compilers "had great minds, phenomenal memories, tremendous, but they did not understand the wisdom itself... they connected these things together only by memory... not according to the deep connection that exists" between the texts.

### Baal HaSulam's Claim

Against that backdrop, Baal HaSulam states plainly that his own capacity to reorder the material came from being "rewarded with a conception... of the soul of the Ari, not because of my good deeds, but by a higher will," and that no one had received anything comparable since the Ari's death. Rav Laitman connects this directly to a stated lineage — Abraham, Moses, Rashbi, the Ari, and Baal HaSulam described as manifestations of one returning soul, "each time corresponding to new degrees of coarseness (Aveyut) and to a new stage in the overall development of humanity."

The same pattern, Rav Laitman notes, applies to the Zohar itself: tradition holds that the original text was roughly twenty times the length of what survives today, disordered and full of gaps — so Baal HaSulam's re-ordering of the Ari's material is treated as the same kind of restorative work, not an isolated case.

## Part 3 — What Actually Marks a True Teacher

### Two Criteria That Don't Work

Rav Laitman names, then rejects, the two standards people instinctively reach for. Not miracles or wonders — "neither of these can serve as proof that a person has spiritual attainment." And not eloquence or mastery of the material, illustrated by Baal HaSulam's own account of arriving in Jerusalem and meeting people who knew the Ari's writings by heart, word for word, "but they didn't understand them even a millimeter beyond the superficial meaning."

### The Real Sign, from Chaim Vital

The letter cites Chaim Vital's *Gate of the Holy Spirit* directly for the actual criterion the Ari gave him: a teacher's words must come true and never prove wrong, and — critically — he must actually bring the student to "one letter," meaning the quality of bestowal (Bina, faith) itself, not merely intellectual content. Rav Laitman's gloss on "he doesn't...":

> "He's not exalting himself, he doesn't attract students to himself, but rather he directs the students to make a vessel toward the Creator... he reveals a little more each time, gradually, in a way that helps and supports them at every step along the path."

A genuine teacher also has to know the secrets of Torah well enough to calibrate what he reveals to what each student can actually absorb — never all at once, and never as a display.

### Why We Can't Judge This Directly

Rav Laitman is explicit that this leaves ordinary students with no direct instrument for measuring a teacher's actual spiritual stature: "we don't even enter the same spiritual realm where his degree can be attained." What can be judged is only indirect — "what he teaches his students, how he leads them... by whether he preserves the path as it's been laid out, directing it toward the goal of adhesion with the Creator."

### Intention vs. Action

A related question is raised about judging people generally: since intention seems to matter more than the act, why not judge by intention? Rav Laitman's answer is that intention is unreliable even to the person who holds it — "the intention is the concealed Torah... neither he nor we truly know what his exact intention was... even it's confused, biased, and concealed from him." For that reason, in this world, "judgment is based only according to the act."

## Part 4 — Workshop: Sitting with the Weight of the Method

### The Responsibility of Having Received This

In the closing student workshop, the dominant reaction to the lesson was not intellectual but emotional — described repeatedly as "a huge weight, a huge responsibility." One student compared it to unwrapping a large, unearned gift: "we're opening the box, and now we've got it in front of us — and what are we going to do with it?" Another named the specific feeling as watching the passion Baal HaSulam brought to this work "pass on to us," alongside real uncertainty about being equal to it.

### What About the People Who Aren't Here?

Several students raised concern for family and loved ones outside the group — wanting to "inspire them" without knowing how, and wanting reassurance "that they're not left behind," while acknowledging that certainty isn't available and the feeling itself has to be carried rather than resolved.

### A Personal Testimony: Finding the Right Place

One participant, joining from London, described decades on a Hasidic and Talmudic path (originally among followers of the Baal Shem Tov) before finding Rav Laitman, and continuing to study daily through serious illness and surgeries, though not yet formally in a group. Asked how to know if one has found the right teacher or group at all, Rav Laitman relayed the test Rabash once gave him directly:

> "Search. If you find somewhere else that seems more suitable to you, then apparently it's more suitable. But if you don't find such a place, then this is probably your place... And that is actually the test."

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. Genuine spiritual greatness is systematically misread by contemporaries, because outward, "inanimate" appearance doesn't change even as inner spiritual growth does — this is why prophets and great Kabbalists are rejected in their own hometowns and generations.

2. The truly great tend to be the most self-effacing, not the most self-important — humility (modeled on Moses) is treated as a mark of real attainment, not an accident of temperament.

3. Social isolation and the friction of public life were not incidental hardships for Baal HaSulam and Rabash but a mechanism the Creator uses to keep His close ones from being absorbed into the surrounding public.

4. The Ari's teaching reached Baal HaSulam only through a broken chain of partial compilers across three generations, each holding a fragment and lacking the attainment to reassemble it correctly — mirroring the Zohar's own fragmentary survival.

5. Baal HaSulam's authority is grounded not in personal merit but in a claimed transmission of the Ari's soul, part of a longer lineage (Abraham, Moses, Rashbi, the Ari) recurring across history to match each generation's spiritual capacity.

6. A true teacher cannot be identified by miracles or by verbal/scholarly mastery — the only real sign is whether he actually brings a student to the quality of bestowal, calibrated to what that student can absorb, without seeking followers for himself.

7. Because students cannot directly perceive a teacher's spiritual level, judgment is necessarily indirect — based on how he leads and whether the path stays oriented toward the Creator — just as, more generally, people are judged only by action, since intention is concealed even from themselves.

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Baal HaSulam | Rabbi Yehuda Ashlag, author of the Sulam commentary on the Zohar; here defending his work and claiming direct transmission of the Ari's soul. |
| Ari (Rabbi Isaac Luria) | 16th-century Kabbalist whose teaching was left in disarray at his death and only fully reordered generations later by Baal HaSulam. |
| Chaim Vital | The Ari's principal disciple; recorded the Ari's teaching while still young, and was instructed by the Ari to keep it hidden. |
| Rav Itzhak HaCohen | Disciple present at the Ari's passing; his account of the Ari's final words is quoted via Chaim Vital's writings. |
| Sitra Achra | "The other side" — the opposing force behind slander and obstruction against genuine spiritual work. |
| Aveyut | Coarseness/thickness of the will to receive — the degree of desire that increases across generations, matched by each era's teaching. |
| Pri Chacham | Collection of Baal HaSulam's letters (including Letter 39, the source read in this lesson). |
| Zohar | Foundational mystical text; noted here as itself surviving only in fragmentary, reordered form — about a twentieth of its original length. |
| One Letter (Bina/Faith) | Chaim Vital's term, via the Ari, for the quality of bestowal a true teacher must actually transmit — the real sign of a genuine Rav. |
| Judging by Action, Not Intention | The principle that people (and teachers) can only be evaluated by their deeds, since intention is concealed even from the person who holds it. |

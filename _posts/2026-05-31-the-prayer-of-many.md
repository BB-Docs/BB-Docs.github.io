### Opening: How Does "My" Prayer Become "Our" Prayer?

This session — held live across the World Kli Congress with hundreds of "tens" (small groups) connected simultaneously — addresses one central question: how does a person's private request to the Creator turn into the "prayer of many," a prayer that actually rises to Him and receives an answer? Rav Laitman uses the day to walk students through what prayer really is, why an individual's prayer alone accomplishes little, and how a group can build one genuine, shared request out of everyone's separate desires. The lesson unfolds in two parts: a live Q&A session with group representatives from around the world, followed by a facilitated workshop that puts the teaching into practice, ending in a mass collective prayer.

---

## Part 1 — What Prayer Actually Is

### Judging Oneself, Not Reciting Words

Rav Laitman opens by redefining prayer away from any set of words or ritual. In Hebrew, "to pray" — *lehitpallel* — reflexively means to judge oneself:

> "To pray is in that a person judges himself. He wishes to reveal himself. And when he reveals himself, he sees how much he has correct things and how much he does not."

Prayer, in this sense, is a private accounting: examining which desires and thoughts can honestly be brought to the Creator, and which cannot yet. This examination itself is the "raising of a lack" — the beginning of a real request.

### The Problem of the Body's Complaint

A student from Moscow asked what it means to "judge myself from the center of the ten." Rav Laitman answered that judging oneself means checking one's own degree of connection to the group, and building a network of relationship with friends from which a genuine lack for the Creator's revelation can be raised — one aimed at bringing Him contentment, not at self-benefit.

A related question, about whether raising a request one doesn't yet fully feel amounts to "cheating," drew a direct answer:

> "No, I don't wish to hide anything from the Creator... rather I want to be open, revealed to him, and the extent to which I want, don't want, understand, or don't understand, I reveal it all to him... So I'm before him like a sick person, patient on the operating table, completely under the control of the creator."

Honesty about one's actual state — rather than performing a prayer that sounds better than it is — is treated as the starting condition for any real appeal to the Creator.

## Part 2 — Why Prayer Must Rise From the Ten

### The Individual Cannot Pray Alone

Repeatedly, in answer after answer, Rav Laitman returned to one structural point: an individual's prayer does not reach the Creator on its own. It must be built collectively:

> "The prayer cannot rise from me to the creator, but it needs to rise from the entire group, from the entire Kli. The Kli is the 10. And therefore, I need to first prepare myself in connection with friends so that it will be as close as possible to a 10."

Practically, this means the "raising of a lack" is not something to manufacture privately. It grows out of ordinary connection — study, conversation, and mutual support between friends throughout the day — from which a shared deficiency naturally becomes visible. As Rav Laitman put it to one group: "the more we in study and speech between us... can reveal what exactly do we want... so accordingly we turn to the Creator."

### What the Creator Actually Hears

Several questions probed exactly what reaches the Creator. Rav Laitman's answer was consistent: the Creator responds only to a lack generated by real integration with one's group —

> "The Creator hears only lack, which emerges as a result of a person's integration in his ten."

He added that even a small group of five to seven people, meeting regularly and trying to formulate a shared request based on what they are studying, is enough to begin this process — it does not require large or formal numbers.

### Three Shifting Points of Focus

Asked how a person keeps from getting lost between caring about the self, the group, and the Creator, Rav Laitman described three points a person moves between — himself, "the ten" (called *Aita*), and the Creator (*Kudsha Brich Hu*) — with emphasis on one or another at different moments. Only at the end of correction, he said, do these three points become experienced as one.

## Part 3 — Praying for the Friend, Not for Myself

### Self-Directed Requests Cause Harm

Rav Laitman and the group read a passage stating that one who separates from the public to pray only for himself — even for something that sounds spiritual, like asking to become a bestower — "inflicts ruin upon his soul." Discussing this, Rav Laitman explained the logic: turning to the Creator with a request centered on oneself, even innocently, distances a person from the Creator rather than bringing him closer, because it repeats the very self-directed orientation that needs correcting.

### Asking on Behalf of the Friend

A recurring instruction was to redirect every personal request outward. Asked whether it is "opposite holiness" to pray for oneself, Rav Laitman answered:

> "Try praying over what your ten wants rather than what you want. Then you will be as Malchut and your nine friends will be as the first nine Sefirot... So each one who prays for his friend is awarded first. When I pray for what the friend wants, it turns out that I am the one who receives the answer."

This was reinforced by a passage read from Rabash: that one cannot free himself from his own "imprisonment" by asking for himself, but is "answered quickly" when asking on behalf of a friend — so that in a group, everyone effectively works for everyone else's need. Rav Laitman extended this even to the desire to become a giver: "If you want to be similar to the creator, ask him for the force that he will give for the others" — even the wish to bestow should be framed as a request made for the friend, not for one's own spiritual attainment.

### A Prayer for the Collective Pleases the Creator More

A passage from Rabash on "the prayer of many" was read and discussed: a person capable of reaching closeness with the Creator may set that personal reward aside and instead ask that the Creator help the entire group reach it — because this brings the Creator more contentment than the individual's own advancement. Rav Laitman's comment distilled the entire lesson to two practical points: there is truly no one to turn to except the Creator, and the real work is learning precisely what to ask Him for.

## Part 4 — Workshop: Praying for What I Don't Yet Feel

### "This Is Not Yet My Prayer, But I Want It to Be"

One of the most direct teachings addressed a common obstacle: how to pray for something — like the group's success, or a friend's need — when a person doesn't genuinely feel that desire yet. Rav Laitman's guidance:

> "Even if I want to say something, and it's not that I actually feel that way, I need to understand and feel that I am raising a prayer that is not yet exactly my prayer, but rather that I would want it to be so. And I need to be aware of that."

In the workshop that followed, group representatives built on this idea in their own words: using envy of Kabbalists who genuinely feel such prayers as a way of increasing one's own desire for them; treating the gap as similar to a child who wants to be an adult and acts the part before he feels it; and calling this preliminary stage "a prayer before a prayer" — asking the Creator simply to create the desire to want the right thing, since even that desire is not fully in a person's own hands.

### The Creator's Single Desire

In a widely repeated clip during the workshop, Rav Laitman described the Creator's own position in this exchange:

> "The Creator has no deficiencies. He has only one deficiency, to help the created beings. Apart from that, he has nothing. And therefore, the more we demand and ask of him, the more we bring him contentment... Ask of him all the time. By doing so, you're actually doing him a favor... I give him work, and that's called the work of the creator... By doing so, I bring him true contentment."

The instruction drawn from this: requests should not be withheld out of hesitancy or a feeling of unworthiness. Asking constantly, and specifically on behalf of others, is itself described as an act of giving to the Creator, provided the request is free of self-serving ego.

## Part 5 — Building the Common Heart: The Global Assembly

### From "I" to "We"

In the closing portion, groups worldwide were guided through a live workshop to turn everything discussed into an actual shared prayer — not a theoretical review. Facilitators emphasized Rav Laitman's point that a person "includes within himself the entire ten, all the friends... standing in his heart," and raises that combined desire, not an individual one, to the Creator. Representatives described this transition as moving from "me" to "we": instead of a private request, forming one sentence, one feeling, that the whole group could stand behind.

### The Assembly Prayer

The session closed with representatives from dozens of countries — speaking in English, French, Spanish, Portuguese, Russian, Hebrew, Italian, and Japanese — offering their group's collective prayer aloud, asking the Creator to unite their hearts "as one man with one heart," to reveal Himself within that unity, and to help them feel one another's merits rather than faults. The facilitators noted that some 850 "tens" around the world were praying together in that same moment.

## Key Themes & Takeaways

1. **Prayer is self-judgment, not recitation.** In Hebrew, to pray (*lehitpallel*) means to examine oneself honestly — what one can and cannot yet bring to the Creator.
2. **An individual's prayer does not reach the Creator alone.** It must rise from "the ten" — a real, connected group — because the Creator "hears only lack that emerges from integration in the ten."
3. **Self-directed requests distance a person from the Creator**, even when they sound spiritual (e.g., asking to become a bestower for oneself). The correction is to ask on behalf of the friend.
4. **Praying for a friend is answered before praying for oneself** — because it reverses the self-oriented default and reflects the intention to bestow.
5. **It's acceptable — and expected — to pray for something not yet genuinely felt.** This is called "a prayer before a prayer": asking the Creator to create the very desire needed.
6. **The Creator's only "deficiency" is the desire to give to His creations**, so asking of Him constantly, cleanly of ego, is described as bringing Him contentment rather than being a burden.
7. **The goal is a single, shared request** — many individual desires becoming one collective heart, raised together as "the prayer of many."

## Key Terms & Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Prayer of Many** | A request that rises not from one individual but from an entire connected group (a "ten"), which the Creator answers because it reflects the intention to bestow rather than self-interest. |
| **Lehitpallel** | Hebrew root for "to pray"; reflexive form meaning to judge oneself — the core redefinition of prayer used throughout the lesson. |
| **Ten (Asara) / Kli** | The basic unit of spiritual work — a small connected group. Described as the vessel ("Kli") from which a genuine collective lack can be raised to the Creator. |
| **Raising a Lack (Man)** | The act of bringing one's deficiency/desire to the Creator as a request; the substance of prayer. |
| **Prayer Before a Prayer** | Asking the Creator to create in you the desire to want the correct thing, when you don't yet genuinely feel it. |
| **Aita** | The point of focus on "the ten" — one of three shifting centers of attention (self, the ten, the Creator) described by Rav Laitman. |
| **Kudsha Brich Hu** | "The Holy One, Blessed Be He" — the Creator; the third of the three shifting points of focus. |
| **Malchut / First Nine Sefirot** | Image used for group prayer roles: the one praying for the friend's need takes the position of Malchut (receiving), while the other nine friends function as the giving Sefirot above. |
| **Dvekut** | Adhesion/closeness with the Creator — the personal attainment some are described as willing to set aside in favor of the group's attainment. |
| **World Kli Congress** | The global, simultaneous gathering (hundreds of "tens" worldwide) in which this session took place. |
